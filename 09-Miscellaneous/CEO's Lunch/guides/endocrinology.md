## Lesson 1: Your T Is Probably Trash.

Endocrinology · Module 1 of 10

[Image: image.png]

Testosterone is the single most important hormone for how you look as a man. It drives muscle growth, fat distribution, facial masculinization, libido, energy, mood. Everything. If your T is in the gutter, you are fighting uphill on every single front of your looksmax. You can train perfectly, eat perfectly, and still look mid if your hormones are cooked.

And here's the thing. Most of you have never tested it. You're guessing. You're hoping. That's cope. You need actual numbers.

[Image: image.png]

———

**How Testosterone Actually Works**

Your brain runs the show. The hypothalamus releases GnRH, which tells the pituitary gland to release LH and FSH. LH goes to the Leydig cells in your testes and says "make testosterone." When T rises high enough, it feeds back to the brain and says "we're good, slow down." That's the HPG axis. That's the whole loop.

Now here's what most guys miss. There are two numbers that matter:
- **Total testosterone** — everything in your blood, bound and unbound. This is what most doctors test. It's not the full picture.
- **Free testosterone** — the fraction that's actually doing work. Not bound to SHBG or albumin. This is what enters your cells and makes things happen.

You can have total T at 700 ng/dL and still feel like garbage if your SHBG is sky high and your free T is in the dirt. **SHBG is the protein that binds testosterone and makes it inactive.** High SHBG = less usable T. You need both numbers.

———

**What Your Numbers Should Actually Be**

Your doctor will tell you 300-1000 ng/dL is "normal range." That's water. A 22 year old at 310 and a 22 year old at 850 are both "normal" according to the lab, but one is thriving and one is struggling to put on muscle and wondering why he's tired all the time.

Here's what you're actually aiming for:
- **Total testosterone** — 600-900 ng/dL is optimal. Below 400 is suboptimal and you need to investigate why. Below 300 is clinically low.
- **Free testosterone** — upper quartile of the reference range. If the range is 50-210 pg/mL, you want to be 150+.
- **SHBG** — lower half of range. 10-57 nmol/L is the reference, you want to be 20-35. Too high and it's eating your free T. Too low and you might have insulin resistance issues.
- **LH** — 1.7-8.6 IU/L, mid range. If your LH is high but your T is low, your testes aren't responding (primary hypogonadism). If both are low, the signal from your brain is weak (secondary hypogonadism). This tells you WHERE the problem is.

[Image: image.png]

———

**What's Killing Your T Right Now**

I'm going to be blunt. Most of you are doing at least 3 of these:
- **Sleeping 6 hours or less.** One week of 5 hour nights drops your T by 10-15%. That's not a small hit. That's going from a 700 to a 600 in a week just from bad sleep. This is the biggest lever and almost everyone is leaving it on the floor.
- **Being above 15% body fat.** Fat tissue contains aromatase, the enzyme that converts your testosterone into estrogen. The fatter you are, the more T gets converted to E2. You're literally turning your masculinity into a feminizing hormone. Get lean.
- **Chronic stress.** Cortisol and testosterone are inversely related. When cortisol goes up, T goes down. If you're chronically stressed, anxious, overworked, or not managing your mental health, your T is paying for it.
- **Drinking alcohol.** Alcohol is directly toxic to the Leydig cells that produce testosterone. Not "kinda bad." Directly toxic. Every weekend binge is tanking your T for days afterward.
- **Very low fat diets.** Cholesterol is the precursor to testosterone. If your dietary fat is too low, you're literally removing the raw material your body needs to make T. Minimum 0.8g fat per kg of bodyweight.
- **Overtraining.** More is not always better. Training 7 days a week with no recovery spikes cortisol and suppresses T. 4-5 hard sessions per week with actual rest days is better than 7 mediocre ones.

[Image: image.png]

———

**The Exact Protocol To Optimize Your T**

Here's what you're going to do. In order. No skipping steps.

**Step 1. Get bloodwork.** Total T, free T, SHBG, LH, FSH, estradiol. Do it fasted, 8-10am. This is your baseline. You cannot optimize what you don't measure. I cover exactly how to get this done in Module 8 but the short version is: go to Marek Health or order through a private lab if your doctor won't order the full panel. Costs $100-200 for everything you need.

**Step 2. Fix your sleep.** 8 hours minimum. Not "I go to bed at midnight and my alarm is at 7." Eight hours of actual sleep. That means you're in bed by 10:30-11 if you're waking at 7. Consistent schedule every single day including weekends. No screens 30 min before bed. Room cold and dark. This alone can move your T by 100+ ng/dL if you've been sleeping poorly.

**Step 3. Get lean.** Sub 15% body fat. Ideally 12-14%. Every percentage of body fat you lose reduces aromatase activity and improves your T:E2 ratio. If you're above 20% right now, this is your number one priority before anything else in this module matters.

**Step 4. Supplement what you're deficient in.** Not random supplements. Specific ones based on your bloodwork.
- **Vitamin D3** — if your 25-OH vitamin D is below 40 ng/mL, take 4000-5000 IU daily with a fat source. Vitamin D deficiency directly suppresses T production. Most people are deficient.
- **Zinc** — 15-30mg daily (zinc picolinate or zinc bisglycinate, not zinc oxide which absorbs terribly). Zinc is required for testosterone synthesis. If you're not eating oysters, red meat, or pumpkin seeds regularly, you're probably low.
- **Magnesium glycinate** — 300-400mg before bed. Improves sleep quality and supports T production. Most people are deficient.

**Step 5. Keep dietary fat at 0.8-1.0g per kg of bodyweight minimum.** If you weigh 80kg that's 64-80g of fat per day. Eggs, olive oil, avocado, fatty fish, nuts. Do not go low fat. Guys on extreme low fat diets consistently show lower T.

**Step 6. Cut alcohol.** Completely if possible. If not completely, absolute minimum. Every drink is working against you. This is not debatable.

**Step 7. Train with compound movements 4x per week.** Squat, deadlift, bench, overhead press, rows, pull ups. Heavy compounds spike T acutely and build the chronic baseline over time. Isolation curls don't do this.

**Step 8. Manage chronic stress.** Whatever that looks like for you. Exercise helps. Adequate sleep helps. Social connection helps. If you're doom scrolling for 4 hours a night or in a toxic living situation, your cortisol is elevated and your T is suppressed. Address it.

[Image: image.png]

———

**Timeline For Results**

If you implement all of this from a bad starting point, here's roughly what to expect:
- **Week 1-2** — better energy and mood from sleep improvement. No measurable T change yet.
- **Week 4-6** — noticeable improvement in gym performance and recovery. Body composition starts shifting if you're also cutting.
- **Week 8-12** — this is when you retest bloodwork. Most guys see a 100-300 ng/dL increase in total T from baseline if they were genuinely poorly optimized before. Free T often improves even more proportionally if SHBG comes down.
- **Month 3-6** — visible changes in body composition, facial definition improving as BF% drops, energy consistently higher.

This is not overnight. It's not a magic pill. It's a lifestyle overhaul that compounds. But the difference between an unoptimized dude running at 380 ng/dL and the same dude at 700 ng/dL six months later is visible. It shows in your face, your body, your energy. It mogs.

———

**Mistakes That Are Costing You**
- **Taking random "testosterone booster" supplements before fixing sleep.** No supplement on earth moves the needle as much as going from 6 hours to 8 hours of sleep. Tribulus, D-aspartic acid, fenugreek, all that stuff is cope. The evidence is either nonexistent or weak. Fix the basics first.
- **Having high total T but never checking SHBG.** If your SHBG is 70+ nmol/L, your total T could be 800 and you'd still have low free T. You'd still feel like crap. Always test SHBG alongside total T.
- **Assuming you have low T based on symptoms alone.** Fatigue and low libido have a hundred possible causes. Don't self diagnose. Get the bloodwork and know for certain.
- **Skipping the retest.** You implement changes for 3 months and never check if they actually worked. You need before AND after numbers. Otherwise you're just hoping.

———

Get the bloodwork. Fix the sleep. Get lean. Supplement what you're actually deficient in. Cut the alcohol. Train heavy. That's it. That's the protocol. Most of you will double your free T doing just these things and you've never tried doing all of them at once. Stop coping with supplements and start doing the work.


## Lesson 2: DHT Is A Double Edged Sword.

Endocrinology · Module 2 of 10

[Image: image.png]

DHT is the most powerful androgen in your body. 3-5x stronger than testosterone at the receptor. It's what gave you your jaw, your brow ridge, your beard, your sex drive. It's also what's eating your hairline alive if you're genetically susceptible. This is the central tradeoff in male looksmaxxing pharmacology and you need to understand it before you touch finasteride or anything else.

———

**How DHT Gets Made**

DHT is not something your body produces on its own. It's converted FROM testosterone by an enzyme called 5-alpha reductase. There are two types:
- **Type 1** — found in your skin, liver, and scalp
- **Type 2** — found in your prostate, hair follicles, and genitals

Finasteride blocks Type 2 only. Dutasteride blocks both. That's why dut is stronger and also why it has more potential for sides. Remember this because it matters when you're making decisions about hair loss treatment in the Hair modules.

Your testosterone gets converted to DHT, DHT binds to androgen receptors with way more force than T ever could, and then it does its thing. The problem is "its thing" includes both building your masculine features AND destroying your hair follicles. Same hormone. Opposite effects depending on where it acts.

[Image: image.png]

———

**What DHT Actually Does For You**

Let me be clear. DHT is not your enemy. Guys who demonize DHT don't understand what it built for them.
- **Facial masculinization** — brow ridge development, jaw growth, chin projection. During puberty, DHT is a major driver of your lower third and brow area becoming masculine. This is literally what separates a Chad jaw from a recessed one during development.
- **Beard growth** — DHT is the primary driver. Guys with naturally thick beards generally have higher DHT or more sensitive androgen receptors in their facial hair follicles. If you're trying to beardmaxx, DHT is your friend.
- **Libido and sexual function** — DHT is the primary androgen for sex drive. Not testosterone. DHT. This is why some guys on finasteride report killed libido even though their testosterone stays the same or even goes up.
- **Confidence and drive** — anecdotal but widely reported. Guys with higher DHT tend to report more assertiveness and motivation. Low inhib energy if you will.

———

**What DHT Does Against You**
- **Male pattern hair loss** — if you carry the genetics for androgenetic alopecia, DHT miniaturizes your scalp hair follicles. They get thinner and shorter each cycle until they stop producing visible hair entirely. This is NOT random. It follows the Norwood pattern. Temples first, then crown, then they merge. If your dad or mom's dad is NW5+, pay attention.
- **Acne** — DHT increases sebum production. More oil, more clogged pores, more breakouts. If you're acne prone and your DHT is high, this is a direct connection.
- **Prostate growth** — not relevant for aesthetics but worth knowing. DHT drives prostate tissue growth which is why finasteride was originally a prostate drug, not a hair drug.

[Image: image.png]

———

**The DHT Tradeoff Explained Simply**

This is the decision every guy losing his hair has to make:

**Keep DHT high:**
- Fuller beard
- Stronger libido
- More masculine features maintained
- More confidence and drive
- Hair loss continues or accelerates
- More acne if prone

**Block DHT (finasteride/dutasteride):**
- Hair loss slows dramatically or stops
- Some regrowth possible
- Reduced acne in some cases
- Potential reduced libido (varies wildly by individual)
- Potential reduced beard growth speed
- Potential mood changes

There is no option where you get everything. That's the reality. If you're NW1 with no family history of baldness, congratulations, you don't have to think about this. If you're NW2 and your temples are creeping back and every man in your family is bald by 30, you need to make a decision and make it soon because hair loss prevention works way better than hair loss reversal.

———

**What Affects Your DHT Levels**

**Raises DHT:**
- **Higher testosterone** — more substrate means more conversion. Optimizing your T will also raise your DHT. That's the deal.
- **Creatine** — one study showed creatine increased DHT by about 56% after 3 weeks of loading. One study. But if you're genetically susceptible to hair loss and loading creatine, this is worth knowing. I'm not saying stop creatine. I'm saying be aware.
- **Resistance training** — acutely raises both T and DHT.
- **Zinc sufficiency** — supports androgen production including DHT conversion.

**Lowers DHT:**
- **Finasteride 1mg daily** — reduces scalp DHT by about 70%. The standard first line treatment for hair loss.
- **Dutasteride 0.5mg daily** — reduces DHT by about 90%. The nuclear option. More effective but more side effect potential.
- **Saw palmetto** — weak natural 5AR inhibitor. Cope tier compared to fin or dut but some guys stack it.
- **Ketoconazole shampoo (Nizoral)** — mild topical anti-androgen. Used 2-3x per week as part of "the big three" hair loss stack.

———

**What You Need To Know Right Now**
- **If you're not losing hair** — don't touch DHT blockers. There's no reason to reduce the most potent androgen in your body if it's not causing a problem. Let it do its job.
- **If you ARE losing hair** — the earlier you act the better. Finasteride works dramatically better at preserving existing hair than regrowing lost hair. If you wait until NW4 to start, you've already lost ground you might not get back. I cover the full protocol in the Hair modules.
- **Don't fear DHT or worship it.** It's a hormone with specific effects. Understand the tradeoff for YOUR genetics and make an informed decision. That's what separates someone who's actually looksmaxxing from someone who's just coping.

———

DHT built your jaw and it's eating your hairline. Same hormone. The tradeoff is real and there's no way around it. Know your genetics, know your priorities, and make the decision with actual data not forum fear mongering in either direction.


## Lesson 3: Estrogen Is Not Your Enemy.

Endocrinology · Module 3 of 10

[Image: image.png]

Estrogen in men is one of the most misunderstood topics in this space. Every forum bro thinks estrogen is pure evil and wants to nuke it into the ground. That's how you end up with destroyed joints, zero libido, depression, and bone density loss. You NEED estrogen. The goal is balance, not elimination.

———

**How Estrogen Works In Men**

The main estrogen in males is estradiol (E2). You make it through a process called aromatization. The enzyme aromatase takes your testosterone and converts some of it into estradiol. This happens primarily in fat tissue, the liver, the brain, and the testes.

Here's why this matters for your looks: **the fatter you are, the more aromatase you have, the more of your testosterone gets converted to estrogen.** You're literally turning your T into E2 through your body fat. This is why overweight guys often have low T and high E2 at the same time. Their own fat is doing it to them.

But estradiol isn't just a waste product. It does real work:
- **Keeps your bones dense and strong** — men with crashed E2 develop osteoporosis
- **Maintains your libido** — you read that right. Men with very low estradiol have terrible sex drives even if T is normal
- **Protects your cardiovascular system**
- **Supports brain function and mood**
- **Keeps your joints lubricated** — this is the first thing guys notice when they crash their E2 with an AI. Joints feel like sandpaper.

[Image: image.png]

———

**What High Estrogen Looks Like**

This is the one everyone worries about, and fair enough. High E2 is a real problem:
- **Gynecomastia** — actual breast tissue growth. Not just chest fat. Once the glandular tissue forms, only surgery removes it. This is a legit failo and preventing it matters.
- **Water retention and facial puffiness** — that bloated, smooth, undefined look even when you're not that fat. Your face looks round and soft. Your jawline disappears. Classic bloatmaxx look.
- **Mood swings and emotional instability** — irritability, crying at random things, feeling emotionally all over the place.
- **Reduced libido** — paradoxically, both too high AND too low E2 kill your sex drive.
- **Fat gain concentrated in chest and hips** — feminized fat distribution pattern. Your body literally stores fat in female patterns when E2 is too high.

———

**What Crashed Estrogen Looks Like**

This is the part the forum bros don't talk about. Guys who take aromatase inhibitors without bloodwork and nuke their E2 to single digits feel TERRIBLE:
- **Joint pain and stiffness** — every joint in your body feels dry and creaky
- **Zero libido** — completely gone. Not low. Gone.
- **Depression and brain fog** — flat mood, can't think clearly, no motivation
- **Bone density loss** — long term crashed E2 literally weakens your skeleton
- **Fatigue** — no energy regardless of sleep quality

Crashing your estrogen is genuinely as bad as having it too high. Arguably worse because the sides hit faster and harder. **Never take an aromatase inhibitor without confirmed high E2 on bloodwork.**

———

**Your Target Numbers**
- **Estradiol (E2)** — optimal range is 20-30 pg/mL. Below 15 you'll start feeling the crashed E2 symptoms. Above 40 and you're getting into high territory.
- **T:E2 ratio** — you want at least 15:1 ideally 20:1. So if your total T is 600, your E2 should be around 30-40 pg/mL. If your T is 600 and your E2 is 60, your ratio is 10:1 and that's a problem.

Always test estradiol alongside testosterone. Testing one without the other is useless. The ratio matters more than either number alone.

———

**How To Manage Estrogen Naturally**

**Step 1. Get lean.** This is the answer to 80% of estrogen problems. Less body fat = less aromatase = less conversion of T to E2. If you're above 15% BF and complaining about high estrogen, your body fat IS the problem. Get to 12-14% and retest before you even think about anything else.

**Step 2. Eat cruciferous vegetables daily.** Broccoli, cauliflower, Brussels sprouts, kale, cabbage. These contain DIM (diindolylmethane) and I3C (indole-3-carbinol) which support healthy estrogen metabolism in the liver. Not a massive effect but it adds up when you're eating them consistently. Aim for 1-2 servings per day.

**Step 3. Cut alcohol.** Alcohol impairs your liver's ability to clear estrogen from your system. So you're making more E2 from being fat AND clearing less of it because you're drinking. Double hit. Cut it.

**Step 4. Supplement zinc.** Zinc has mild aromatase inhibiting properties. 15-30mg daily. Not going to be dramatic but it helps at the margins and most guys are deficient anyway.

**Step 5. Reduce xenoestrogen exposure.** These are synthetic chemicals that mimic estrogen in your body. Plastics (BPA, phthalates), receipts, pesticides, certain personal care products. Switch to glass or stainless steel water bottles and food containers. Stop microwaving food in plastic. Choose natural personal care products when possible. Is this going to transform your E2? Probably not dramatically. But it's easy to do and there's no downside.

[Image: image.png]

———

**When To Actually Worry**

Get bloodwork. If your E2 is 20-30 pg/mL and your T:E2 ratio is good, you don't need to do anything special. You're balanced. Most guys who implement the protocol from Module 1 (sleep, leanness, training) find their estrogen falls in line without ever directly addressing it.

If your E2 is above 40 pg/mL despite being lean, that's worth discussing with a doctor. If you're developing gyno symptoms (tender lumps behind the nipple), that needs medical attention quickly.

But **do not** take an aromatase inhibitor because a forum post scared you. Do not take DIM supplements in megadoses because you read that estrogen is bad. Get the bloodwork, know your numbers, and respond to actual data.

———

Estrogen is not the enemy. Too much is bad. Too little is equally bad. Get lean, eat your vegetables, cut the alcohol, and test your E2 alongside your testosterone. For most of you, fixing body fat will fix the problem entirely. Stop trying to nuke a hormone you actually need.


## Lesson 4: Growth Hormone Is The Youth Drug.

Endocrinology · Module 4 of 10

[Image: image.png]

Growth hormone and IGF-1 are the reason teenagers can eat garbage and still grow muscle. They're why a 17 year old's skin glows and recovers from acne overnight. They're also why you start looking and feeling worse in your late 20s when these hormones begin declining. GH is the closest thing to an anti-aging hormone that exists naturally. And most of you are destroying your own production every single night.

———

**How Growth Hormone Actually Works**

Your pituitary gland releases GH in pulses throughout the day. But the biggest pulse by far happens during deep slow-wave sleep. Not light sleep. Not REM. Deep sleep specifically. This is the pulse that matters most.

GH doesn't directly do much on its own. It signals your liver to produce IGF-1 (insulin-like growth factor 1), and IGF-1 is the actual workhorse. IGF-1 drives:
- **Muscle protein synthesis** — building and repairing muscle tissue
- **Fat burning (lipolysis)** — breaking down stored fat for energy
- **Collagen production in skin** — this is why optimized GH = better skin quality, less wrinkles, tighter complexion
- **Bone and cartilage growth** — during puberty this literally builds your skeleton and face
- **Overall tissue repair and recovery**

Here's the brutal part. GH production peaks during puberty (2-3x adult levels), stays high through your early 20s, and then drops roughly 14% per decade after that. By the time you're 40, you're running on a fraction of what you had at 18. You cannot stop this decline entirely. But you can slow it dramatically or speed it up based on your lifestyle choices.

[Image: image.png]

———

**What's Killing Your GH Right Now**

There's one thing that suppresses GH release more than anything else: **insulin.**

When insulin is elevated, GH cannot pulse properly. They're essentially on opposite ends of a seesaw. Insulin up = GH down. This is basic endocrinology and it has massive practical implications.

If you eat a big carb heavy meal right before bed, you spike insulin right when your body is supposed to be releasing its biggest GH pulse of the day. You literally block the most important hormonal event of your entire 24 hour cycle with a bowl of pasta at 11pm. That's self sabotage.

The other GH killers:
- **Bad sleep or not enough sleep** — if you're getting 5-6 hours you're not hitting enough deep sleep cycles for adequate GH pulsing. This is the number one lever.
- **Being fat** — obesity significantly blunts GH pulse amplitude. The heavier you are, the less GH you produce per pulse.
- **Alcohol before bed** — destroys sleep architecture. Wrecks the deep sleep phase where GH pulses happen. Even 2-3 drinks eliminates your overnight GH pulse almost entirely.
- **Chronic stress and high cortisol** — cortisol suppresses GH secretion.

———

**How To Maximize Your GH Naturally**

**Step 1. Sleep 8+ hours on a consistent schedule.** This is not optional. Deep slow-wave sleep is when the big GH pulse happens. Less sleep = less deep sleep = less GH. Every hour you cut is hormonal robbery from yourself. Go to bed and wake up at the same time every single day. Your circadian rhythm needs consistency to optimize deep sleep phases.

**Step 2. Stop eating 2-3 hours before bed.** Let insulin drop before you sleep. If you go to bed at 11, your last meal is at 8 or 8:30. No late night snacks. No bedtime protein shake. Nothing. Let insulin fall so GH can rise.

**Step 3. Stay lean.** Sub 15% BF. Ideally 12-14%. Body fat above 18% measurably blunts GH pulsing. The leaner you are, the bigger and more frequent your GH pulses.

**Step 4. Train with intensity.** High intensity resistance training produces the largest acute GH spike of any exercise type. Heavy compound lifts with short rest periods. This is not the main lever (sleep is) but it adds up over time.

**Step 5. Don't drink alcohol before bed.** I know I keep saying this across every module. That should tell you how bad alcohol is for your hormones. It wrecks sleep architecture, kills the GH pulse, raises cortisol the next day, and is toxic to Leydig cells. If you're serious about optimizing, alcohol is the first thing to go.

[Image: image.png]

———

**GH By Age: What To Expect**
- **12-18 (puberty)** — peak GH output. IGF-1 levels 200-900 ng/mL. This is the window where GH is literally building your face and body. If you're in this age range and reading this, SLEEP. I cannot stress this enough. You are in the highest leverage hormonal window of your entire life. Mewing, sleeping 9+ hours, eating enough protein, and training will yield results during puberty that are physically impossible to replicate at any other age.
- **18-25** — still high GH. IGF-1 150-400 ng/mL. This is still prime time. Optimize aggressively.
- **25-40** — declining. IGF-1 100-300 ng/mL. Lifestyle optimization becomes increasingly important because natural output is dropping.
- **40+** — low. IGF-1 75-200 ng/mL. This is where the decline becomes visually noticeable. Skin quality drops, recovery slows, muscle maintenance gets harder.

———

**Mistakes That Are Robbing You**
- **Eating a huge meal before bed every night.** Most common GH killer I see. Guys eating dinner at 10pm and wondering why they wake up puffy and soft. Insulin is suppressing your GH pulse all night.
- **Sleeping 5-6 hours and thinking supplements will make up for it.** No amount of arginine or GABA or any "GH booster" supplement comes close to what an extra 2 hours of sleep does. Not even in the same universe. The supplements are cope. The sleep is real.
- **Being overweight and ignoring it.** Obese individuals have severely blunted GH pulsing. If you're carrying significant extra body fat, your GH production is a fraction of what it could be. This compounds all the other hormonal problems from being fat.
- **Teenagers staying up until 3am.** If you're 14-18 and sleeping 5 hours a night, you are literally wasting the single most important hormonal window of your life. The damage you're doing to your development by skipping sleep during puberty cannot be undone later. This is not recoverable. Sleep.

———

Sleep 8+ hours. Stop eating 2-3 hours before bed. Stay lean. Train hard. Cut alcohol. That's how you keep your GH as high as your genetics and age allow. Every night you skip sleep or eat late, you're leaving muscle, fat loss, skin quality, and recovery on the table. And if you're in puberty, this is 10x more important than everything else in this entire course combined.


## Lesson 5: Cortisol Is Eating Your Face.

Endocrinology · Module 5 of 10

[Image: image.png]

Cortisol is the hormone that's silently undoing everything you're building. You're training, eating right, sleeping decent, and still looking soft and puffy? Still holding water in your face? Still losing muscle despite hitting the gym? Cortisol might be the reason. Chronically elevated cortisol destroys muscle, stores fat in your face and gut, kills your testosterone, wrecks your skin collagen, and ruins your sleep. It's the anti-looksmax hormone.

———

**Cortisol Is Not Always Bad**

Let me be clear. Cortisol in short bursts is fine. It's necessary. When you wake up in the morning, cortisol spikes to get you alert and functional. When you train, cortisol spikes to mobilize energy. When you're in actual danger, cortisol helps you survive. That's all healthy.

The problem is when cortisol stays elevated all day every day. Chronic elevation. That's when it starts destroying everything:
- **Breaks down muscle tissue (catabolism)** — you're literally losing gains while sleeping if cortisol is chronically high
- **Stores fat in your face and midsection** — "moon face" is a real medical term for what chronic cortisol does. Your face gets round, puffy, and undefined. Your jawline disappears under water and fat. Your midsection bloats. Classic bloatmaxx without the calories to justify it.
- **Directly suppresses testosterone** — cortisol and T are inversely related. When cortisol goes up, T comes down. This is not subtle. Chronic stress literally lowers your testosterone.
- **Degrades skin collagen** — accelerated aging. Fine lines, thin skin, dull complexion. Your skinmaxx routine can't overcome cortisol eating your collagen from the inside.
- **Disrupts sleep** — elevated evening cortisol makes it hard to fall asleep and reduces deep sleep quality. And poor sleep raises cortisol the next day. It's a death spiral.
- **Causes water retention** — puffy face, puffy body, undefined everything. You look 5 lbs heavier than you are.

[Image: image.png]

———

**What's Driving Your Cortisol Up**

Be honest with yourself on this list:
- **Poor sleep** — THE biggest driver. One bad week of sleep and your cortisol is elevated for days. Most of you are running on 6 hours and wondering why you look tired and puffy. This is why.
- **Overtraining** — more is not better. Training 6-7 days a week with high intensity and no recovery days spikes cortisol chronically. Your body is in a constant state of stress and never gets to fully repair. You feel run down, sore all the time, and your strength stalls. That's not dedication. That's overtraining.
- **Aggressive calorie deficits** — cutting on 1200 calories while training hard is a cortisol bomb. Your body thinks it's starving. Cortisol goes through the roof. You lose muscle, hold water, stall on fat loss, and feel miserable. Max deficit should be 400-500 calories below your TDEE. Anything more aggressive and cortisol starts winning.
- **Chronic psychological stress** — work, relationships, financial stress, social media doom scrolling, toxic living situations. Your body doesn't distinguish between a lion chasing you and being anxious about your job. The cortisol response is the same.

## Lesson 6: Why Your Cut Stalled.

Endocrinology · Module 6 of 10

[Image: image.png]

Insulin and leptin. These two hormones decide whether your body stores fat or burns it, whether you can keep cutting or your metabolism shuts down, and whether your other hormones stay online or crash. If you've ever hit a wall on a cut where nothing moves for weeks despite being in a deficit, leptin crashed on you. If you eat before bed every night and wonder why you look soft, insulin is blocking your GH. Understanding these two hormones is the difference between a successful cut and a stall that makes you quit.

———

**Insulin: The Storage Hormone**

Every time you eat, especially carbs, your pancreas releases insulin. Insulin's job is to shuttle glucose out of your blood and into your cells. Muscle, fat cells, liver. That's it. That's what insulin does.

The problem is that **when insulin is elevated, your body is in storage mode.** It's putting things away, not burning them. And critically for your looksmax, insulin directly suppresses GH release. Remember from Module 4? The biggest GH pulse happens during deep sleep. If insulin is elevated at bedtime because you just ate, that pulse gets crushed.

But here's the nuance that the "insulin is evil" crowd misses: **insulin is also anabolic.** After training, insulin helps shuttle amino acids into muscle for repair and growth. Carbs around your workout are productive. The problem isn't insulin itself. It's insulin at the WRONG time.
- **High insulin (fed state)** — nutrients getting stored. Fat mobilization stops. GH is suppressed. This is fine after training. This is terrible before bed.
- **Low insulin (fasted state)** — fat gets mobilized for energy. GH pulses freely. This is the state you want during sleep and during extended gaps between meals.

When you become insulin resistant from being overweight, eating too much sugar chronically, or being sedentary, your body pumps out MORE insulin to get the same job done. Insulin resistance is directly associated with lower testosterone in males. Fat guys with insulin resistance have suppressed T and blunted GH. It's a hormonal disaster.

[Image: image.png]

———

**Leptin: The Starvation Alarm**

Leptin is produced by your fat cells. The more fat you have, the more leptin you produce. Leptin signals to your brain: "we have enough energy stored, keep metabolism running, keep hormones online." It's an energy availability signal.

When everything is normal, this system works fine. But here's where it wrecks your cut:

**When you diet hard, leptin drops.** Your fat cells are shrinking, they produce less leptin, and your brain gets the signal "energy is running out." Your brain's response? **Slow everything down.** Metabolic rate drops. Testosterone drops. GH drops. Thyroid hormones drop. You feel exhausted, hungry, cold, and weak. Fat loss stalls. This is not a willpower problem. This is your endocrine system slamming the brakes because it thinks you're starving.

This is why crash diets always fail. You drop to 1200 calories, lose weight fast for 2-3 weeks, then everything grinds to a halt. You feel terrible. Your training suffers. You look flat and stringy instead of lean and defined. You're losing muscle and holding water. Leptin crashed and took your hormones with it.

———

**How To Manage Insulin For Your Looksmax**

**Step 1. Do not eat 2-3 hours before bed.** I've said this in every module because it matters that much. Let insulin drop before sleep so your GH pulse fires properly. If you go to bed at 11, last food at 8-8:30. Non negotiable.

**Step 2. Put your carbs around training.** Pre and post workout are when insulin is your friend. The insulin spike from carbs helps shuttle nutrients into muscle tissue when it's primed for growth. This is the one time high insulin is productive. A scoop of rice or oats before training and a carb source after is fine. Carbs at 10pm scrolling your phone is not the same thing.

**Step 3. Consider a fasting window.** 14-16 hours of fasting extends the low-insulin fat burning window. Skip breakfast, eat from noon to 8pm. This isn't magic, it just means more hours in a low-insulin state where fat mobilization and GH pulsing are optimized. If this doesn't fit your life or training schedule, don't force it. The 2-3 hour pre-bed rule is more important.

**Step 4. Choose lower glycemic carb sources.** Rice, oats, sweet potatoes, fruit. These produce a more gradual insulin response compared to sugar, white bread, and processed carbs that spike you hard and fast. This isn't about being anti-carb. It's about managing the insulin curve.

———

**How To Manage Leptin During A Cut**

This is where most guys destroy their cuts without realizing it.

**Rule 1. Never go below 1600 calories.** Below this threshold leptin drops rapidly and your hormones start shutting down regardless of how much fat you have left to lose. The calories you "save" by going lower cost you in hormonal suppression, muscle loss, and metabolic slowdown. Not worth it. Ever.

**Rule 2. Include a maintenance calorie day every 10-14 days.** One day at your maintenance calories with higher carbs specifically. Carb refeeds are the most effective way to temporarily boost leptin back up. This sends a "we're fed" signal to your brain and restores some hormonal function. It's not a cheat day. It's a strategic refeed. Keep protein the same, drop fat slightly, add 300-500 extra calories entirely from carbs.

**Rule 3. Keep protein high.** 1g per lb of bodyweight during a cut minimum. Protein is the most leptin-sparing macronutrient. It helps preserve muscle mass under caloric restriction and produces more satiety per calorie than carbs or fat.

**Rule 4. Don't cut for longer than 12-16 weeks without a break.** After 12-16 weeks of continuous deficit, take 1-2 weeks at maintenance calories. Let your metabolism and hormones normalize. Then resume if needed. This makes the total fat loss period longer but the results are dramatically better because you maintain muscle and hormonal function.

- Constantly hungry despite eating "enough" protein
- Fatigue that sleep doesn't fix
- Training performance dropping week over week
- Feeling cold all the time (thyroid hormones dropped)
- Libido gone (testosterone dropped)
- Weight loss completely stalled for 2+ weeks despite verified deficit

If this is you right now, **take a diet break.** 7-14 days at maintenance. It's not giving up. It's strategy. Your hormones need to come back online before you can continue losing fat effectively. Pushing harder when leptin is crashed is pure cope. You're just losing muscle and feeling worse.

Your fat cells are pumping out leptin like crazy but your brain has stopped listening to it. Meanwhile you're insulin resistant so your pancreas is dumping out extra insulin to compensate, which suppresses your T and GH. It's a hormonal prison.

## Lesson 7: Your Thyroid Might Be The Problem.

Endocrinology · Module 7 of 10

[Image: image.png]

If you're losing hair diffusely (not in the Norwood pattern), gaining fat despite eating right, your skin is dry and dull, you're constantly tired, and your outer eyebrows are thinning, your thyroid might be cooked. This is one of the most commonly missed causes of looking and feeling terrible. Guys blame their training, their diet, their genetics, and never think to check the tiny butterfly shaped gland in their neck that controls their entire metabolic rate.

———

**What Your Thyroid Does**

Your thyroid gland produces two hormones:
- **T4 (thyroxine)** — the inactive storage form. This is what your thyroid actually makes in bulk. Think of it as the raw material.
- **T3 (triiodothyronine)** — the active form. This is what actually enters your cells and sets your metabolic rate. T4 gets converted to T3 primarily in the liver.

The thyroid is controlled by the HPT axis. Hypothalamus releases TRH, pituitary releases TSH, TSH tells your thyroid to produce T4 and T3. When levels are sufficient, it feeds back and TSH drops. When levels are low, TSH rises to tell the thyroid to work harder.

This is why **TSH is the screening test**. High TSH = your thyroid isn't producing enough and the brain is yelling at it to work harder. Low TSH = either your thyroid is overproducing or you're taking too much thyroid medication.

———

**What Thyroid Problems Look Like**

**Hypothyroid (underactive, the common one):**
- Unexplained fat gain or inability to lose fat despite a real deficit
- Diffuse hair thinning (all over, not just temples and crown like male pattern baldness)
- Outer third of your eyebrows thinning or disappearing
- Dry flaky skin that doesn't respond to skincare products
- Constantly cold, especially hands and feet
- Fatigue that sleep doesn't fix
- Brain fog and sluggish thinking
- Constipation

**Hyperthyroid (overactive, less common):**
- Unintended weight loss despite eating enough
- Hair loss (different pattern from hypo, often more rapid)
- Anxiety, restlessness, heart racing
- Sweating excessively
- Difficulty sleeping from feeling wired

**Subclinical hypothyroid (the sneaky one):**

This is the one that gets missed constantly. Your TSH is slightly elevated (say 4.0-6.0) but your T4 is still technically in range. Doctors say "you're fine" because you're in the reference range. But you're gaining fat slowly, your hair is subtly thinning, you're more tired than you should be, and your skin isn't great despite doing everything right. This is where knowing **optimal vs normal** matters.

[Image: image.png]

———

**Your Target Numbers**
- **TSH** — reference range is 0.4-4.0 mIU/L. Optimal is 1.0-2.5. If your TSH is 3.5 and your doctor says it's normal, it IS in range, but it's not optimal. Anything above 2.5 with symptoms deserves attention.
- **Free T4** — reference range 0.8-1.8 ng/dL. You want mid to upper range.
- **Free T3** — reference range 2.3-4.1 pg/mL. You want mid to upper range. This is the active hormone that matters most. If your T4 is normal but T3 is low, you have a conversion problem.

**Always get all three tested.** Most doctors only check TSH. That's not enough. You need free T4 and free T3 to see the full picture. TSH can be normal while T3 conversion is impaired, especially during aggressive dieting.

———

**What Supports Your Thyroid**

Your thyroid needs specific nutrients to function. If any of these are deficient, your thyroid performance drops:

**Iodine** — the raw material for T4 synthesis. Without iodine your thyroid literally cannot make hormones. If you're not eating seafood or dairy regularly and you don't use iodized salt, you might be low. Most table salt is iodized. Sea salt and pink Himalayan salt are NOT. If you switched to fancy salt and your thyroid tanked, now you know why. Make sure you're getting iodine from somewhere.

**Selenium** — required for converting T4 to T3. This is the conversion step. Without selenium, you can have normal T4 but low T3 and feel hypothyroid. The single best source is Brazil nuts. Just 1-2 per day gives you your full daily selenium. Don't overdo it because selenium toxicity is real (don't eat a whole bag). 1-2 nuts. That's it.

**Zinc** — involved in thyroid hormone metabolism. You're already supplementing this for testosterone (Module 1). Same 15-30mg daily dose covers thyroid support too.

**Iron** — required for thyroid peroxidase, the enzyme that makes thyroid hormones. If you're iron deficient (check ferritin on bloodwork), your thyroid can't produce hormones efficiently. Red meat is the best source. Leafy greens have non-heme iron which absorbs less well. If ferritin is below 30, consider supplementation.

**Vitamin D** — supports thyroid receptor function. Again, you should already be addressing this from Module 1.

[Image: image.png]

———

**What Hurts Your Thyroid**
- **Extended very low calorie dieting** — this is a big one. Severe restriction reduces T4 to T3 conversion. Your body downregulates metabolism to conserve energy. This is another reason crash diets destroy you. Not only does leptin crash (Module 6), but your thyroid slows down too. Double hit to metabolism.
- **Excessive raw cruciferous vegetables** — broccoli, cauliflower, kale, Brussels sprouts contain goitrogens that can mildly interfere with thyroid function in large raw quantities. Cooking deactivates most goitrogens. So eat your cruciferous vegetables (they're great for estrogen metabolism from Module 3) but cook them most of the time. A raw broccoli salad occasionally is fine. A raw kale smoothie every day for months might not be.
- **Chronic stress** — high cortisol interferes with T4 to T3 conversion. Another reason managing cortisol (Module 5) matters for everything.

———

**When To Actually Worry**

If you're experiencing diffuse hair loss, unexplained weight gain, chronic fatigue, dry skin, and brain fog, get a thyroid panel on your next bloodwork. TSH, free T4, free T3. If TSH is above 2.5 with symptoms, discuss it with a doctor who understands optimal vs normal ranges. Subclinical hypothyroidism is extremely common and treatable.

If you're losing hair and it's NOT in the Norwood pattern (temples and crown) but instead thinning all over including the sides and back, thyroid is a prime suspect. Male pattern baldness doesn't touch the sides and back. Diffuse thinning everywhere = check your thyroid and your ferritin.

———

Your thyroid runs your metabolism, your hair growth cycle, your skin quality, and your energy. If those are all off despite doing everything else right, the answer might be a blood test and 2 Brazil nuts a day. Get TSH, free T4, and free T3 tested. Don't accept "normal" when you should be optimal. And stop crash dieting because you're suppressing the one gland that controls how many calories you burn.

Impact: MODERATE-HIGH. Cortisol directly suppresses T. If you're chronically stressed, your cortisol is elevated and your T is paying for it. Train, sleep, limit doom scrolling, address toxic situations in your life. Easier said than done but the hormonal impact is real.

**9. Fix magnesium deficiency. 300-400mg magnesium glycinate before bed.**

Impact: MODERATE. Magnesium improves sleep quality, supports T production, and helps manage cortisol. Most people don't get enough from diet. Glycinate form specifically because it's well absorbed and doesn't cause GI issues. Taking it before bed also helps with sleep onset. Two birds one stone.

**10. Get 20-30 minutes of sunlight daily on exposed skin.**

Impact: MODERATE. Direct vitamin D production, circadian rhythm regulation (better sleep), and there's evidence of a direct testicular photoreception pathway that may boost T independently of vitamin D. Just get outside in the morning or midday. Face and forearms exposed. Not through a window.

**11. Cut endocrine disruptors.**

Impact: MODERATE. Replace plastic water bottles and food containers with glass or stainless steel. Don't microwave in plastic. Use natural personal care products when possible. Avoid receipts (BPA). The effect of any single exposure is small but the cumulative load from modern life is not trivial.

**14. Tongkat Ali (Eurycoma longifolia). 200-400mg daily.**

Impact: LOW-MODERATE. Some evidence for reducing SHBG and modestly increasing free testosterone. Not dramatic but one of the few herbal supplements with decent studies behind it. Only worth adding after everything above is handled.

**What's Cope And What's Not**

Let me save you money. These do NOT meaningfully raise testosterone despite being marketed as T boosters:
- **Tribulus terrestris** — cope. No meaningful T increase in multiple studies. The most overhyped T booster in history.
- **D-Aspartic acid** — cope. Short term spike that returns to baseline within weeks. No sustained benefit.
- **Fenugreek** — cope. May slightly reduce DHT conversion but doesn't raise total T meaningfully.
- **Maca** — cope for T. May help libido perception but doesn't actually change testosterone levels.
- **Boron** — mild effect at best. Not worth worrying about.
- **Any product labeled "testosterone booster" at GNC** — cope. If it worked they'd sell it at pharmacies not supplement stores.

Don't spend money on any of these until you've maxed out sleep, leanness, training, vitamin D, zinc, alcohol elimination, and dietary fat. Those seven things produce more hormonal improvement than every supplement on this list combined.

## Lesson 10: Your Age Changes Everything.

Endocrinology · Module 10 of 10

Your hormonal profile at 16 is nothing like your hormonal profile at 35. And what you should be doing at each age is completely different. Teenagers in this community need different advice than 30 year olds. A 45 year old has different options available than a 22 year old. This module breaks down what's happening at each stage, what to prioritize, and when pharmaceutical intervention becomes a legitimate conversation instead of cope.

———

**The Four Stages**

**Stage 1: Puberty (10-18). The Irreplaceable Window.**

This is the single highest leverage period of your entire life for your looks. Not close. During puberty, GH and T are at peak output. GH is running 2-3x adult levels. IGF-1 can be 200-900 ng/mL. These hormones are literally building your face and body. Brow ridge development, jaw growth, maxillary projection, shoulder width, height, muscle foundation. All of it happening RIGHT NOW if you're in this window.

What to do during puberty:
- **Sleep 9+ hours.** Not 8. Nine or more. You are in peak GH production and every hour of deep sleep is building your skeletal structure. Staying up until 3am gaming is literally robbing yourself of facial development you can never get back. This is not reversible. There is no surgery that replicates what natural GH does to your bones during puberty.
- **Eat enough.** This is not the time to cut. Your body needs calories and protein to grow. 1g protein per lb bodyweight minimum. Don't be afraid of eating at surplus if you're lean. You need the building materials.
- **Train with resistance.** Start with bodyweight if you're young, progress to weights. Compound movements. This amplifies the T and GH response during the window when it matters most.
- **Mew.** Proper tongue posture against the palate. During puberty when bones are still developing, consistent mewing actually has a realistic chance of influencing maxillary position. After growth plates close, the window shrinks dramatically.
- **Get sunlight daily.** Vitamin D production, circadian rhythm support, direct benefits to the hormonal environment.
- **NO pharmaceutical intervention.** Do not touch finasteride, do not touch any hormonal supplement, do not even think about TRT. Your HPG axis is still developing. Interfering with it during puberty can cause permanent damage to your natural production. Natural optimization ONLY during this stage.

If you're 14-18 reading this: **you have an advantage that every single person over 25 in this community would pay any amount of money to have back.** Don't waste it. Sleep. Eat. Train. Mew. That's it. The hormones will do the rest.

———

**Stage 2: Prime (18-30). Peak Adult Output.**

Your T and GH are at their adult peak. This is when you should be building the physique and habits that serve you for decades. The optimization playbook from Module 9 applies in full force here.

What to do in your prime:
- **Get baseline bloodwork** at 18-20 and again at 25. Know your numbers during peak years so you have a reference point for life.
- **Max out every lifestyle lever from Module 9.** Sleep, leanness, training, nutrition, vitamin D, zinc, alcohol elimination. You're at peak hormonal output. If your T is low during this period with good lifestyle, there's a genuine underlying issue worth investigating.
- **Build the physique NOW.** Muscle is easiest to build during peak hormonal years. The compound interest of training in your 20s pays dividends in your 30s and 40s. Every year of training matters more when your hormones are optimized.
- **Address hair loss early if it's starting.** Norwood progression accelerates in the early-to-mid 20s for genetically susceptible guys. Finasteride at NW2 prevents far more loss than starting at NW4. The DHT tradeoff discussion from Module 2 is most relevant here.
- **Establish your skincare routine.** Tretinoin, SPF daily, vitamin C serum. Collagen production is still high but starts declining. Starting retinoids in your early 20s is the single best anti-aging move for your skin.

———

**Stage 3: The Decline Begins (30-45). Optimization Matters More.**

Testosterone drops roughly 1% per year after 30. GH drops about 14% per decade. You won't notice it at 31. You'll notice it at 38. Recovery takes longer. Fat comes on easier. Muscle is harder to maintain. Skin starts losing elasticity faster. Energy isn't what it was.

Here's the based truth: **lifestyle optimization becomes MORE important as you age, not less.** A 22 year old can sleep 6 hours, eat garbage, and still look decent because his hormones are carrying him. A 35 year old doing the same thing looks and feels significantly worse because the hormonal buffer is gone.

What to do in the decline phase:
- **Annual bloodwork.** Every year after 30. Track the trajectory. Catch problems early.
- **Double down on sleep and stress management.** These become even more impactful because your baseline hormonal output is lower. You can't afford to lose percentage points to bad sleep when you're already declining naturally.
- **Resistance training is non-negotiable.** Not optional. Not "when I have time." Mandatory. Muscle loss accelerates without resistance training after 30. The guys who look 30 at 45 are the ones who've been training consistently for 15 years.
- **Keep body fat low.** Aromatase activity from body fat hits harder when baseline T is already declining. A 35 year old at 20% BF has significantly worse hormones than a 35 year old at 13% BF. The gap is larger than at 22.
- **Consider therapeutic intervention if T falls below 400 with symptoms.** This is the age range where a conversation with a knowledgeable doctor about TRT becomes legitimate if lifestyle optimization isn't enough. Not as a first line. After everything else is handled.

**Stage 4: Significant Decline (45+). Medical Territory.**

After 45, many men experience noticeable hormonal decline even with great lifestyle habits. T can drop below 400 naturally. GH output is a fraction of what it was. Recovery from training is significantly slower. Body composition gets harder to maintain.

What changes here:
- **TRT becomes a legitimate medical consideration.** If total T is consistently below 300-350 ng/dL with symptoms (fatigue, low libido, muscle loss, depression, fat gain), TRT under medical supervision is a real option. This is not "cheating." This is hormone replacement for clinically deficient levels. Same as someone with hypothyroidism taking thyroid medication.
- **The goal shifts from building to maintaining.** Preventing muscle loss, bone density decline, and functional capacity becomes the primary objective. Resistance training, adequate protein, and sleep are the pillars.
- **Regular monitoring is essential.** Bloodwork every 6-12 months. If on TRT, PSA, hematocrit, estradiol, and lipids need regular monitoring.

———

**When TRT Makes Sense And When It Doesn't**

**TRT is appropriate when:**
- Total T is consistently below 300 ng/dL on multiple tests (not one bad test)
- You have symptoms: persistent fatigue, low libido, muscle loss, depression, poor recovery
- All lifestyle levers from Module 9 have been genuinely optimized for 3+ months
- Secondary causes have been investigated (pituitary issues, thyroid, medications, sleep apnea)
- You're under medical supervision with regular monitoring

**TRT is NOT appropriate when:**
- You're under 25 and your HPG axis is still maturing
- You haven't optimized lifestyle first (most guys in this category)
- You want supraphysiological levels for gym performance (that's a steroid cycle, not TRT)
- You had one low test from an afternoon blood draw after a night of drinking
- You want a shortcut instead of doing the work

**Important:** Exogenous testosterone shuts down your natural production. For young men (under 25), this means your HPG axis, which is still developing, gets permanently disrupted. Recovery of natural production after stopping TRT is not guaranteed, especially if started young. This is not something to take lightly.

———

**The Agepill Is Real But Lifestyle Slows It**

Yes, your hormones decline with age. That's biology. You cannot stop it completely. But the RATE of decline is dramatically affected by lifestyle. Studies show that men who maintain healthy body composition, exercise regularly, sleep well, and avoid alcohol experience significantly slower testosterone decline than men who don't.

A well-optimized 40 year old can have better hormonal status than a poorly optimized 25 year old. That's not cope. That's data. The agepill is real but it's not a death sentence if you're actually doing the work.

The guys who look incredible at 40 and 50 didn't start at 40. They've been stacking decades of good habits. The compound interest of consistent training, good nutrition, adequate sleep, and low body fat is staggering over 10-20 years. Start now regardless of your age.

———

Your age determines your hormonal landscape but your lifestyle determines how you navigate it. Teenagers: sleep 9 hours and don't waste the window. 20s: build the foundation hard. 30s: optimize more aggressively because the margin gets thinner. 40s+: monitor closely and consider medical options if truly needed. The protocol changes but the principles don't. Sleep, leanness, training, nutrition, and data. That's the game at every age.
