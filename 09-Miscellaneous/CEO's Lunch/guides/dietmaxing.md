## Lesson 1: Calories Are The Master Variable.

Diet · Module 1 of 10

[Image: image.png]

I don't care what supplements you take, what skincare you use, or how many hours you spend mewing. If you don't have your calories dialed in, none of it matters. Calories are the single variable that controls whether you get lean enough to actually show your bone structure or stay bloated forever wondering why your jaw doesn't pop.

This is water to most of the fitness world but I watch guys in here every single day skipping this step. You're buying collagen peptides while eating 3,200 calories with no idea what your body actually needs. That's cope. Fix this first.

———

**The Only 3 States That Exist**

Your body is always in one of three states relative to how many calories it burns in a day. That number is called your TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure). Every decision you make about food comes back to this number.
- **Caloric surplus** -- eating more than you burn. Your body takes the extra and uses it to build muscle (if you're training) or stores it as fat (if you're not). This is how you bulk.
- **Caloric deficit** -- eating less than you burn. Your body pulls from stored fat to make up the difference. This is how you get lean. This is how your cheekbones and jawline finally show up.
- **Maintenance** -- eating roughly what you burn. Weight stays the same. You're just... existing.

That's it. There's no fourth option. There's no magic food that burns fat. There's no hack that bypasses thermodynamics. Surplus, deficit, or maintenance. Pick one based on where you're at.

[Image: image.png]

———

**How To Calculate Your TDEE (Step By Step)**

Most of you have never actually done this. You're guessing. Stop guessing.

**Step 1. Calculate your BMR.**

This is how many calories your body burns just to keep you alive. Breathing, pumping blood, regulating temperature. You'd burn this many calories lying in bed doing literally nothing all day.

Use the Mifflin-St Jeor equation. It's the most accurate one for most guys:

**BMR = (10 x weight in kg) + (6.25 x height in cm) - (5 x age) + 5**

Example: you're 80kg, 180cm, 22 years old.

(10 x 80) + (6.25 x 180) - (5 x 22) + 5 = 800 + 1125 - 110 + 5 = **1,820 calories**

That's your BMR. Your body burns roughly 1,820 calories just existing.

**Step 2. Multiply by your activity level.**

Be honest here. Most of you are going to pick "moderate" when you're actually sedentary. If you sit at a desk or in class most of the day and train 3-4x a week, you're "lightly active" at best.
- **Sedentary** (desk job, no training) -- BMR x 1.2
- **Lightly active** (train 3-4x/week, otherwise sitting) -- BMR x 1.375
- **Moderately active** (train 4-5x/week, somewhat active job) -- BMR x 1.55
- **Very active** (train 6x+/week, physical job) -- BMR x 1.725

Using the example above with "lightly active": 1,820 x 1.375 = **2,503 calories**

That's your TDEE. That's your maintenance. That's the number everything is measured against.

[Image: image.png]

**Step 3. Pick your phase.**

Where are you right now? Be honest.
- **Above 15% body fat?** You're cutting. Period. Set a deficit of 400-500 calories below your TDEE. Using our example: 2,503 - 450 = roughly **2,050 calories per day.**
- **Below 15% but want more muscle?** Lean bulk. Add 200-300 calories above TDEE. So roughly **2,750 calories per day.**
- **Happy where you're at?** Maintain at TDEE. Eat roughly **2,500 per day.**

If you're above 15% BF there is zero reason to be bulking. You're just adding fat on top of fat and making your face puffier. I don't care how badly you want to "get big." Get lean first. Your face will thank you.

———

**How To Actually Track This**

Knowing your number means nothing if you don't track what you eat. And here's the thing most of you don't want to hear: people underestimate their calorie intake by 30-50%. That's not a small margin of error. That's the difference between a deficit and a surplus.

**Download MyFitnessPal or MacroFactor.** MacroFactor is better because it adjusts your TDEE based on your actual weight data over time, which means your estimated TDEE gets more accurate every week. MyFitnessPal is free and works fine if you just need to log food.

**Here's what you do:**

1. Weigh your food with a kitchen scale. Not eyeballing. Not "about a cup." Weigh it in grams. A $15 kitchen scale is the single best investment you will make for your physique.
2. Log everything. Every meal, every snack, every splash of olive oil. The calories you "forget" are usually the ones keeping you fat.
3. Weigh yourself every morning after using the bathroom, before eating or drinking. Write it down.
4. Use your **weekly average weight** to track progress. Not daily. Daily weight fluctuates 1-3 lbs based on water, sodium, carbs, sleep. The weekly average is what matters.
5. If your weekly average isn't moving after 2 full weeks, adjust by 150-200 calories. Drop 150 on a cut. Add 150 on a bulk.

[Image: image.png]

**Track for a minimum of 4 weeks before you decide anything isn't working.** Most of you quit after 5 days because you don't see abs yet. That's not how bodies work. Commit to 4 weeks of honest tracking.

———

**The Floors You Never Cross**

Cutting calories too hard is one of the most common ways guys sabotage themselves. You think eating 1,200 calories will get you lean faster. It won't. Here's what actually happens:
- Your testosterone tanks. Your body downregulates T production when it thinks you're starving.
- You lose muscle. Without enough calories your body eats its own muscle for energy. Now you're skinny fat instead of lean.
- Your energy crashes. Training suffers. Recovery suffers. Sleep suffers. Everything gets worse.
- You rebound. You can't sustain it, you binge, and you end up fatter than when you started.

**Never go below 1,600 calories.** That's the floor for males. If your calculated deficit puts you below 1,600, eat 1,600 and add more activity instead.

**Never let protein drop below 1.6g per kg of bodyweight.** On a cut it should be higher (2.0-2.2g/kg) but 1.6 is the absolute floor. We'll cover macros in detail in Module 2.

———

**Why This Matters For Your Face**

Here's the part most of you need to hear. Your face at 20% body fat and your face at 12% body fat are literally two different faces. The cheekbones you think you don't have? They're there. The jawline you think is weak? It might not be. The hollow cheeks you see on models? That's low body fat plus decent zygomatic structure.

You've never seen your real face because you've never been lean enough to see it.

Getting your calories right isn't just about having a six pack. It's the single highest ROI thing you can do for your face. More than skincare. More than mewing. More than any supplement. **Leanmaxxing starts here.**

———

**Mistakes that are costing you right now**
- **Never calculating your TDEE.** You're flying blind. You have no idea if you're in a surplus or deficit. That's why nothing is changing.
- **Eyeballing portions.** You think that chicken breast is 200 calories. It's 350. You think that "drizzle" of olive oil is 40 calories. It's 120. Get a scale.
- **Crash dieting.** Sub-1,400 calorie diets tank your testosterone, eat your muscle, and end in a binge. A 400-500 calorie deficit is all you need.
- **Bulking while fat.** If you're above 15% BF and adding a surplus, you're just getting fatter and increasing estrogen conversion. Cut first.
- **Quitting after a week.** You didn't gain the fat in a week. You're not losing it in a week. Commit to 4 weeks minimum.

———

Go calculate your TDEE right now. Use the formula above or go to [tdeecalculator.net](http://tdeecalculator.net) if you want it done for you. Pick your phase. Download an app. Get a kitchen scale. Start tracking tomorrow morning. This is the foundation everything else in this course is built on. Skip it and you're building on sand.


## Lesson 2: Macros Are Not Optional.

Diet · Module 2 of 10

[Image: image.png]

Module 1 told you how much to eat. This module tells you what to eat. And before you say "calories are all that matter" -- yeah, for weight loss they are. But for whether you lose fat or muscle? For whether your hormones stay intact or crash? For whether your skin looks good or like garbage? Macros decide all of that.

Two guys can eat the exact same number of calories and one loses fat while keeping muscle and the other just gets skinny fat. The difference is macros. This is not optional if you're serious about your physique and your face.

———

**The 3 Macros and What They Do**

**Protein -- 4 calories per gram.** This is the one that matters most. Protein builds muscle, repairs tissue, keeps you full longer than anything else, and burns more calories during digestion than carbs or fat. Your body literally cannot maintain muscle without adequate protein. On a cut, protein is the difference between getting lean and getting skinny fat. Non-negotiable.

**Carbohydrates -- 4 calories per gram.** Your body's primary fuel source. Stored in your muscles as glycogen, which is what powers your workouts. Go too low on carbs and your training suffers, your energy tanks, and your mood goes to hell. Carbs are not the enemy. Being in a surplus is the enemy.

**Fat -- 9 calories per gram.** This is what makes your hormones. Testosterone literally cannot be produced without dietary fat. It also carries vitamins A, D, E, and K into your body. Drop fat too low and your T crashes, your skin dries out, and your joints start hurting. Fat has a hard floor you do not go below.

[Image: image.png]

———

**How To Set Your Macros (Exact Numbers)**

You set these in order. Protein first, fat second, carbs fill whatever is left. Always this order.

**Step 1. Set protein.**
- **Cutting:** 2.2g per kg of bodyweight. Higher on a cut because your body is more likely to break down muscle for energy. More protein protects against that.
- **Bulking:** 1.8g per kg. You can get away with slightly less because the surplus itself is muscle-sparing.
- **Maintaining:** 1.6-2.0g per kg.

**Step 2. Set fat.**
- **Minimum floor:** 0.8g per kg of bodyweight. Never go below this. Ever. Your testosterone depends on it.
- **Target:** 1.0-1.2g per kg. This keeps hormones humming and gives you enough fat-soluble vitamin absorption.

**Step 3. Fill the rest with carbs.**

Take your total calorie target, subtract protein calories and fat calories, and whatever is left goes to carbs.

———

**Real Example -- 80kg Guy Cutting at 2,050 Calories**

Let's use the same guy from Module 1. 80kg, TDEE of 2,500, cutting at 2,050.
- **Protein:** 80kg x 2.2 = 176g. That's 176 x 4 = 704 calories from protein.
- **Fat:** 80kg x 1.0 = 80g. That's 80 x 9 = 720 calories from fat.
- **Carbs:** 2,050 - 704 - 720 = 626 calories left. 626 / 4 = 156g of carbs.

**Daily macros: 176g protein, 80g fat, 156g carbs. 2,050 total calories.**

That's it. That's what you plug into MyFitnessPal or MacroFactor. Not a guess. Not vibes. Actual numbers.

[Image: image.png]

———

**How To Actually Hit 170+ Grams Of Protein**

This is where most of you fail. You set the target and then eat 90g of protein because you have no idea what high-protein eating actually looks like. Here's what 170-180g of protein in a day looks like across 4 meals:

**Meal 1** -- 4 eggs scrambled + 100g chicken breast = ~50g protein

**Meal 2** -- 200g Greek yogurt + scoop of whey = ~45g protein

**Meal 3** -- 200g chicken thigh or beef + rice = ~45g protein

**Meal 4** -- 150g white fish or cottage cheese = ~35g protein

That's 175g protein spread across 4 meals. 30-50g per meal. This matters because your body can only use so much protein for muscle building at once. Eating 100g in one meal and 20g in the others is not the same as spreading it out.

**Best protein sources:** chicken breast, chicken thigh, beef, eggs, white fish (tilapia, cod), Greek yogurt, cottage cheese, whey protein. These should be the backbone of your diet. Everything else is a side character.

[Image: image.png]

———

**Timing Your Carbs**

Carbs aren't the enemy but you shouldn't just eat them randomly either. Carbs go around your training.
- **Pre-workout (1-2 hours before):** This is where your biggest carb meal goes. Rice, oats, potato. This fills your glycogen stores so you actually have energy to push hard.
- **Post-workout:** Second biggest carb window. Your muscles are primed to absorb glycogen. Rice, fruit, potato.
- **Other meals:** Keep carbs moderate. Protein and fat focused.

On rest days you can shift some carb calories to fat calories. This is not mandatory but some guys find it helps with energy and satiety on days they're not training. Don't overthink this. Hit your totals.

———

**Mistakes that are costing you right now**
- **Not tracking protein.** If you only track one macro, track this one. Most guys think they're eating 150g when they're eating 80g. Weigh and log it.
- **Cutting fat too low.** Some of you go below 40g of fat per day because you think fat makes you fat. It doesn't. Calories in surplus make you fat. Low fat just kills your testosterone, dries your skin, and destroys your joints.
- **Fearing carbs.** Keto works for fat loss but it kills your gym performance. If you're gymmaxxing and leanmaxxing at the same time, you need carbs for fuel. Just put them around your training.
- **Eating all your protein in one or two meals.** Your body has a ceiling for how much protein it can use per sitting for muscle building. 30-50g per meal across 3-5 meals beats one massive 100g protein meal.
- **Not setting macros in the right order.** Protein first. Fat second. Carbs fill the rest. Every single time. If you start with carbs you'll have no room left for what actually matters.

———

Go back to your calorie number from Module 1. Multiply your bodyweight in kg by 2.2 for protein (if cutting) or 1.8 (if bulking). Multiply by 1.0 for fat. Fill the rest with carbs. Plug those into your tracking app. Aim for 30-50g protein per meal across 4 meals. That's the system. Run it for 4 weeks before changing anything.


## Lesson 3: The 6 Micros That Actually Matter.

Diet · Module 3 of 10

[Image: image.png]

You got your calories dialed. You got your macros set. Now here's the layer most of you are completely sleeping on. Micronutrients. Vitamins and minerals. The stuff that makes your hormones work, your skin clear, your hair grow, and your sleep actually recover you.

Most guys eating a standard Western diet are deficient in at least 2-3 of the ones that matter most. And the symptoms don't show up as "you have a deficiency." They show up as low energy, bad skin, thinning hair, poor sleep, low libido, and brain fog. You've been blaming your genetics when it might just be that you're eating garbage and not getting enough zinc.

———

**The Big 6 -- What You Actually Need**

I'm not going to give you a list of 30 micronutrients. That's useless. These are the 6 that have the biggest impact on how you look and feel, and the 6 that most guys are deficient in.

**1. Vitamin D**

Your body makes testosterone partly through vitamin D. It also affects bone density, immune function, and mood. If you live anywhere that isn't sunny year-round, you're probably deficient. Studies show that supplementing D3 in deficient men directly raises testosterone levels.
- **How to get it:** 20-30 minutes of direct sunlight on your skin daily. If that's not happening (and for most of you it isn't), supplement 2,000-4,000 IU of Vitamin D3 daily.
- **Must take with a meal that contains fat.** D3 is fat-soluble. Take it on an empty stomach and you're wasting it.

**2. Zinc**

Directly involved in testosterone production, skin healing, and hair growth. Zinc deficiency literally causes low T, acne, and hair loss. Oysters are the single best food source. Most of you aren't eating oysters though, so supplementing makes sense.
- **Dose:** 15-25mg daily of zinc picolinate or zinc bisglycinate. These forms absorb well. Zinc oxide (the cheap stuff in most multivitamins) is poorly absorbed.
- **Take with food.** Zinc on an empty stomach makes you nauseous.
- **Do not exceed 40mg long-term without adding 1-2mg copper.** High zinc depletes copper. This is the number one zinc supplementation mistake.

**3. Magnesium**

This one is a sleeper. Magnesium is involved in over 300 enzymatic processes in your body. Most relevant to you: it directly improves sleep quality and lowers cortisol. Bad sleep and high cortisol means higher body fat, worse recovery, puffier face, dark circles. Magnesium fixes this for a lot of guys.
- **Dose:** 300-400mg of magnesium glycinate before bed. Glycinate is the best form because it's highly bioavailable and has a calming effect. Magnesium oxide (the cheap pharmacy stuff) just gives you diarrhea.
- **Best food sources:** dark chocolate (85%+), nuts, pumpkin seeds, leafy greens.

**4. Omega-3 (EPA/DHA)**

Controls inflammation throughout your entire body. Inflammation is what makes your skin red, your face puffy, your joints hurt, and your recovery slow. Omega-3 actively resolves inflammation. The Western diet is massively skewed toward omega-6 (from seed oils and processed food) and deficient in omega-3.
- **Best source:** fatty fish 2-3x per week. Salmon, mackerel, sardines.
- **If you're not eating fish consistently:** supplement 2-3g of EPA/DHA daily. Not 2-3g of fish oil -- check the label for actual EPA and DHA content. Most cheap fish oil capsules have like 300mg of actual EPA/DHA per capsule. You'd need 7-8 of those. Get a concentrated one.

**5. Vitamin C**

Your body cannot make collagen without vitamin C. Collagen is what keeps your skin firm, elastic, and youthful. Vitamin C also protects against UV damage and helps absorb iron. If you're supplementing collagen (Module 4) without getting enough vitamin C, you're wasting your money.
- **Best sources:** bell peppers (more vitamin C than oranges), citrus, strawberries, broccoli.
- **Supplement:** 500-1,000mg daily if your diet is inconsistent.

**6. Vitamin K2**

K2 tells calcium where to go in your body. When you take Vitamin D, your body absorbs more calcium. Without K2, that calcium can end up in your arteries instead of your bones. D3 and K2 always go together.
- **Best sources:** hard cheese, egg yolks, natto (fermented soybeans), grass-fed butter.
- **Supplement:** 100-200mcg of the MK-7 form daily. Take it with D3.

[Image: image.png]

———

**The Actual Protocol**

Here's exactly what your daily micronutrient stack looks like. This covers 90% of what you need:

**Morning with breakfast (must contain fat):**
- Vitamin D3 -- 2,000-4,000 IU
- Vitamin K2 (MK-7) -- 100-200mcg
- Omega-3 -- 2-3g EPA/DHA
- Zinc picolinate -- 15-25mg

**Before bed:**
- Magnesium glycinate -- 300-400mg

**Throughout the day from food:**
- Vitamin C from bell peppers, citrus, berries (or supplement 500mg if diet is inconsistent)

That's it. This costs maybe $30-40/month total and covers the deficiencies that are probably tanking your hormones, sleep, and skin right now. This mogs every $80 "test booster" or "super greens" powder on the market.

[Image: image.png]

———

**Why Your Multivitamin Is Probably Garbage**

I know some of you are thinking "I take a multivitamin so I'm good." You're not. Here's why:
- Most multivitamins use the cheapest, worst-absorbed forms. Zinc oxide instead of zinc picolinate. Magnesium oxide instead of glycinate. Vitamin D2 instead of D3. You're paying for ingredients that pass through your body and end up in the toilet.
- The doses are often too low to fix an actual deficiency. 400 IU of vitamin D when you need 3,000. 10mg of zinc when you need 20.
- Some nutrients compete for absorption when taken together. A well-designed targeted stack beats a kitchen sink multivitamin every time.

Targeted supplementation of the specific deficiencies you actually have is always better than a generic multi. Get bloodwork if you can. At minimum, get the Big 6 covered with quality forms.

———

**Mistakes that are costing you right now**
- **Taking zinc long-term without copper.** Zinc and copper compete for absorption. High zinc for months without copper supplementation depletes your copper stores. Add 1-2mg copper or cycle off zinc for a week every month.
- **Taking vitamin D without K2.** D3 increases calcium absorption. K2 directs that calcium to your bones instead of your arteries. Always pair them. Always.
- **Taking fat-soluble vitamins on an empty stomach.** Vitamins A, D, E, and K need dietary fat to absorb. Take them with a meal or you're wasting them.
- **Ignoring omega-3.** This is one of the most consistently deficient nutrients in modern diets and one of the highest-ROI supplements you can take. Your skin, joints, brain, and inflammation levels all depend on it.
- **Spending money on test boosters before covering the basics.** D3, zinc, and magnesium do more for your testosterone than any $60 supplement with a jacked dude on the label. Cover the foundation first.

———

Order D3, K2, zinc picolinate, magnesium glycinate, and a quality omega-3. Set up your morning and nighttime stack. Start tomorrow. This is one of the easiest wins in the entire course and most of you are leaving it on the table.


## Lesson 4: Eat For Your Skin And Hair.

Diet · Module 4 of 10

[Image: image.png]

Your skin and hair are direct reflections of what you eat. Full stop. You can spend $200 a month on serums and topicals but if your diet is trash, your skin will still look mid and your hair will still be thinning. No topical product fully compensates for a garbage diet. It's like putting premium wax on a rusted car.

This module covers the specific nutrients that drive collagen production, skin clarity, and hair growth from the inside out. This is skinmaxxing from the nutrition angle and most of you are completely ignoring it.

———

**Why Your Diet Shows Up On Your Face**

Your skin is your largest organ. It's constantly rebuilding itself using the raw materials you give it through food. Collagen (the protein that keeps your skin firm) and keratin (the protein your hair is made of) both require amino acids from dietary protein to be produced. If you're not eating enough protein, your skin gets loose and your hair gets thin. Simple as that.

But it's not just protein. There are three processes happening in your skin every day that your diet directly controls:
- **Collagen synthesis.** Your body is constantly making new collagen to replace old collagen. This requires protein, vitamin C, and specific amino acids. Without these, your skin loses elasticity and firmness.
- **Glycation.** This is the big one nobody talks about. When you eat sugar, the glucose molecules bind to collagen fibers and make them stiff and brittle. This is called glycation. It literally degrades the structural protein in your skin. High sugar diet = faster aging, duller skin, more wrinkles. This is not speculation. It's well documented.
- **Inflammation.** Chronic inflammation from bad food shows up as redness, acne, puffiness, and accelerated aging. We cover this in depth in Module 7 but the short version is: processed food and sugar drive inflammation, whole foods and omega-3 resolve it.

[Image: image.png]

———

**The Nutrients That Build Your Skin And Hair**

**Protein (the foundation).** Collagen is a protein. Keratin is a protein. Your body cannot build either one without adequate dietary protein. If you're eating less than 1.6g/kg and wondering why your skin and hair look bad, there's your answer. This is the single most important nutritional input for skin and hair, and most of the "skin nutrition" guides online bury it under a list of exotic supplements.

**Collagen peptides.** Supplementing 10-15g of collagen peptides daily has been shown to improve skin elasticity and hydration. But here's the thing the supplement companies don't tell you: collagen supplements don't directly replace your skin collagen. They break down into amino acids and those amino acids stimulate your body's own collagen production. They work, just not the way the marketing says. **Always take collagen with vitamin C.** Your body needs vitamin C as a cofactor to synthesize collagen. Without it, the collagen supplement is largely wasted.

**Vitamin C.** Required for collagen synthesis. Full stop. Without enough vitamin C, your body literally cannot make new collagen. Bell peppers have more vitamin C than oranges. Eat them. Also protects against UV damage and helps your body absorb iron (which matters for hair, keep reading).

**Iron.** Iron deficiency is one of the most common and most overlooked causes of hair shedding. Your hair follicles need iron to function properly. When iron drops, your body pulls it from "non-essential" places first, and hair follicles are one of the first to get starved. This causes diffuse thinning (telogen effluvium) that looks like your hair is just getting thinner everywhere. **Do not supplement iron without getting bloodwork first.** Too much iron is dangerous. Get your ferritin levels checked. If they're low, your doctor can guide supplementation.

**Biotin.** I need to be honest about this one because it's the most overhyped skin and hair supplement out there. Biotin only helps if you're actually biotin deficient, and most people eating a normal diet are not. The supplement industry has convinced everyone that biotin = hair growth. The evidence doesn't support that unless there's an actual deficiency. Focus your money on protein, vitamin C, and getting your iron checked first.

**Antioxidants.** Free radical damage from UV, pollution, and metabolic processes accelerates skin aging. Antioxidants neutralize free radicals. Best sources: blueberries, green tea, dark chocolate (85%+), tomatoes (lycopene). Eat these regularly rather than supplementing individual antioxidants.

———

[Image: image.png]

**The Skin And Hair Nutrition Protocol**

1. **Hit protein targets every single day.** Minimum 1.6g/kg. This is the foundation. No supplement compensates for low protein.
2. **Supplement collagen peptides.** 10-15g daily mixed into a drink or smoothie. Take it with vitamin C (eat an orange, some bell peppers, or take 500mg vitamin C at the same time).
3. **Eat vitamin C rich foods daily.** Bell peppers, citrus, strawberries, broccoli. If your diet is inconsistent, add 500mg supplement.
4. **Cut sugar and refined carbs.** This is the single most impactful dietary change for skin quality. Reduces glycation (collagen degradation), lowers insulin-driven sebum production (less acne), and reduces inflammation. Within 2-3 weeks of cutting sugar, most guys notice visibly clearer skin.
5. **Get your iron checked via bloodwork.** Especially if you're experiencing diffuse hair thinning. Do not randomly supplement iron. Get tested first.
6. **Eat antioxidant-rich foods regularly.** Berries, green tea, dark leafy greens, dark chocolate. Build these into your regular rotation.

———

**What A Skin-Optimized Day Of Eating Looks Like**

**Breakfast:** 4 eggs (protein + biotin + fat-soluble vitamins) + bell pepper slices (vitamin C) + blueberries (antioxidants)

**Lunch:** Salmon or chicken with rice and broccoli (protein + omega-3 + vitamin C)

**Snack:** Greek yogurt with mixed berries + collagen peptides stirred in (protein + collagen + antioxidants + vitamin C)

**Dinner:** Beef with sweet potato and spinach (protein + iron + zinc + antioxidants)

Notice what's not here? Candy, soda, fast food, processed garbage. The stuff that glycates your collagen, spikes your insulin, and makes your skin look 5 years older than it should.

[Image: image.png]

———

**Mistakes that are costing you right now**
- **Spending $50/month on biotin supplements.** Unless you've tested deficient, biotin isn't doing anything for you. That money is better spent on collagen peptides + vitamin C which actually have evidence behind them.
- **Ignoring sugar's effect on your skin.** Glycation is one of the most underappreciated causes of premature aging. Every time you drink a soda or eat candy you're literally degrading the collagen in your face. Stop.
- **Taking collagen without vitamin C.** Your body needs vitamin C to actually synthesize collagen. Taking collagen peptides without it is like buying wood but not having nails. The raw material is there but nothing gets built.
- **Treating hair loss with diet alone when the cause is DHT.** If your hair loss follows a Norwood pattern (receding temples, thinning crown), that's androgenetic alopecia. It's hormonal. Diet can optimize the environment for your hair but it will not stop genetic pattern hair loss. That requires finasteride or dutasteride. See the Hair module.
- **Not eating enough protein and wondering why your skin looks bad.** This is the most basic one and the one most people miss. Your skin and hair are literally built from protein. Eat enough of it.

———

Hit your protein. Supplement collagen + vitamin C. Cut the sugar. Get your iron checked if your hair is thinning. Eat real food with antioxidants. This is not complicated but it requires consistency. Your skin and hair 3 months from now will look completely different from today if you actually do this.


## Lesson 5: Leanmaxxing Is The Cheat Code.

Diet · Module 5 of 10

[Image: image.png]

I've said it before and I'll say it again: leanmaxxing is the single highest ROI thing you can do for your face. Bar none. More than skincare. More than mewing. More than any supplement. Getting to sub-12% body fat reveals bone structure that most of you have never seen on yourselves. Cheekbones pop. Jawlines sharpen. The ogee curve appears. Hollow cheeks develop. You literally look like a different person.

Most guys have never been truly lean. You think you're at 15% when you're actually at 20%. You've never seen your real face. This module tells you exactly how to get there without losing muscle, tanking your hormones, or spending a year being miserable.

———

**What Happens To Your Face At Each BF%**

This is not theory. This is what actually happens as you drop body fat:

## Lesson 6: Stop Dirty Bulking.

Diet · Module 6 of 10

[Image: image.png]

I see this constantly. Guy finally gets lean, starts seeing his jawline, getting compliments, looking good. Then he decides he wants to get big so he starts "bulking" by eating everything in sight. 3,500 calories. 4,000 calories. Pizza, burgers, whatever fits. Two months later his face is puffy again, his jawline is gone, and he's back to bloatmaxxing. Months of cutting, wasted.

Here's the thing most of you don't understand: eating more does not build muscle faster. Your body has a maximum rate of muscle growth. Anything you eat beyond that gets stored as fat. A 1,000 calorie surplus does not build more muscle than a 300 calorie surplus. It just builds more fat. A dirty bulk is not a bulk. It's just getting fat with extra steps.

———

**How Much Muscle You Can Actually Build**

This is the part you need to hear because it will calibrate your expectations and prevent you from eating yourself fat again.

Maximum natural muscle gain rates (per month, assuming you're actually training hard):
- **Beginner (0-1 year of proper training):** 1.0-1.5kg per month. This is your golden window. Newbie gains are real and you should take full advantage.
- **Intermediate (1-3 years):** 0.5-1.0kg per month. Still solid progress but it's slowing down.
- **Advanced (3+ years):** 0.25-0.5kg per month. This is where impatient guys start dirty bulking because progress feels slow. Don't.

So if you're an intermediate lifter, the absolute maximum muscle you can build in a month is about 1kg. That's 2.2 lbs. That's it. If the scale is going up 3-4kg a month, the extra is fat. Not muscle. Fat. And that fat is going straight to your face, your gut, and your love handles.

**The Clean Bulk Protocol**

**Rule 1: Start lean.**

Do not begin a bulk above 13-14% body fat. I'm serious. Here's why: the fatter you are, the more your body converts testosterone to estrogen through a process called aromatization. So bulking while fat literally worsens your hormonal environment. You gain more fat, less muscle, and your face gets puffier. Start a bulk between 10-13% BF. That's the sweet spot.

**Rule 2: Small surplus.**

200-300 calories above your TDEE. That's it. Not 500. Definitely not 1,000. Your body can only build so much muscle per day and you only need a slight surplus to fuel that process. Anything beyond 300 calories over maintenance is just becoming body fat.

For our 80kg guy with a TDEE of 2,500:
- Bulking calories: 2,750-2,800
- Protein: 1.8-2.0g/kg = 144-160g
- Fat: 1.0g/kg = 80g
- Carbs: fill the rest = ~280-300g

**Rule 3: Train hard and progressively.**

A caloric surplus only converts to muscle if there's a training stimulus demanding it. If you're eating in a surplus and not training hard enough, all those extra calories just become fat. Progressive overload. More weight on the bar over time. More reps. More volume. That's what tells your body to build muscle.

**Rule 4: Monitor and stop at 15-16%.**

Track your body composition monthly. When you hit 15-16% body fat, stop the bulk and cut back down to 10-12%. Then bulk again. This cycle of lean bulk then mini-cut keeps you looking good year-round instead of spending half the year as a puffy mess.

This is the part most guys don't know about. When your body fat increases, an enzyme called aromatase becomes more active. Aromatase converts testosterone into estrogen. So the fatter you get on a bulk, the worse your testosterone-to-estrogen ratio becomes. Which means:
- More fat gain (estrogen promotes fat storage)
- More water retention (puffy face, bloated look)
- Potentially gyno if it gets bad enough
- Worse muscle building because the hormonal environment deteriorated

This is why dirty bulking is actually counterproductive. You're creating a hormonal environment that makes it harder to build muscle and easier to gain fat. The guys who stay lean during their bulk literally build muscle more efficiently because their hormones are optimized.

## Lesson 7: Inflammation Is Ruining Your Face.

Diet · Module 7 of 10

You're doing everything else right. Calories are set. Macros are dialed. You're getting lean. But your face still looks puffy. Your skin has a reddish undertone. You're breaking out for no reason. And the standard Western diet is basically an inflammation factory. Seed oils, sugar, processed food, alcohol. All of it is driving inflammation in your body right now.

**What Chronic Inflammation Does To Your Looks**

This isn't the kind of inflammation you get from a sprained ankle. That's acute inflammation and it goes away. Chronic low-grade inflammation sits in your body for months or years at a low level, constantly doing damage. Here's what it does specifically to your appearance:
- **Facial puffiness and water retention.** Inflammation drives fluid retention in your tissues. This is why some guys are at 12% body fat but still look soft and puffy in the face. The leanness is there but the inflammation is masking it.

**What's Causing It (The Stuff You Need To Remove)**

The ideal omega-6 to omega-3 ratio is about 4:1. The average Western diet is 15-20:1. That's a massive inflammatory load coming from oils that are in literally everything. Fast food is cooked in it. Packaged food is made with it. Restaurant food is drowning in it. This is the single biggest dietary source of inflammation for most people.

**Replace with:** olive oil (for dressings and low-medium heat cooking), avocado oil (for high heat cooking), butter or ghee.

**2. Refined sugar and high-fructose corn syrup.** Sugar drives inflammation through two mechanisms: it creates Advanced Glycation End products (AGEs) that damage collagen, and it spikes insulin which triggers inflammatory cascading. Every soda, every candy bar, every "cheat meal" with sugary sauces is driving inflammation that shows up on your face.

**3. Ultra-processed food.** Anything with an ingredients list you can't pronounce. Preservatives, emulsifiers, artificial colors, and the combination of seed oils + sugar + additives creates an inflammatory cocktail. If it comes in a bag or box and has 15+ ingredients, it's probably pro-inflammatory.

**4. Alcohol.** This one hurts to hear but even moderate drinking drives measurable inflammation. Alcohol increases intestinal permeability (leaky gut), which lets bacterial toxins into your bloodstream, triggering a systemic inflammatory response. Even 2-3 drinks per week raises inflammatory markers. Your face after a night of drinking isn't just dehydration. It's inflammation.

Without black pepper, most of the curcumin passes through you unused. Add turmeric + black pepper to food, or take a curcumin supplement with piperine.

**Green tea.** Contains EGCG, an anti-inflammatory catechin. 2-3 cups daily. Also has a modest metabolic boost which helps on a cut.

**Dark chocolate (85%+).** High in flavanols which reduce inflammatory markers. 20-30g daily. Stick to 85% or higher. Milk chocolate is too much sugar and defeats the purpose.

## Lesson 8: When You Eat Matters Less Than You Think.

Diet · Module 8 of 10

[Image: image.png]

I need to be upfront with you on this one. Meal timing is the least important variable in your nutrition. Calories come first. Macros come second. Food quality comes third. Meal timing is fourth. Most of the stuff you see online about "anabolic windows" and "fasting for fat loss" is either massively exaggerated or straight up wrong.

That said, there are a few timing principles that actually matter, specifically for growth hormone and training performance. So let me separate the real from the cope.

———

**Intermittent Fasting: What It Actually Does**

IF is not magic. When calories and protein are matched, intermittent fasting produces the exact same fat loss as eating normally throughout the day. Let me say that again. **Same calories, same protein = same results whether you eat in 8 hours or 16 hours.** The fat loss comes from the deficit, not from the fasting window.

So why does anyone do it? Two reasons that actually matter:

**1. Growth hormone pulsing.** When you're fasted, your insulin is low. When insulin is low, growth hormone can pulse more freely. GH is released in bursts throughout the day, and insulin actively suppresses those bursts. So fasting windows do create a hormonal environment where GH output increases. This is real and measurable. But it's a modest effect, not a game-changer by itself.

**2. Appetite control.** Some guys find it easier to eat in a deficit when they compress their eating window. If your total daily target is 2,000 calories, eating two 800-calorie meals and a 400-calorie snack in an 8-hour window can feel more satisfying than spreading 2,000 across 5 small meals that leave you hungry after each one. This is a preference thing, not a physiological advantage.

If IF works for your lifestyle and you can still hit your protein target, use it. If it makes hitting protein harder or kills your training performance, don't use it. It's a tool, not a religion.

[Image: image.png]

———

**The Timing Principles That Actually Matter**

Here are the few meal timing rules that have real evidence behind them. Everything else is noise.

**1. Don't train fasted unless you're adapted to it.**

Training on an empty stomach means low glycogen stores. Low glycogen = less energy = worse performance = less stimulus for muscle growth. If you're doing IF and your training falls in your fasting window, you're leaving gains on the table. Either shift your eating window to cover your training, or at minimum have a small carb source before you train (even 20-30g of a fast-digesting carb like a banana or rice cake).

**2. Eat carbs before and after training.**

Your biggest carb meals should bookend your training. Pre-workout carbs (1-2 hours before) fill glycogen stores so you have fuel. Post-workout carbs help replenish what you used. This is not about an "anabolic window." It's about performance and recovery.

Example for a guy eating 160g carbs on a cut:
- Pre-workout meal: 60-70g carbs (rice, oats, potato)
- Post-workout meal: 50-60g carbs (rice, fruit, potato)
- Other meals: 30-40g carbs split across remaining meals

**3. Protein spread across the day beats front-loading.**

Muscle protein synthesis has a ceiling per meal. Eating 150g of protein at dinner and 20g at each other meal is not as effective as eating 40g per meal across 4 meals. We covered this in Module 2 but it's worth repeating here because it's the one timing principle that consistently makes a difference.

**4. Don't eat a massive meal right before bed.**

When you eat a big meal, insulin spikes. Insulin suppresses growth hormone. Your biggest GH pulse of the day happens during the first 1-2 hours of deep sleep. If you eat a huge meal at 10pm and go to bed at 11pm, you're spiking insulin right when your body wants to release GH. Leave at least 2 hours between your last big meal and sleep.

Small exception: a small portion of casein protein (slow-digesting) before bed has modest evidence for improving overnight muscle protein synthesis. A scoop of casein or some cottage cheese an hour before bed is fine. A 1,200-calorie feast at midnight is not.

[Image: image.png]

———

**Two Approaches: Pick One**

**Option A: Intermittent Fasting (16:8)**

1. Fast from 8pm to 12pm the next day. Water, black coffee, and tea are fine during the fast.
2. Break your fast at noon with a protein-rich meal. 40-50g protein.
3. Train in the afternoon (within your eating window).
4. Post-workout meal with protein and carbs.
5. Last meal by 7:30-8pm. Give yourself that 2-hour gap before sleep.
6. Hit all macro targets within the eating window. This is harder in a compressed window so plan ahead.

**Option B: Traditional Timing (3-5 Meals Spread)**

1. Eat 3-5 meals per day, each with 30-50g protein.
2. Carb-focused meal 1-2 hours before training.
3. Protein + carb meal within 2 hours after training.
4. Avoid large meals within 2 hours of sleep.
5. Optional: small casein or cottage cheese serving 1 hour before bed.

Both work. The best one is the one you can actually stick to while hitting your calorie and protein targets. If IF makes you skip protein because you can't eat enough in 8 hours, don't do IF. If spreading meals out makes you graze and overeat, try compressing your window. Adherence beats optimization.

———

**Myths That Need To Die**
- **"Eating breakfast boosts your metabolism."** No it doesn't. When total daily calories are equal, breakfast vs no breakfast makes zero difference to metabolic rate or fat loss. If you're not hungry in the morning, don't force yourself to eat.
- **"The post-workout anabolic window is 30 minutes."** This was massively overblown. The "window" is more like 4-6 hours. Total daily protein matters way more than slamming a shake within 30 minutes of your last set. Don't stress about this.
- **"Eating at night makes you fat."** Eating at night doesn't make you fat. Eating in a surplus makes you fat. The timing is irrelevant for fat loss. The only reason to avoid huge late-night meals is the GH suppression during sleep, not fat gain.
- **"More meals = faster metabolism."** Eating 6 small meals does not burn more calories than eating 3 larger meals with the same total. Thermic effect of food is based on total intake, not frequency.

———

**Mistakes that are costing you right now**
- **Obsessing over timing before getting calories and macros right.** If your total protein is 90g and your calories are off by 500, perfect meal timing changes absolutely nothing. Get the fundamentals from Modules 1 and 2 nailed first.
- **Training fasted and wondering why your workouts suck.** Your muscles run on glycogen. No carbs before training = no fuel = worse performance = fewer gains. Eat something before you train.
- **Using IF as an excuse to eat less protein.** Compressing your eating window makes it harder to hit protein targets. If you're doing 16:8, you need to be intentional about getting 40-50g protein at every meal in that window.
- **Eating a massive meal right before bed.** Big insulin spike before sleep kills your GH pulse. 2-hour gap minimum between your last big meal and lights out.

———

Calories first. Macros second. Food quality third. Timing fourth. If the first three aren't dialed in, don't waste energy on timing. If they are, put carbs around training, spread protein across meals, leave a gap before bed, and pick IF or traditional based on what you can actually stick to. That's it.

**How Supplements Actually Work**

Supplements do one of two things:

1. **Fill a deficiency.** If you're deficient in vitamin D, supplementing D3 raises your levels and everything downstream improves (testosterone, mood, immune function). If you're NOT deficient, extra D3 does nothing. Most supplements work this way. They fill a gap. No gap = no benefit.
2. **Provide an ergogenic effect.** A small number of supplements have a performance benefit regardless of deficiency status. Creatine and caffeine are the two main ones. They work for almost everyone because the mechanism is different from filling a gap.

That's it. Supplements don't replace food, training, or sleep. They optimize an already solid foundation. If your diet is trash and you sleep 5 hours a night, no supplement stack is saving you. Fix the basics first.

**The Full Tier List**

**TIER 1: NON-NEGOTIABLE (Take These)**

- **Zinc picolinate -- 15-25mg daily with food.** Directly involved in testosterone production, skin healing, and hair growth. Don't exceed 40mg long-term without adding copper.
- **Magnesium glycinate -- 300-400mg before bed.** Improves sleep quality, lowers cortisol, supports 300+ enzymatic processes. Glycinate form. Not oxide.
- **Omega-3 (EPA/DHA) -- 2-3g daily.** Controls inflammation, supports skin barrier, brain function. Check the label for actual EPA/DHA content, not total fish oil. Most cheap capsules are underdosed.
- **Creatine monohydrate -- 3-5g daily.** The most researched performance supplement in existence. Increases strength, supports muscle growth, and has cognitive benefits. No loading phase needed. Just 3-5g every day, forever. **One note:** creatine has been shown to slightly increase DHT conversion, which is relevant if you're predisposed to hair loss. If you're on finasteride it's probably fine. If you're already thinning and not treating it, be aware of this.

**The Two Hormonal Goals For Males**

**Goal 1: Maximize testosterone and DHT.** These are the androgens responsible for everything masculine. Muscle mass, fat loss, jawline development, body hair, deep voice, libido, confidence, assertiveness. More T = more masculine phenotype = better looks, better performance, better everything.

**Goal 2: Minimize excess estrogen.** Men need some estrogen (it's important for joint health and bone density). But excess estrogen causes fat gain (especially in the chest and hips), water retention (puffy face, soft look), emotional instability, gynecomastia in bad cases, and it suppresses testosterone through negative feedback. The higher your estrogen relative to testosterone, the worse you look and feel.

Your diet directly influences both of these. Here's how.

**Foods That Support Testosterone**

**Whole eggs.** 3-5 daily. Stop throwing away the yolks. Cholesterol is literally the precursor to testosterone. Your body takes dietary cholesterol and converts it through a chain of reactions into pregnenolone, then DHEA, then testosterone. Egg yolks are also loaded with vitamin D, vitamin K2, B vitamins, and healthy fats. If there's one food that's basically a natural T supplement, it's whole eggs. The old cholesterol-heart disease fear has been substantially debunked by modern research. Eat your eggs.

**Red meat (beef, lamb).** 3-4x per week. High in zinc, saturated fat (which supports testosterone synthesis), iron, B12, and creatine. Beef is one of the most nutrient-dense foods on the planet for male hormonal health. Grass-fed is better but conventional is still good. Don't let perfect be the enemy of good.

**Fatty fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines).** 2-3x per week. Omega-3 reduces inflammation that disrupts hormone signaling. Also a natural source of vitamin D. The omega-3 to omega-6 balance is critical for hormonal health and fatty fish is the best way to improve it.

**Oysters.** The single highest dietary source of zinc on the planet. Zinc is directly required for testosterone production. 6 oysters gives you ~75mg of zinc, which is more than enough. Eat them when you can. If oysters aren't accessible, beef and pumpkin seeds are your next best whole food zinc sources.

**Olive oil.** Use as your primary cooking and dressing oil. Research shows olive oil supports Leydig cell function (the cells in your testes that produce testosterone). It also replaces seed oils, so you're both adding an anti-inflammatory fat and removing a pro-inflammatory one.

**Cruciferous vegetables (broccoli, cauliflower, Brussels sprouts, cabbage).** Eat daily. These contain a compound called DIM (diindolylmethane) that supports healthy estrogen metabolism. DIM helps your body metabolize estrogen through a pathway that produces less potent estrogen metabolites, effectively reducing estrogenic activity. Not a miracle compound but a solid daily habit.

**Foods That Kill Testosterone or Raise Estrogen**

**Alcohol.** This is the number one dietary testosterone killer and it's not close. Alcohol is directly toxic to Leydig cells, which are the cells that produce testosterone. Even moderate consumption (2-3 drinks per week) measurably reduces testosterone and increases estrogen. A night of heavy drinking can suppress T for 24-72 hours. If you're serious about optimizing hormones, alcohol is the first thing to go. Not reduce. Go.

**Seed oils (excess omega-6).** Vegetable oil, canola oil, soybean oil, sunflower oil. The omega-6 overload drives chronic inflammation which disrupts the entire hormonal signaling cascade. We covered this in Module 7 but it's worth repeating: the inflammatory load from seed oils is actively suppressing your testosterone. Replace with olive oil and avocado oil.

**Refined sugar.** Chronic sugar consumption drives insulin resistance, which directly reduces testosterone production. Studies show that after consuming glucose, testosterone drops by up to 25% for 1-2 hours. If you're eating sugar multiple times a day, your T is being suppressed repeatedly throughout the day. Cut it.

**Very low fat diets.** Your body cannot produce testosterone without dietary fat. Fat is the literal building block of sex hormones. Studies show that when dietary fat drops below 20% of total calories, testosterone declines significantly. This is why the fat floor of 0.8g/kg from Module 2 is so important. Never go below it. Even on a cut.

**Soy in excessive quantities.** I need to be nuanced here because this gets blown out of proportion. Soy contains phytoestrogens (isoflavones) that can weakly bind to estrogen receptors. In normal dietary amounts (some tofu or edamame a few times a week), this is not going to meaningfully affect your testosterone. The studies showing hormonal effects involved very high intake (multiple servings daily over extended periods). Don't panic about soy. But don't drink half a gallon of soy milk daily either.

**Licorice root.** This one actually has solid evidence. Licorice root (the actual herb, often found in teas and supplements) measurably reduces testosterone. If you're drinking licorice root tea regularly, stop.

**The Estrogen Connection: Why Staying Lean Matters**

This is the piece that ties the entire Diet section together. Your body fat contains an enzyme called aromatase that converts testosterone into estrogen. The fatter you are, the more aromatase activity you have, and the more testosterone gets converted into estrogen.

This is why leanmaxxing (Module 5) isn't just about facial aesthetics. Staying lean is itself a hormonal optimization strategy. Getting to and maintaining sub-15% BF keeps aromatase activity low and preserves your testosterone-to-estrogen ratio. Your diet, your body composition, and your hormones are all connected.
