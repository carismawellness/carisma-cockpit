## Lesson 1: Stop Exercising. Start Training.

Training · Module 1 of 10

[Image: image.png]

Most of you go to the gym and just "work out." You do some bench, some curls, maybe hop on a machine if it's open, leave when you feel tired. That's not training. That's a hobby. And hobbies don't build physiques.

Training means you understand three things: progressive overload, volume, and frequency. If you don't know what those words mean or you're not actively tracking them, you are wasting your time. I don't care if you've been going to the gym for two years. If you can't tell me what you lifted last Tuesday and how it compared to the week before, you're a gymcel spinning your wheels.

[Image: image.png]

———

**Progressive Overload Is Non-Negotiable**

Your muscles only grow when you force them to do more than they're used to. Same weight, same reps, same sets every week? Your body has zero reason to change. You're just maintaining.

Progressive overload means you are doing **more** over time. More weight on the bar. More reps at the same weight. More sets. Something has to go up or you're not growing. Period.

Here's exactly how to apply it:
- **Option 1: Add weight.** You hit 3x10 at 60kg on bench last week. This week try 62.5kg. Even 1.25kg plates count. Micro plates exist for a reason.
- **Option 2: Add reps.** You hit 3x8 at 60kg. This week hit 3x9 or 3x10. Once you hit the top of your rep range, bump the weight up and start again at the bottom.
- **Option 3: Add sets.** You did 3 sets last week. Do 4 this week. More volume = more stimulus.

The rep range method is the simplest progression scheme that actually works. Pick a rep range like 8-12. Start at 8 reps. Add reps each session until you hit 12. Then add weight and go back to 8. Repeat forever. This is how you get stronger and bigger without overthinking it.

———

**Volume Is The Growth Driver**

Volume = sets x reps x weight. It's the total amount of work you do. And it's the primary thing that determines how much muscle you build.

Here are the numbers you need to know for each muscle group per week:
- **Chest** — 12-16 sets per week (minimum 8 to grow)
- **Back** — 14-20 sets per week (minimum 10)
- **Shoulders** — 12-16 sets per week (minimum 8)
- **Arms** — 10-14 sets per week (minimum 6)
- **Legs** — 14-20 sets per week (minimum 10)
- **Neck** — 4-6 sets per week (minimum 3)

If you're doing 6 sets of chest per week and wondering why your chest won't grow, there's your answer. You're barely at the minimum. Most of you need more volume, not more exercises.

[Image: image.png]

———

**Frequency: Hit Everything Twice**

Training each muscle once a week is a cope from the 90s bodybuilding era. "Chest Monday, back Tuesday" and then you don't hit chest again for 7 days. That's leaving gains on the table.

Hitting each muscle group **twice per week** produces significantly more growth than once per week. The protein synthesis response from a training session peaks at 24-48 hours and then drops back to baseline. If you're waiting a full 7 days, you're spending 5 of those days not growing.

This is why upper/lower splits and push/pull/legs exist. They let you hit everything twice in a week without sessions lasting 2 hours.

**What this looks like for a beginner (0-12 months):**
- Full body, 3 days per week (Mon/Wed/Fri)
- 3-4 sets per exercise, 8-12 reps
- Compound movements only: squat, deadlift, bench, row, overhead press, pull-ups
- Add weight when you hit all reps with clean form
- Rest 2-3 minutes between sets

**What this looks like for an intermediate (1-3 years):**
- Upper/lower split 4 days per week, or PPL 5-6 days
- 10-20 sets per muscle group per week
- Heavy compounds at 5-8 reps, isolation work at 12-20 reps
- Track every single set in an app or notebook

[Image: image.png]

———

**The Part Where Most Of You Fail**
- **Not tracking workouts.** If you aren't writing down your weights and reps, progressive overload is a guess. Download Strong or Hevy or use a notebook. I don't care which one. Just track it.
- **Training each muscle once a week.** Bro splits are for guys on gear who can grow from anything. You're natural. Hit it twice.
- **Only training in one rep range.** Doing 3x10 on everything forever is leaving growth on the table. Mix in heavy sets of 5-8 and pump sets of 15-20.
- **Chasing soreness.** Soreness means nothing. Progression means everything. I'd rather you add 2.5kg to your squat and feel nothing the next day than be crippled from some random drop set that didn't make you stronger.

———

Track your lifts. Hit every muscle twice a week. Push for more weight or more reps every session. That's it. That's the game. Everything else in this course builds on these three things so get them locked in now.


## Lesson 2: The Aesthetic Blueprint.

Training · Module 2 of 10

[Image: image.png]

You're not trying to be a powerlifter. You're not trying to be a bodybuilder. You're trying to build a physique that mogs in a t-shirt and makes your face look better. That means specific proportions, specific muscle priorities, and staying lean enough that people can actually see what you've built.

Most guys in the gym have no target. They just "lift" and hope they end up looking good. That's like driving with no GPS and wondering why you're lost. This module is your GPS. These are the exact numbers, the exact priorities, and the exact split you should be running.

———

**The Numbers That Actually Matter**

Forget bench press PRs. Forget what you weigh. These are the measurements that determine whether your physique looks aesthetic or not:
- **Shoulder-to-waist ratio (SWR)** — this is the single most important proportion. Your bideltoid width divided by your waist. Target: **1.618 minimum, 1.8+ is elite.** This is literally the golden ratio. It's what creates the V-taper that women rate as most attractive in every study ever done on male physiques.
- **Body fat percentage** — **10-14%.** This is where your jaw pops, your cheekbones show, your abs are visible, and your muscle definition actually shows through clothing. Above 15% you're hiding everything you've built under a layer of cope.
- **Neck circumference** — **15-16.5 inches (38-42cm).** A thick neck frames your face and makes your entire upper body look more powerful. Most guys completely ignore this.
- **Waist circumference** — roughly **45% of your height.** If you're 6'0 (183cm), that's about a 32-33 inch waist. Anything wider and your SWR suffers.

[Image: image.png]

Measure yourself right now. Grab a tape measure. Measure your shoulders at the widest point (around both delts), measure your waist at the navel. Divide shoulders by waist. That's your SWR. Write it down. That number is your baseline and everything you do from here is about pushing it higher.

———

**Muscle Priority Ranking For Aesthetics**

Not all muscles are created equal when it comes to how you look. Here's the exact priority order and why:
- **1. Lateral deltoids.** This is the money muscle. Your lateral delts are what create shoulder width. Bigger lateral delts = wider bideltoid measurement = higher SWR. They're the single most impactful muscle for your silhouette. Most gymcels barely train them and wonder why they look narrow.
- **2. Lats.** Your lats create the bottom half of the V-taper. Wide lats make your waist look smaller even if it hasn't changed. The illusion of width on top and narrowness below is the entire game.
- **3. Neck.** A thick neck frames the face, makes your jaw look wider, and signals physical dominance. It's the most overlooked muscle in aesthetic training and the highest ROI addition to any program.
- **4. Upper chest.** Fills out t-shirts, creates that shelf look from the side. Incline work is king here.
- **5. Glutes and legs.** Foundation. Proportional legs prevent the "light bulb" look. Strong glutes improve posture and how you carry yourself.
- **6. Upper traps.** The slope from neck to shoulder. Adds thickness and frames the neck.
- **7. Arms.** Visible in most clothing but they're detail work, not structure. Build the frame first.
- **8. Abs.** Visible abs are a body fat thing, not a training thing. You don't need 200 crunches. You need 12% BF.

———

**The Exact Split You Should Run**

This is a Push/Pull/Legs split with shoulder and back emphasis. Run it 6 days per week. If you can only do 5, drop one leg day.

**PUSH DAY (Chest, Shoulders, Triceps)**
- Incline dumbbell press — 4x8-10, rest 2 min
- Flat barbell bench — 3x6-8, rest 2-3 min
- Cable lateral raise — 4x12-15, rest 60 sec
- Overhead press (dumbbell or barbell) — 3x8-10, rest 2 min
- Tricep pushdown — 3x12-15, rest 60 sec
- Overhead tricep extension — 3x12-15, rest 60 sec

**PULL DAY (Back, Rear Delts, Biceps)**
- Weighted pull-ups (or lat pulldown) — 4x6-10, rest 2-3 min
- Barbell row — 4x8-10, rest 2 min
- Straight arm pulldown — 3x12-15, rest 60 sec
- Face pulls — 4x15-20, rest 60 sec
- Barbell curl — 3x8-12, rest 60 sec
- Hammer curl — 3x10-12, rest 60 sec

**LEG DAY (Quads, Hamstrings, Glutes, Neck)**
- Barbell squat — 4x6-8, rest 2-3 min
- Romanian deadlift — 3x8-10, rest 2 min
- Leg press — 3x10-12, rest 2 min
- Leg curl — 3x12-15, rest 60 sec
- Calf raise — 4x12-15, rest 60 sec
- Neck curl (plate) — 3x15-20, rest 60 sec
- Neck extension (plate) — 3x15-20, rest 60 sec

**Weekly schedule:** Push / Pull / Legs / Push / Pull / Legs / Rest

Notice how lateral raises are on push day AND you could add an extra set on the second push day. Notice how face pulls are on every pull day. Shoulders and back get hammered twice per week with extra volume because they're the priority. Arms get enough indirect work from compounds plus a few direct sets. That's all they need.

[Image: image.png]

———

**The Three Phases**

**Phase 1: Get lean.** If you're above 15% BF, cut first. You can't see your frame under fat. You can't assess what needs work. You don't even know your real SWR yet. Get to 12-13% then assess.

**Phase 2: Build the frame.** Run the split above in a modest surplus of 200-300 calories. Not 500. Not "dirty bulk." Two to three hundred calories above maintenance. You're building muscle, not getting fat again. This phase lasts 6-9 months.

**Phase 3: Maintain and refine.** Once your SWR is 1.6+ and BF is 12-14%, you're in maintenance territory. Small surpluses to keep building weak points, mini cuts when BF creeps above 15%. This is where you live long term.

The biggest mistake I see is guys trying to do Phase 2 while still in Phase 1 territory. You're 20% body fat running a bulk. You can't even see your lateral delts under the fat so how would you know if they're growing? Lean out first. Always.

[Image: image.png]

———

**What Most Guys Get Wrong**
- **Prioritizing arms over shoulders and back.** Arms are detail work. Lateral delts and lats change your entire silhouette. A guy with big arms but narrow shoulders looks worse than a guy with moderate arms and wide delts. Not even close.
- **Ignoring the neck.** Neckmaxxing is the single most slept on thing in gymmaxxing. I'll cover it in detail in Module 5 but start thinking about it now.
- **Bulking to 20%+ BF.** "I'm bulking bro" is the biggest cope. You're just fat. And being fat kills your testosterone via aromatization, hides your muscle definition, and bloats your face. Keep bulks tight, 200-300 calorie surplus max.
- **No target numbers.** If you can't tell me your SWR, your BF%, and your neck circumference, you're training blind.

———

Measure your SWR, your BF%, and your neck right now. Those three numbers tell you exactly where you stand. Run the PPL split above. Prioritize lateral delts, lats, and neck. Stay lean. This is the blueprint. Follow it.


## Lesson 3: Your Compounds Are Trash.

Training · Module 3 of 10

[Image: image.png]

Compound lifts build 80% of your physique. Squat, deadlift, bench, overhead press, pull-ups, rows. These are the movements that actually put mass on your frame. But if your technique is garbage, you're building on a cracked foundation and eventually something breaks.

I see it constantly. Guys loading up weight they have no business touching because their ego can't handle being the dude squatting 60kg. So they quarter squat 120kg and wonder why their legs don't grow and their knees hurt. That's not training. That's a cope with a barbell.

Here's exactly how to do each lift properly and where most people screw up.

———

**Squat**
- Bar position: high bar (on upper traps) for most guys. Low bar only if you're competing in powerlifting.
- Feet shoulder width apart, toes pointed out 15-30 degrees.
- Big breath into your belly, brace your core HARD before you descend. Think about bracing for a punch to the stomach.
- Break at the hips and knees at the same time. Don't shoot your hips back first, that turns it into a good morning.
- **Hit parallel or below.** Your hip crease goes below the top of your knee. Every rep. No exceptions. Quarter squats don't build legs, they build delusion.
- Drive through your whole foot on the way up. Not your toes, not your heels. Whole foot.

**The biggest mistake:** Not hitting depth. If you can't squat to parallel with the weight, it's too heavy. Drop it and do it right.

[Image: image.png]

———

**Deadlift**
- Bar directly over mid-foot. Not against your shins, not 6 inches away. Mid-foot.
- Hip-width stance. Closer than you think.
- Hinge at the hips, grip the bar just outside your legs. Mixed grip or hook grip, straps are fine for working sets.
- Pull the slack out of the bar before you pull. You should hear the bar click against the plates. Arms are ropes, they don't bend.
- Push the floor away with your legs. Don't think about pulling the bar up, think about pushing the ground down.
- Hips and shoulders rise at the same rate. If your hips shoot up first and your back rounds, the weight is too heavy.
- Lock out by squeezing your glutes at the top. Don't hyperextend your back.

**The biggest mistake:** Rounding your lower back. This is how you herniate a disc. If your back rounds, the weight is too heavy or your bracing sucks. Fix it before you add weight.

[Image: image.png]

———

**Bench Press**
- **Retract and depress your scapulae.** This is the cue that fixes 90% of bench problems. Pull your shoulder blades together and push them down toward your hips. This creates a stable platform and protects your shoulders.
- Grip slightly wider than shoulder width. Find a width where your forearms are vertical at the bottom of the rep.
- Unrack, lower the bar to your lower chest/nipple line. Touch your chest. Pause. Press.
- Do not bounce the bar off your chest. That's cheating and it's going to crack a rib eventually.
- Elbows at 45-75 degrees from your torso. Not flared out to 90, that destroys your shoulders over time.
- Drive your feet into the floor the entire time. Leg drive is real.

**The biggest mistake:** Flat back on the bench with shoulders shrugged up. You're pressing off an unstable base and your shoulders will pay for it.

———

**Overhead Press**
- Bar starts at collarbone height, resting on your front delts.
- Grip just outside shoulder width.
- Brace your core hard. No lower back arch. If you're arching, it's too heavy or your core is weak.
- Press the bar straight up. Your head has to move back slightly as the bar passes your face, then forward again once it clears.
- Lock out fully overhead with the bar directly over your mid-foot.

**The biggest mistake:** Turning it into an incline bench by leaning way back. That's not overhead pressing, that's just ego lifting with extra steps.

———

**Pull-Up**
- Start from a dead hang. Full arm extension. Every rep.
- Pull by driving your elbows down and back, not by yanking with your hands.
- Chin clears the bar at the top.
- Full extension at the bottom. No half reps. No kipping.
- If you can't do 5 clean pull-ups, use the assisted machine or do negatives until you can.

**The biggest mistake:** Half reps. If your arms aren't fully extending at the bottom, you're doing half pull-ups and building half a back.

———

**Barbell Row**
- Hip hinge position with your torso at roughly 45 degrees. Some guys go more horizontal, that's fine, just be consistent.
- Pull the bar to your lower chest/upper abdomen. Not to your hips.
- Drive your elbows back, not out to the sides.
- Squeeze your shoulder blades together at the top. Hold for a second.
- Control the weight down. Don't just drop it.

**The biggest mistake:** Using so much weight that it turns into a hip thrust with a slight arm bend. If your torso is swinging up 30 degrees on every rep, the weight is too heavy.

[Image: image.png]

———

**The Non-Negotiable Rules**
- **Film yourself.** Set your phone up from the side and record your working sets. You can't feel what your form looks like. You need to see it. Do this at least once a month for every compound.
- **Start lighter than you think.** Your ego is your biggest enemy in the gym. Nobody cares what's on the bar. They care what your physique looks like in 12 months.
- **Warm up progressively.** Empty bar, then 40% of your working weight, then 60%, then 80%, then your working sets. Never go straight to heavy.
- **Full range of motion on every rep.** Partials build partial muscles. Full ROM produces more hypertrophy. This is settled science.

———

Film your next session. Watch the footage. Be honest about what you see. If your squat isn't hitting depth, your deadlift back is rounding, or your bench shoulders are rolling forward, fix it before you add another plate. Technique is the foundation. Everything else in this course is pointless if this is broken.


## Lesson 4: Build The V-Taper.

Training · Module 4 of 10

[Image: image.png]

Your shoulders and back are the two muscle groups that determine whether you mog in a t-shirt or look like everyone else. Wide delts create bideltoid width. Thick lats create the taper down to the waist. Together they give you the V-taper that makes your SWR go up and your waist look smaller even if it hasn't changed by a single inch.

Most guys in the gym do way too much chest and arms and almost nothing for lateral delts and lats. Then they wonder why they look narrow from the front and flat from the side. This module fixes that. Every exercise, every set, every rep, exactly how to target each muscle head.

———

**Understanding Your Shoulders**

Your shoulder has three heads and each one does something different. You need to know this because most guys only train one of them and neglect the other two.
- **Anterior (front) delt.** Gets hammered by every pressing movement. Bench press, overhead press, incline press. If you press at all, your front delts are getting enough work. You almost never need to directly isolate them. Front raises are a waste of your time.
- **Lateral (side) delt.** This is the muscle that creates shoulder WIDTH. It's what pushes your bideltoid measurement wider. It only gets hit directly by lateral raises and wide grip upright rows. If you're not doing lateral raises, your shoulders are not getting wider. Period.
- **Posterior (rear) delt.** The most neglected head. Rear delts create that 3D look from the side, improve your posture by pulling your shoulders back, and protect your shoulder joint. If your rear delts are weak, your shoulders round forward and you look narrow from the side even if your lateral delts are decent.

[Image: image.png]

———

**The Lateral Delt Bible**

This is the money muscle. Here's every exercise you should be using and exactly how to do them.

**1. Cable lateral raise (single arm, leaning away)**

This is the best lateral delt exercise that exists. The cable keeps tension on the muscle through the entire range of motion, unlike dumbbells which have zero tension at the bottom. Lean slightly away from the cable stack to increase the stretch at the bottom.
- 4 sets of 12-15 reps per arm
- 60 second rest between sets
- Raise to shoulder height, not above. Pinky slightly higher than thumb at the top.
- Control the negative. 2 seconds up, 2 seconds down.
- **Use light weight.** If you're swinging, it's too heavy. I don't care if the guy next to you is using 20kg. If your traps are taking over and your body is rocking, you're not hitting the lateral delt.

**2. Dumbbell lateral raise (strict form)**

Good when cables aren't available. Less effective because tension drops at the bottom of the rep.
- 3 sets of 12-15 reps
- Slight forward lean at the hips, about 10-15 degrees
- Lead with the elbow, not the hand. Think about pushing your elbows out to the sides.
- Stop at shoulder height. Going above that shifts the load to traps.

**3. Wide grip upright row (cable or barbell)**

This is a compound movement that hits both lateral and rear delts when done with a wide grip. Grip should be 1.5x shoulder width.
- 3 sets of 10-12 reps
- Pull to chest height. Elbows go out and up.
- **Wide grip only.** Close grip upright rows impinge your shoulder. If it hurts, your grip is too narrow.

**Weekly lateral delt volume target: 8-10 direct sets minimum.** This is on top of whatever pressing you do. Pressing doesn't meaningfully hit lateral delts. You need isolation.

———

**Rear Delts: Stop Neglecting Them**

**1. Face pulls (cable, rope attachment)**

The single best exercise for rear delts and external rotators. Non-negotiable in every pull session.
- 4 sets of 15-20 reps
- Set the cable at face height
- Pull toward your face, spreading the rope apart at the end. Your hands should end up beside your ears.
- Squeeze for a full second at the end of each rep
- Light weight. This is not a rowing movement. If you're using your whole body, it's too heavy.

**2. Reverse fly (cable or dumbbell)**
- 3 sets of 12-15 reps
- Slight bend in the elbows, locked in position throughout
- Squeeze the rear delts at the top. Don't just swing the weight.

**3. Chest-supported rear delt row**

Lie face down on an incline bench set to about 30-45 degrees. Row dumbbells with elbows flared out wide. This isolates the rear delts and takes your lower back completely out of the equation.
- 3 sets of 12-15 reps
- Elbows flared wide, not tucked. Tucked elbows hits mid back, not rear delts.

**Weekly rear delt volume: 6-8 direct sets.**

———

**Building Lats That Create The Taper**

Your lats are the widest muscle on your body. When they're developed, they create that visible flare when you stand with your arms at your sides. That flare IS the V-taper.

**1. Weighted pull-ups**

The king of back exercises for width. Nothing else comes close. If you can do 10+ bodyweight pull-ups, start adding weight with a dip belt.
- 4 sets of 6-10 reps
- Dead hang at the bottom. Full extension. Every rep.
- Pull by driving your elbows down toward your hips, not behind you.
- Chin over bar at the top.
- Rest 2-3 minutes between sets

**2. Wide grip lat pulldown**

For when pull-ups aren't heavy enough for high rep work or you need more volume after pull-ups.
- 3 sets of 10-12 reps
- Lean back slightly, pull the bar to your upper chest
- Squeeze your lats at the bottom of each rep

**3. Straight arm pulldown**

This isolates the lats with zero bicep involvement. Great finisher.
- 3 sets of 12-15 reps
- Slight bend in elbows, locked position
- Pull the bar or rope down to your thighs in an arc
- Feel the stretch at the top, squeeze the contraction at the bottom

**Weekly lat volume: 8-10 direct sets from vertical pulling plus 6-8 sets of rowing.**

———

**Back Thickness: The Rowing Movements**

Width comes from vertical pulling. Thickness comes from horizontal rowing. You need both.
- **Barbell row** — 4x8-10, heavy. The mass builder. Drive elbows back, squeeze at the top.
- **Seated cable row** — 3x10-12, moderate weight. Focus on the squeeze. Your shoulder blades should touch at the end of each rep.

———

**The Full Weekly Shoulder/Back Protocol**

Spread across two pull days and two push days per week:

**Shoulder volume per week:**
- Cable lateral raise — 4x12-15 (twice per week = 8 sets)
- Wide grip upright row — 3x10-12 (once per week = 3 sets)
- Face pulls — 4x15-20 (twice per week = 8 sets)
- Reverse fly — 3x12-15 (once per week = 3 sets)
- Overhead press — 3x8-10 (twice per week = 6 sets)

**Total: ~28 sets per week for shoulders (lateral + rear + pressing)**

**Back volume per week:**
- Weighted pull-ups — 4x6-10 (twice per week = 8 sets)
- Lat pulldown — 3x10-12 (once per week = 3 sets)
- Straight arm pulldown — 3x12-15 (once per week = 3 sets)
- Barbell row — 4x8-10 (once per week = 4 sets)
- Cable row — 3x10-12 (once per week = 3 sets)

**Total: ~21 sets per week for back**

That's aggressive volume. If you're not used to this much, start at the lower end and build up over 4-6 weeks. Don't just jump in at maximum volume on week one, you'll burn out.

[Image: image.png]

———

**What Most Guys Get Wrong**
- **Only doing overhead press for shoulders.** OHP is a front delt exercise. It does almost nothing for lateral width. You need lateral raises. Lots of them.
- **Swinging on lateral raises.** Using 15kg dumbbells and heaving them up with your whole body. Your traps are doing the work, not your lateral delts. Drop to 7kg and do them strict. Nobody cares how much you lateral raise.
- **Zero rear delt work.** Your shoulders round forward. You look narrow from the side. Your shoulder joint is begging for an injury. Face pulls fix all of this.
- **Pulldowns instead of pull-ups.** Pull-ups produce more lat activation. If you can do pull-ups, do pull-ups. Pulldowns are a supplement, not a replacement.
- **Prioritizing chest over back.** If you bench 4 days a week and row once, you're going to look like a question mark from the side. Back needs MORE volume than chest, not less.

———

Your lateral delts and lats are what create the V-taper. Without them, no amount of chest and arms will make you look aesthetic. Cable lateral raises every session. Pull-ups every pull day. Face pulls always. That's the formula. Your SWR will thank you in 6 months.


## Lesson 5: Neckmaxxing Is Not Optional.

Training · Module 5 of 10

[Image: image.png]

The neck is the most slept on muscle group in the entire gym. Almost nobody trains it directly. And that's exactly why it's one of your biggest opportunities to mog.

A thick neck does three things that directly impact your looks:
- It makes your jaw appear wider and more defined when viewed from the front. The neck creates the base that frames your lower third.
- It completely changes how you look in shirts, collars, and anything with a neckline. A thin neck in a crew neck t-shirt looks fragile. A thick neck in the same shirt looks powerful.
- It signals physical dominance. People unconsciously perceive guys with thicker necks as stronger and more capable. This is a studied halo effect for men.

Target neck circumference: **15-16.5 inches (38-42cm)** for most guys. Measure right now. Wrap a tape around the thickest part of your neck, just below your Adam's apple. If you're under 15 inches, you have serious room to grow and it's going to make a visible difference fast.

[Image: image.png]

———

**The Muscles You're Training**

You need to understand the anatomy so you know what each exercise is actually hitting.
- **Sternocleidomastoid (SCM).** The two large muscles on the front/sides of your neck that you can see and feel when you turn your head. These create the visible mass of the neck from the front. Trained by neck flexion (chin toward chest) and lateral flexion (ear toward shoulder).
- **Deep cervical extensors.** The muscles on the back of your neck. Trained by neck extension (looking up against resistance). These create neck thickness from the side view.
- **Upper traps.** The slope from the base of your neck out to your shoulders. Traps frame the neck from behind and create that powerful look from the front. Trained by shrugs and face pulls.

You need all three. A lot of guys just do shrugs and think they're training their neck. Shrugs hit your traps but they do nothing for the SCM or the cervical extensors. You need direct neck work.

[Image: image.png]

———

**The Exact Neckmaxxing Protocol**

**BEGINNER (first 6-8 weeks):**

Start with no weight or a 5lb/2.5kg plate. I'm dead serious. Your neck has probably never been trained directly and the connective tissue needs time to adapt. Going heavy too fast is how you get hurt.

Do this 2x per week, tacked onto the end of any workout:
- **Plate neck curl (flexion)** — lie face up on a bench with your head hanging off the edge. Place a folded towel on your forehead, plate on top. Curl your chin toward your chest. 3 sets of 20 reps.
- **Plate neck extension** — lie face down on a bench with your head hanging off. Towel on the back of your head, plate on top. Extend your head up. 3 sets of 20 reps.
- **Lateral neck flexion** — lie on your side, head hanging off the bench. Plate on the side of your head with towel. Raise your ear toward your shoulder. 2 sets of 15 each side.

**Progression:** Add 2-3 reps per session until you hit 25 reps per set. Then add 2.5lbs/1kg and drop back to 15 reps. Build back up. Repeat.

**INTERMEDIATE (after 6-8 weeks of beginner protocol):**

Get a neck harness. A $20-30 neck harness is the single best investment you can make for neckmaxxing. It lets you load the neck properly and progress in a way that plates on a bench can't match long term.

Do this 3x per week:
- **Neck harness extension** — standing or seated, head facing down, extend up against resistance. 4 sets of 15-20 reps.
- **Plate neck curl (flexion)** — same as beginner but with more weight. 4 sets of 15-20 reps.
- **Lateral neck flexion** — with plate or band. 3 sets of 15 each side.
- **Barbell shrugs** — heavy. 4 sets of 10-12 reps. Hold the top for 2 seconds.
- **Face pulls** — 4 sets of 15-20 reps. Already in your pull day from Module 4 but listing it here because it contributes to the upper trap shelf.

**Progression scheme:** Same as beginner. Add reps first, weight second. The neck responds to volume, not maximal load. Never go below 12 reps on neck work. The risk reward ratio of heavy low rep neck training is terrible.

———

**Timeline Of Results**
- **Weeks 1-4:** You won't see much. Your neck is adapting. Connective tissue is strengthening. Don't rush it.
- **Weeks 4-8:** You start feeling your neck is "fuller." Shirts might fit slightly differently around the collar. Measurements start moving.
- **Weeks 8-16:** Visible difference. People start noticing. You've probably added 0.5-1 inch to your neck circumference. This is where the face-framing effect kicks in.
- **Months 4-6+:** Significant change. Your neck looks trained. It frames your jaw. Collared shirts look completely different. The halo effect is real.

The neck is one of the fastest responding muscle groups in the body. Most guys see measurable growth within 8-12 weeks of consistent training. You just have to actually do it.

———

**Mistakes That Will Set You Back**
- **Going too heavy too fast.** The number one neckmaxxing mistake. Your neck has tiny muscles and tendons that have never been loaded. Start embarrassingly light and build up over 6-8 weeks. A neck strain sets you back months.
- **Only training flexion OR extension.** You need both. Only training curls and ignoring extension creates imbalance. Only training extension and ignoring curls means your SCM doesn't grow and you don't get that front-view thickness.
- **Only doing shrugs.** Shrugs are for traps. Traps are part of the equation but they are NOT your neck. Direct neck flexion and extension are non-negotiable.
- **Training neck once a week.** Not enough frequency. The neck recovers fast and can be trained 2-3x per week. More frequency = more growth.
- **Quitting after 4 weeks because you don't see results.** 4 weeks is the adaptation phase. Results start at week 8. Commit to 12 weeks minimum before judging.

———

Measure your neck right now. Order a neck harness if you don't have one. Start the beginner protocol this week. In 3 months you'll have a noticeably thicker neck that frames your jaw, fills out your shirts, and gives you a halo that 99% of guys in the gym don't have because they never bothered to train it. This is free real estate.


## Lesson 6: Get Lean Or Stay Invisible.

Training · Module 6 of 10

[Image: image.png]

Leanmaxxing is the single highest ROI thing you can do for your appearance. Not skincare. Not fashion. Not a new haircut. Getting to 10-14% body fat changes your face, your physique, and how people treat you more than anything else in this entire course.

At 20% BF your jawline is buried, your cheekbones are hidden, your muscle is invisible under a layer of fat, and your face is bloated. At 12% your zygos pop, your gonial angle shows, your V-taper is visible, and you look like a different person. Same bone structure. Same muscle. Completely different level of attractiveness.

This module tells you exactly how to calculate your deficit, how to set up a cut, when to use a mini cut vs a full cut, and how to keep every pound of muscle while the fat comes off.

———

**Step 1: Calculate Your TDEE**

TDEE = Total Daily Energy Expenditure. It's how many calories you burn per day. You need to know this number before you do anything else because your deficit is calculated from it.

**The formula:**

Start with your BMR (Basal Metabolic Rate). Use the Mifflin-St Jeor equation:
- **Men:** BMR = (10 x weight in kg) + (6.25 x height in cm) - (5 x age) - 5

Example: 80kg, 180cm, 22 years old. BMR = (10 x 80) + (6.25 x 180) - (5 x 22) - 5 = 800 + 1125 - 110 - 5 = **1810 calories.**

Now multiply by your activity level: (or u can just ask chatgpt to do this for you tbh)
- **Sedentary (desk job, no exercise):** BMR x 1.2
- **Lightly active (1-3 days gym):** BMR x 1.375
- **Moderately active (3-5 days gym):** BMR x 1.55
- **Very active (6-7 days gym + active job):** BMR x 1.725

Same example, training 5 days per week: 1810 x 1.55 = **~2806 calories TDEE.**

That's your maintenance. Eat that and you stay the same weight. Now we subtract from it.

**The real world check:** Track your food for 7 days at your current intake. Weigh yourself daily at the same time (morning, after bathroom, before food). If your weight is stable, your average daily intake IS your TDEE. This is more accurate than any formula. The formula gets you in the ballpark. 7 days of tracking gets you the real number.

[Image: image.png]

———

**Step 2: Set Your Deficit**

Your deficit determines how fast you lose fat. Too aggressive and you'll lose muscle. Too conservative and you'll be dieting for 6 months and hate your life. Here's exactly what to do:

**Standard cut (recommended for most guys):**
- **Deficit: 500 calories below TDEE**
- **Rate of loss: ~1lb / 0.5kg per week**
- **Duration: 8-16 weeks depending on how much fat you need to lose**
- Best for: going from 18-20% down to 12-13%. This is the most sustainable approach with the best muscle retention.

**Aggressive cut (for faster results, more risk):**
- **Deficit: 750-1000 calories below TDEE**
- **Rate of loss: ~1.5-2lbs / 0.7-1kg per week**
- **Duration: 4-8 weeks max before taking a diet break**
- Best for: guys above 20% BF who have more fat to lose. Higher body fat = more aggressive deficit is tolerable because your body has plenty of fat to fuel itself.

**Mini cut (short precision cut):**
- **Deficit: 750-1000 calories below TDEE**
- **Rate of loss: ~1.5-2lbs / 0.7-1kg per week**
- **Duration: 3-6 weeks only**
- Best for: guys already at 14-16% who want to sharpen up to 11-13% without a long dieting phase. You go hard for a few weeks, get lean, then go back to maintenance or a small surplus. In and out.

Using our example above: TDEE of 2806. Standard cut = **~2300 calories per day.** That's your target.

[Image: image.png]

———

**Step 3: Set Your Macros**

Calories determine if you lose weight. Macros determine if you lose fat or muscle. Here's exactly how to split them:
- **Protein: 1g per pound of bodyweight (2.2g per kg).** This is non-negotiable during a cut. High protein is the number one thing that preserves muscle in a deficit. If you're 180lbs, eat 180g protein per day. Every day. No excuses.
- **Fat: 0.3-0.4g per pound of bodyweight.** Don't go below this. You need dietary fat for hormone production. Too little fat on a cut crashes your testosterone. At 180lbs that's 54-72g of fat per day.
- **Carbs: whatever calories are left.** After protein and fat are set, fill the remaining calories with carbs. Carbs fuel your training. Don't fear them.

Example at 2300 calories, 180lbs:
- Protein: 180g = 720 calories
- Fat: 65g = 585 calories
- Carbs: (2300 - 720 - 585) / 4 = ~249g carbs

That's your setup. Track it in MyFitnessPal or Cronometer. Weigh your food with a kitchen scale for the first 2-3 weeks until you can eyeball portions. Most people massively underestimate how much they eat.

———

**Step 4: Training During A Cut**

This is where most guys lose all their gains. They start a cut and either stop lifting or switch to "light weight high reps to tone." Both are catastrophic mistakes.

**The rules:**
- **Keep the same exercises.** Don't change your program just because you're cutting.
- **Keep the same weight on the bar.** Do NOT drop the weight. If you were squatting 100kg for 4x8, keep squatting 100kg. That heavy load is the signal that tells your body "we need this muscle, don't eat it for energy."
- **Reduce volume slightly if recovery suffers.** Drop from 4 sets to 3 sets if you're feeling beat up. But keep the weight the same.
- **Reduce training days if needed.** Going from 6 days to 4-5 days is fine. Recovery is harder in a deficit.
- **Never go below 3 training days per week.** Any less and you're sending the signal that you don't need the muscle.

The number one mistake on a cut: reducing the weight on the bar. The moment you start lifting lighter "because I'm cutting," your body thinks it doesn't need the muscle anymore and it starts breaking it down. Maintain intensity, reduce volume if needed. That's the formula.

———

**Step 5: Know When To Stop**

**Stop cutting when:**
- You've hit 10-12% BF and your jaw is visible, abs are showing, and muscle definition is clear
- Your strength has dropped more than 10% on your main lifts
- You've been in a deficit for more than 16 weeks straight (take a 2-week diet break at maintenance then reassess)
- Your sleep is destroyed, your mood is tanked, and your libido is gone (signs of hormonal crash from dieting too long)

**After the cut:** Reverse diet back to maintenance. Add 100-150 calories per week until you're back at TDEE. Don't just go from 2300 back to 2800 overnight, you'll rebound hard.

**Important:** Your face and lower abs are usually the LAST places to lean out. If your arms are veiny and your upper abs show but your lower belly is still soft and your face still looks bloated, you're not done. Stay the course. Those last 2-3% are where the face gains happen.

[Image: image.png]

———

**What Most Guys Get Wrong**
- **Not tracking calories.** "I'm eating clean" is not a deficit. Clean food has calories too. You can gain weight eating chicken and rice if you eat enough of it. Track your intake.
- **Dropping weights in the gym.** Already covered this but it's so important I'm saying it twice. Heavy weights preserve muscle. Light weights on a cut = muscle loss.
- **Doing excessive cardio instead of eating less.** You can't outrun a bad diet. A 500 calorie deficit from food is easier and more sustainable than burning 500 calories on a treadmill every day. Dial the food in first, use cardio as a supplement.
- **Stopping too early.** Most guys get to 15% and think they're lean. They're not. The face doesn't really transform until 12-13%. Push through.
- **No diet breaks.** If you've been cutting for 12+ weeks and you're stalling, take 2 weeks at maintenance. It resets hunger hormones, restores training performance, and makes the next phase of the cut way more effective.

———

Calculate your TDEE. Subtract 500. Hit 1g protein per pound. Keep lifting heavy. Push to 12%. That's it. This is the most transformative thing you can do for your appearance and it doesn't cost anything except discipline. Stop making excuses and start tracking.


## Lesson 7: Cardio Without Killing Gains.

Training · Module 7 of 10

[Image: image.png]

Cardio is the most misunderstood tool in the gym. Half of you do zero and wonder why your cut stalls. The other half run 45 minutes on the treadmill every day and wonder why they look like a deflated balloon with no muscle. Both are wrong.

Cardio is not the primary fat loss tool. Your diet is. Cardio is a supplement to your diet that accelerates the process, improves your cardiovascular health, and has direct aesthetic benefits that most guys don't even know about. But it has to be the right type and the right amount or it actively fights your muscle gains.

———

**The Cardio Hierarchy For Aesthetics**

Not all cardio is created equal. Here's the ranking from best to worst for someone trying to build and maintain an aesthetic physique:

**1. Walking (the king)**

I'm dead serious. Walking is the single best form of cardio for aesthetics. Zero muscle loss risk. Zero cortisol spike. Zero impact on recovery. Burns significant calories when you do enough of it. And it directly reduces facial bloating by lowering cortisol and improving lymphatic drainage.
- **Target: 8,000-12,000 steps per day. Every single day.**
- This alone can burn 300-600 extra calories per day depending on your weight and pace
- Non-negotiable year round, whether you're cutting or bulking

**2. Incline treadmill walking**

Walking on a steep incline. Set the treadmill to 10-15% incline, 3.0-3.5 mph. This burns roughly the same calories as jogging but with zero impact on your joints and almost no cortisol response.
- 30-45 minutes, 2-3x per week
- Heart rate stays in the 120-140 range (fat burning zone)
- This is the go-to "cardio session" for any aesthetic focused program

**3. LISS (Low Intensity Steady State)**

Cycling, swimming, elliptical at a moderate pace. Heart rate 60-70% of max. Good for cardiovascular health and fat loss. Low recovery cost.
- 30-45 minutes, 2-3x per week
- Good alternative if you hate the treadmill

**4. HIIT (High Intensity Interval Training)**

Short bursts of all-out effort followed by rest. Example: 30 seconds sprint, 60 seconds walk, repeat for 15-20 minutes. Time efficient but spikes cortisol and takes a real toll on recovery.
- **Maximum 1 session per week during a cut**
- Never do HIIT on the same day as heavy leg training
- Skip it entirely during a bulk unless you genuinely enjoy it

**5. Long distance running (the gains killer)**

Anything over 45 minutes at a moderate to high pace. This is the cardio that actually destroys muscle. It spikes cortisol, suppresses testosterone, and burns through glycogen that your muscles need for recovery. Look at any marathon runner's physique. That's what excessive steady-state cardio does.
- **Avoid during any cut or lean bulk**
- If you enjoy running, keep it under 30 minutes and treat it as LISS

[Image: image.png]

———

**The Exact Cardio Protocol**

**During a bulk (keeping fat gain minimal):**
- 8,000-10,000 steps daily (walking)
- 2 incline walks per week (30 min each)
- That's it. Don't overdo it. You're trying to grow.

**During a cut (accelerating fat loss):**
- 10,000-12,000 steps daily (walking)
- 2-3 incline walks or LISS sessions per week (30-45 min)
- 1 HIIT session per week maximum (optional)
- **Always do weights first, cardio after.** If you do cardio before lifting, you deplete glycogen and your training suffers. Weights are the priority. Cardio is the supplement.
- If you can, separate cardio and weights by at least 6 hours. Morning cardio, evening weights. Or vice versa.

**Year round minimums (non-negotiable):**
- 8,000 steps per day
- 2 low intensity cardio sessions per week
- These are for cardiovascular health, not just aesthetics. Your heart needs training too.

———

**Why Cardio Matters For Your Face**

Beyond fat loss, low intensity cardio has direct aesthetic benefits that most guys don't realize:
- **Reduces facial bloating.** Walking and light cardio lower cortisol. Lower cortisol means less water retention in the face. If you look puffy in the morning, daily walks will help more than any debloating supplement.
- **Improves skin quality.** Increased blood flow delivers more oxygen and nutrients to the skin. Guys who do regular cardio have noticeably better skin tone and complexion.
- **Better circulation to hair follicles.** Scalp blood flow is a factor in hair health. Not a cure for hair loss but it supports everything else you're doing for your hair.
- **Improves lymphatic drainage.** Your lymphatic system doesn't have a pump like your heart. It relies on muscle contractions and movement to drain fluid. Walking is the most effective way to keep it moving. Less lymphatic stagnation = less facial puffiness.

———

**What Most Guys Get Wrong**
- **Using cardio to compensate for a bad diet.** "I ate 3500 calories so I'll just run it off." No. You'll burn maybe 400-500 calories in a 45 minute run and still be in a surplus. Fix the diet first. Cardio is a supplement, not a solution.
- **Running for 60+ minutes to get lean.** You're destroying muscle, spiking cortisol, and suppressing testosterone. Switch to incline walking. Same calorie burn, none of the damage.
- **Doing cardio before weights.** Your glycogen is depleted and your training performance tanks. Always lift first.
- **Zero cardio on a bulk.** "I'm bulking I don't need cardio." Your heart doesn't know you're bulking. 2-3 light sessions per week keeps cardiovascular fitness and makes your next cut way easier.
- **HIIT every day.** HIIT is a stress response. Doing it 4-5 times a week in a deficit is a recipe for elevated cortisol, poor recovery, and muscle loss. Once a week maximum.

———

Walk 10,000 steps a day. Do 2-3 incline walks per week. Skip the long distance running. Always lift before cardio. That's the entire cardio plan for an aesthetic physique. Stop overcomplicating it.


## Lesson 8: You Grow When You Sleep.

Training · Module 8 of 10

[Image: image.png]

Training is the stimulus. Recovery is the response. If your recovery sucks, your training is worthless. You're just breaking muscle down and never building it back up. And the single most important recovery variable is sleep.

Growth hormone gets released during deep sleep. Protein synthesis peaks during sleep. Cortisol drops during sleep. Testosterone production happens during sleep. Literally every process that builds muscle and burns fat is maximized when you're unconscious. If you're getting 5-6 hours a night and wondering why you're not growing, there's your answer. You're sabotaging every hour you spend in the gym.

———

**The Sleep Protocol**

**Duration: 7.5-9 hours per night. 8 is the target.**

Not "in bed for 8 hours scrolling your phone." 8 hours of actual sleep. That means you need to be in bed with the lights off for 8.5-9 hours to actually get 8 hours of sleep after accounting for time to fall asleep.

**The rules:**
- **Same bedtime and wake time every day.** Including weekends. Your circadian rhythm is a real thing and it runs on consistency. Sleeping in until noon on Saturday and then trying to sleep at 10pm Sunday doesn't work. Pick a schedule and stick to it within 30 minutes every single day.
- **No screens 45-60 minutes before bed.** Blue light suppresses melatonin production. Your phone is telling your brain it's still daytime. Put it in another room. Read a book. Stare at the ceiling. I don't care. Just stop scrolling.
- **Room temperature: 65-68F / 18-20C.** Your core body temperature needs to drop for you to fall into deep sleep. A hot room prevents this. If you can't control your room temp, sleep with fewer blankets or use a fan.
- **Complete darkness.** Blackout curtains or a sleep mask. Any light in the room reduces sleep quality even if you don't notice it. Cover LEDs on electronics. Make it pitch black.
- **No caffeine after 2pm.** Caffeine has a half-life of 5-6 hours. That 4pm pre-workout is still half-active in your system at 10pm. Drink your coffee in the morning and cut it off by early afternoon.
- **No alcohol.** Alcohol destroys sleep architecture. You might fall asleep faster but you skip deep sleep and REM sleep. A night of drinking wrecks your recovery for 2-3 days. It also directly suppresses protein synthesis. If you're serious about building a physique, alcohol has to go or be minimized to rare occasions.

[Image: image.png]

———

**Nutrition For Recovery**

Sleep is number one but what you eat after training matters too.
- **Post-workout meal within 2 hours of training.** Doesn't need to be immediate. The "anabolic window" of 30 minutes is overblown. But don't wait 5 hours either.
- **30-50g protein in your post-workout meal.** This is non-negotiable. Your muscles were just broken down and they need amino acids to rebuild. A chicken breast and rice. A shake with some fruit. Whatever you want, just hit the protein number.
- **Carbs with your post-workout meal.** Carbs replenish muscle glycogen that was depleted during training. Rice, potatoes, bread, pasta. This isn't the time to be low carb.
- **Hydrate.** Even mild dehydration impairs recovery. You should be drinking at least 3 liters of water per day, more if you're training hard and sweating. If your pee isn't clear or light yellow, you're not drinking enough.

———

**Rest Between Sessions**

Minimum **48 hours** between training the same muscle group. This is why PPL works, each muscle gets hit twice per week with full recovery in between.

On rest days:
- Light walking. Get your steps in.
- Stretching or mobility work if you have tight areas
- Eat at the same level. Rest days are not "low calorie days." Your muscles are rebuilding. Feed them.

———

**Signs You're Under-Recovering**

If you see more than one of these, you need to address your recovery before adding more training:
- **Strength dropping across multiple sessions.** One bad session is normal. Three bad sessions in a row means accumulated fatigue. Take a deload.
- **Sleeping poorly despite being exhausted.** This is your nervous system being fried from too much training stress. Classic overtraining sign.
- **Joint pain that doesn't go away.** Connective tissue recovers slower than muscle. Persistent joint soreness means you're outpacing your tendons.
- **Mood is tanked, you're irritable for no reason.** Hormonal disruption from excess stress. Training is a stressor. Too much without recovery and your hormones suffer.
- **You're getting sick more often.** Overtraining suppresses the immune system.

———

**The Deload**

Every **6-10 weeks**, take a deload week. This is not a week off. It's a strategic reduction in training volume to let accumulated fatigue dissipate.

**How to deload:**
- Same exercises as your normal program
- Same weight on the bar (maintain intensity)
- **Half the sets** (cut volume by 40-50%)
- Focus on technique. Make every rep perfect.
- Sleep even more than usual this week
- Eat at maintenance or slight surplus

After a proper deload week, you should come back feeling stronger and more motivated. If you've never deloaded before, the first time you do it you'll be shocked at how much better your next training block feels.

Most guys think deloading is weak. It's the opposite. Deloading is how you keep progressing long term instead of running yourself into a plateau that lasts months. Every serious lifter deloads. The guys who don't are the ones stuck at the same weights for years wondering why.

———

**Stop Coping With Ice Baths**

Quick note on cold exposure because I know someone's going to ask. Ice baths and cold plunges after training have been shown to **blunt the muscle building response.** The inflammation that happens after training is part of the growth signal. When you dump yourself in cold water, you suppress that signal.

Cold exposure is fine on rest days or in the morning for energy and mood. But don't do it within 4-6 hours of a training session if you're trying to build muscle. You're literally slowing down the process you just spent an hour in the gym creating.

———

Sleep 8 hours in a cold dark room. Eat protein and carbs after training. Rest 48 hours between hitting the same muscle. Deload every 6-10 weeks. Cut the alcohol. These aren't optional extras. They're the difference between a guy who looks the same after 2 years of training and a guy who's actually built something. Protect your recovery like you protect your training.


## Lesson 9: Max Your Hormones Naturally.

Training · Module 9 of 10

[Image: image.png]

Your hormones dictate how fast you build muscle, how easily you burn fat, how good your skin looks, and how you feel every day. Testosterone, growth hormone, and IGF-1 are the anabolic hormones. Cortisol is the catabolic one. The goal is to push the first three up and keep cortisol in check. And the way you train has a massive impact on all of them.

This isn't about supplements or TRT or anything exogenous. This is about using training, body composition, sleep, and daily habits to keep your natural hormone production at the top of your genetic range. Most guys are operating at 50-60% of their natural potential because of how they live. Fixing that is free and the results are dramatic.

———

**How Training Affects Your Hormones**

Every training session produces acute hormonal spikes. The type of training you do determines how big those spikes are and what hormones get elevated.

**What maximizes testosterone response:**
- **Compound movements.** Squat, deadlift, bench, overhead press. These recruit the most muscle mass and produce the largest testosterone spike. A set of heavy squats produces more testosterone than 10 sets of bicep curls. This is why compounds are at the start of every session.
- **Heavy loads.** 70-85% of your 1 rep max. This is the sweet spot. Heavy enough to trigger a strong hormonal response but not so heavy that your CNS gets fried.
- **4-5 training days per week.** Consistent resistance training chronically elevates your baseline testosterone over time. Guys who train regularly have measurably higher resting T than sedentary guys.

**What maximizes growth hormone response:**
- **Moderate to high volume.** More total sets = bigger GH spike. This is one of the reasons why 10-20 sets per session works better than 5 sets and going home.
- **Shorter rest periods on accessory work.** 60-90 seconds between isolation sets creates more metabolic stress which drives GH higher. Heavy compounds still get 2-3 minutes rest, but your lateral raises, curls, and face pulls should have shorter rest.
- **Sleep.** The biggest GH release happens during deep sleep, not during training. Training creates the stimulus. Sleep delivers the hormone. This is why Module 8 matters so much.

**What spikes cortisol (avoid this):**
- **Sessions longer than 75-90 minutes.** Cortisol starts climbing hard after 60 minutes of intense training. Get in, do the work, get out. If your sessions are 2 hours, you're doing too much or resting too long.
- **Excessive high intensity cardio.** HIIT more than 1-2x per week chronically elevates cortisol. Long runs do the same. This is why walking and incline treadmill are king.
- **Training in a massive sleep deficit.** If you slept 4 hours, you're already cortisol dominant. Training on top of that makes it worse. Better to rest and train tomorrow.

[Image: image.png]

———

**Body Fat Is A Hormone Killer**

This is one of the most important connections in this entire course. **Excess body fat actively converts testosterone into estrogen** through a process called aromatization. Fat cells contain an enzyme called aromatase that takes your testosterone and turns it into estradiol (estrogen).

The fatter you are, the more aromatase activity you have, and the lower your effective testosterone is. This is why guys above 18-20% body fat often have measurably lower T levels and feel sluggish, unmotivated, and have low libido.

Getting to 12-15% body fat is one of the single most effective things you can do to optimize your testosterone naturally. No supplement, no "test booster," nothing will do more for your T levels than dropping from 22% to 13% body fat. Leanmaxxing IS hormone optimization.

———

**The Daily Habits That Move The Needle**

Training is one lever. These daily habits are the other levers. Ignore any of them and your hormones suffer.
- **Sleep 8+ hours.** Already covered in Module 8 but I'm repeating it because it's that important. GH is released during deep sleep. Testosterone is produced during sleep. One week of sleeping 5 hours drops testosterone by 10-15%. That's enormous.
- **Get 20-30 minutes of sunlight daily.** Preferably morning sunlight within the first hour of waking. Sunlight exposure regulates your circadian rhythm (better sleep = more GH), increases vitamin D production (which supports testosterone), and improves mood (lower cortisol).
- **Manage chronic stress.** Cortisol and testosterone have an inverse relationship. When cortisol is chronically high, testosterone drops. This isn't about occasional stress, it's about chronic daily stress from work, relationships, finances, social media. If your life is a constant stress storm, your hormones are paying for it.
- **Eat enough dietary fat.** Your body needs cholesterol to produce testosterone. Going below 0.3g of fat per pound of bodyweight measurably reduces testosterone production. Eggs, olive oil, avocados, fatty fish. Don't fear dietary fat.
- **Hit your micronutrients.** Zinc, magnesium, vitamin D, and boron are all directly involved in testosterone production. Most guys are deficient in at least one. A basic ZMA supplement and vitamin D3 supplement covers the bases.
- **Minimize alcohol.** Alcohol directly suppresses testosterone for up to 72 hours after consumption. It also impairs sleep quality and increases estrogen. Every night of drinking sets your hormones back days.

———

**The Exact Protocol For Hormone-Optimized Training**

1. **Lead every session with a heavy compound.** Squat or deadlift first, 4x6-8 at 75-85% of 1RM. This triggers the largest acute testosterone and GH response.
2. **Follow with moderate-load accessory work.** 3-4 exercises, 3x10-15 reps, 60-90 second rest between sets. The combination of metabolic stress and volume drives GH.
3. **Keep sessions under 75 minutes.** After 60-75 minutes, cortisol rises faster than testosterone. Get the work done and leave.
4. **Train 4-5 days per week.** Consistent stimulus improves baseline T over time.
5. **Get to 12-15% body fat and stay there.** This alone does more for your T than any supplement stack.
6. **Sleep 8 hours. Morning sunlight. Minimize alcohol. Eat enough fat.** The non-training variables that complete the picture.

———

**What Doesn't Work**
- **"Testosterone booster" supplements.** Tribulus, ashwagandha, fenugreek, D-aspartic acid. Most of these do nothing measurable. Ashwagandha may reduce cortisol slightly which can indirectly help but it's not raising your T in any meaningful way. Save your money. The basics above do 100x more.
- **Only doing isolation exercises.** Curls and lateral raises don't produce meaningful hormonal responses. You need the big compounds. Squats and deadlifts move the needle. Curls don't.
- **Training through sleep deprivation.** Training on 5 hours of sleep with jacked up cortisol is counterproductive. You're creating the stimulus in an environment where recovery can't happen. Rest first, train when you've slept.

———

Lift heavy compounds. Stay lean. Sleep 8 hours. Get morning sunlight. Eat enough fat. Minimize alcohol. That's natural hormone optimization. No supplement required. No magic protocol. Just doing the basics consistently. Most guys refuse to do this and then wonder why they feel and look like garbage.

**What Actually Works And What It Does**

**Anabolic-Androgenic Steroids (AAS)**

Synthetic testosterone derivatives. They massively increase muscle protein synthesis, slash recovery time, and improve body composition at a rate that natural training simply cannot match.

How dramatic? In one of the most cited studies ever done, guys who injected testosterone and didn't even train gained more muscle in 10 weeks than guys who trained naturally. Let that sink in. The juice group doing nothing outgained the natural group hitting the gym. When you combine testosterone with training, the results are even more extreme.

The most common AAS for aesthetics:
- **Testosterone (base of every cycle).** The foundation. Every other compound is added on top of a testosterone base. Comes in multiple forms (enanthate, cypionate, propionate) with different half-lives.
- **Anavar (Oxandrolone).** Mild oral steroid popular for cutting. Hardens muscle, improves vascularity. Lower risk profile than most but still suppresses natural T.
- **Trenbolone.** Extremely powerful. Insane muscle building and fat loss effects. Also insane side effects: night sweats, insomnia, aggression, cardiovascular damage. Not worth it for aesthetics in my opinion but guys still use it.
- **Nandrolone (Deca).** Good for joint health and mass building but notorious for causing sexual dysfunction and mood issues.

**SARMs (Selective Androgen Receptor Modulators)**

Marketed as "safer alternatives to steroids." Here's the truth: SARMs are NOT side-effect free. They still suppress your natural testosterone. They still stress your liver with oral versions. And because they're unregulated research chemicals sold online, you often don't even know what's actually in the bottle. Third party testing has shown that many SARMs products contain actual steroids, prohormones, or completely different compounds than what's on the label.

The most common SARMs: Ostarine (MK-2866), Ligandrol (LGD-4033), RAD-140. They produce moderate muscle gains but with suppression and unknown long-term risks. If you're going to suppress your T anyway, testosterone itself is actually safer because at least you know exactly what you're taking.

**Growth Hormone (GH) and Peptides**

Exogenous GH improves body composition, skin quality, recovery, and fat loss. But it's expensive ($500-1000+ per month), needs to be injected daily, and carries risks including insulin resistance and acromegaly (growth of hands, feet, and facial bones) at high doses. GH peptides (MK-677, Ipamorelin, CJC-1295) are cheaper alternatives that stimulate your body to produce more GH. Lower risk but also lower effect.

**The Real Risks Nobody Talks About**

Every influencer shows you the gains. Nobody shows you the blood work 5 years later. Here's what actually happens:
- **Testosterone suppression.** Your body detects the exogenous testosterone and shuts down its own production. Your natural T drops to near zero during a cycle. This is why PCT (Post Cycle Therapy) exists. But PCT doesn't always fully restore natural levels. Some guys never fully recover, especially after long or repeated cycles.
- **Cardiovascular damage.** AAS increase LDL cholesterol, decrease HDL cholesterol, increase left ventricular mass, and accelerate atherosclerosis. This damage is cumulative and dose-dependent. Long-term AAS users have measurably thicker arterial walls and reduced cardiac function. This is the risk that actually kills people.
- **Hair loss acceleration.** If you're genetically prone to male pattern baldness, AAS will accelerate it dramatically. Especially DHT derivatives like Masteron and Winstrol. You might build the physique but nuke your hairline in the process.
- **Acne.** Hormonal acne on the back and shoulders is extremely common on cycle. Can be severe.
- **Testicular atrophy.** Your testicles shrink during a cycle because they're not producing testosterone anymore. HCG is used to prevent this but it's an additional injectable.
- **Mood and psychological effects.** Trenbolone in particular is notorious for aggression, paranoia, and insomnia. Even testosterone at high doses can cause mood swings and irritability.
- **Dependency.** Many guys do one cycle, love the results, and never want to go back to natural. They blast and cruise for years. This turns a "temporary experiment" into a lifelong commitment with compounding health risks.

**What Natural Training Can Actually Achieve**

Before anyone considers PEDs, be honest about whether you've actually reached your natural ceiling. Most guys are nowhere close. Here's what's realistically achievable naturally with 3-5 years of consistent, intelligent training:
- SWR of 1.6-1.8
- 10-14% body fat year round
- Physique that puts you in the top 5-10% of men in any room
- Strength levels that most people would consider impressive
- A face that's fully revealed because you're actually lean

The gap between natural and enhanced is real. But the gap between an untrained guy and a natural guy who's been training properly for 3-4 years is MASSIVE. Most of the transformation you want, the one that actually changes how people treat you, happens within your natural potential.

If you haven't been training consistently for at least 3-4 years with dialed nutrition and sleep, you haven't earned the right to even think about PEDs. That's not a moral judgment. It's practical. The risk-reward ratio is worst for guys who haven't exhausted their natural gains first.

**Harm Reduction: If You Choose To Use**

I'm not your dad. If you're an adult and you've made an informed decision, here's how to minimize the damage:
- **Get bloodwork before, during, and after.** Total testosterone, free testosterone, LH, FSH, estradiol, lipid panel (HDL, LDL, triglycerides), liver enzymes (AST, ALT), hematocrit, prolactin. Do NOT run hormonal compounds without monitoring your blood. That's reckless.
- **Start with testosterone only.** Your first cycle should be testosterone and nothing else. 300-500mg per week for 12-16 weeks. Adding multiple compounds on your first cycle multiplies the variables and the risks. You don't know how your body reacts yet.
- **Have PCT ready BEFORE you start.** Nolvadex (tamoxifen) 20mg/day for 4-6 weeks starting 2-3 weeks after your last injection (for enanthate/cypionate). PCT is not optional. Skipping it leaves your natural production suppressed for months.
- **Use an AI (aromatase inhibitor) only if needed.** Anastrozole 0.5mg every other day IF you get estrogen side effects (gynecomastia, excessive water retention). Don't take an AI preemptively, crashed estrogen is worse than slightly elevated estrogen.
- **Keep cycles moderate length.** 12-16 weeks on, then PCT, then at minimum the same amount of time off before considering another cycle. Blasting year-round is how you destroy your health.
- **Do NOT use if you're under 25.** Your brain, your hormonal system, and your growth plates are still developing. The risk of permanent damage is significantly higher.
- **Do NOT use if your training, diet, and sleep aren't already dialed.** PEDs don't fix a bad foundation. They amplify whatever you're already doing. If your training and diet suck, you'll get mediocre results with all the risks.

**The Bottom Line**

PEDs work. That's not debatable. The question is whether the results justify the risks for you personally, given where you're at right now.

If you're under 25, the answer is no. If you haven't trained consistently for 3+ years with good nutrition and sleep, the answer is no. If you can't afford regular bloodwork, the answer is no. If you're doing it because you're impatient rather than because you've genuinely plateaued at your natural ceiling, the answer is no.

Most of you reading this can build the physique you want naturally. The physique that actually transforms how people perceive you, that creates the V-taper and frames your face and fills out your shirts, that's achievable without compounds. It just takes longer. And that's okay.

Do the work naturally first. Get your SWR to 1.6+. Get to 12% body fat. Build the frame.
