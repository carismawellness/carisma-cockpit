## Day 15 — "Why I Won't Inject Your Under-Eyes"

I turn down more tear trough requests than I accept. And I'm going to tell you exactly why.

The tear trough sits 2 to 3 millimetres from the infraorbital vessels.

The infraorbital artery, if occluded by filler, can cause vision loss.

There are 511 documented cases of vision loss from filler in the AAO 2024 data.

The highest-risk areas are around the eye, the nose and the glabella between the brows.

That's not a reason to never treat the tear trough. It's a reason to treat it with extreme caution.

What I look for before I agree:

First, I assess whether the darkness is from thin skin showing vessels or from hollowing. Those are different problems with different solutions.

If it's thin skin, PN therapy will improve texture over 6 weeks without touching a high-risk vascular zone.

If it's genuine hollowing, I check anatomy and fat pad position before I decide whether filler is appropriate.

And if there's true fat herniation, structural bags, filler makes it worse, not better. Every time.

The patients who have had bad tear trough experiences almost always had one of two things: wrong assessment of the cause, or an injector who said yes when they should have said no.

Saying no to under-eye filler is often the right answer.
