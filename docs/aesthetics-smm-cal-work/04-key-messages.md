## Day 1 — "Would You Inject Salmon DNA Into Your Face? I Would."

Think of polynucleotides like a renovation crew for a house that is structurally sound but needs some repair work. They do not bulldoze the house — they go in, fix the load-bearing walls, rewire the electrics, and leave the building in genuinely better condition. That is exactly what polynucleotide (PN or PDRN) therapy does to skin tissue.

Polynucleotides are purified chains of DNA derived from salmon sperm — yes, really. The DNA is processed to a pharmaceutical grade so there is no biological material remaining, only the nucleotide building blocks your skin cells recognise and use. When injected into the dermis, these chains stimulate fibroblast activity (fibroblasts are the cells that make collagen and elastin), promote local tissue hydration, and reduce inflammation. The result, seen clearly at four to six weeks, is skin that looks more rested, more luminous, and less hollowed — not because something was added on top, but because the tissue itself improved.

| Feature | Polynucleotides (PN/PDRN) | Classic HA Filler |
|---|---|---|
| Mechanism | Stimulates your own cells to repair | Adds volume directly |
| Best for | Tissue quality, dark circles, fine lines | Structural volume deficit |
| Visible result | 4–6 weeks (gradual) | Immediate |
| Reversible? | Metabolises naturally over 6–12 months | Yes — dissolves with hyaluronidase |
| Downtime | Minimal — small bumps settle in hours | Minimal |

Where does it work best? The under-eye area is the signature application. The periorbital skin is the thinnest on the face and the hardest to treat with volumising filler. Polynucleotides improve the tissue quality itself — the transparency, the texture, the mild hollowing — rather than trying to push volume into an area that often reacts poorly to it.

A note on expectations: this is not a one-treatment wonder. A course of two to three sessions spaced four weeks apart is standard. The improvements are real and lasting, but they are gradual — and that is entirely the point. We are rebuilding, not filling.

**Bottom line:** If your concern is skin quality, fine lines, or a hollowed under-eye that does not need volume, polynucleotides are one of the most clinically logical treatments available right now.

## Day 2 — "Anti-Wrinkle Treatment Does NOT Freeze Your Face — Let Me Prove It"

The "frozen face" association with anti-wrinkle treatment is, almost without exception, a dosing problem — not a product problem. Understanding this distinction changes everything.

Here is the biology. Neuromodulator injections work by blocking the signal between a nerve and a muscle. When a nerve tells a muscle to contract, it releases a chemical messenger called acetylcholine. The treatment temporarily interrupts that signal at the neuromuscular junction. At low, precise doses in the right anatomical location, you get softening of dynamic movement — the kind that over years etches lines into the skin. At high doses or in the wrong plane, you lose expression. One is medicine. The other is a technique error.

Think of it like a dimmer switch versus a light switch. A light switch is either fully on or fully off. A dimmer gives you everything in between. Conservative, anatomy-aware dosing is the dimmer. The frozen look happens when someone flips the switch all the way off — too many units, placed too broadly.

| Approach | Typical Units in Forehead | Expression Retained? | Lines Softened? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Low-dose anti-wrinkle treatment (preventative) | 4–8 units | Full expression | Dynamically, yes |
| Standard dosing | 10–15 units | Mostly yes | Yes |
| Overdose (frozen look) | 20+ units | No | Yes, but unnaturally |

**Myth to address head-on:** "Anti-wrinkle treatment ruins your face." What you have likely seen in celebrity photos or online is the result of repeated overdosing, often combined with filler — not the product itself. The product, used correctly, is one of the most studied injectables in medicine with a 30-year clinical safety record.

What I check before I inject: your full range of expression, your brow position, the natural asymmetries of your face. Good technique means you should still be able to raise your eyebrows, show surprise, squint in the sun. You simply do it with less effort — and with fewer lines forming in the process.

**Bottom line:** The frozen face is a choice, not a side effect. At Carisma, we do not make that choice.

## Day 3 — "My Results Are So Natural, No One Believes I've Had Anything Done"

The best injectable result is the one nobody can name. Not because the treatment was secretive — but because it worked with your face rather than against it.

The "undetectable era" in aesthetics is not a trend invented on social media. It reflects a genuine shift in clinical philosophy: the goal of good injectables is to make you look like a well-rested, healthy version of yourself, not like a different person. When that is achieved — when someone says "you look great, did you change your hair?" — that is the highest compliment in aesthetic medicine.

What does this look like in practice? It means treating the *cause* of tiredness, not just the symptom. A patient who looks tired often has three overlapping things happening: slight brow descent (which a few precisely placed units can lift), a subtle hollow under the eye (which a skin booster can improve the tissue quality of), and early movement lines in the forehead (which low-dose anti-wrinkle treatment softens before they become static creases). Treat all three conservatively, and the effect is transformative — yet unattributable to any single treatment.

**The combination that creates this:**

| Treatment | What it addresses | Result visible at |
|---|---|---|
| Low-dose anti-wrinkle treatment | Dynamic expression lines, brow position | Day 7–14 |
| Skin Booster (Profhilo / PN) | Skin quality, luminosity, fine hydration lines | Week 4 |
| Low-dose filler (if needed) | Specific hollow or proportion | Immediate–Day 14 |

**Quick rules for the natural look:**
1. Start with less. You can always add; you cannot easily subtract.
2. Treat the cause, not just the symptom. If your face looks tired, find out why.
3. Review at two weeks. Small adjustments make a large difference to symmetry.
4. Space treatments out. Combining everything on one day often leads to over-correction.

*Subtle by Design* — that is the only aesthetic philosophy worth having.

## Day 4 — "Lip Flip or Lip Filler — I'll Tell You Which One You Actually Need"

These are two entirely different treatments that happen to both involve the lips. Choosing between them depends on what your lips actually need — and the two answers look nothing alike.

A lip flip uses a tiny amount of anti-wrinkle treatment (usually two to four units) injected into the orbicularis oris muscle — the circular muscle that rings the mouth. When that muscle relaxes slightly at the top edge, the upper lip curls outward, giving the impression of more lip show without adding any volume. The result is subtle, soft, and particularly effective for patients whose upper lip rolls inward when they smile, hiding what is already there. It suits patients who want the illusion of a fuller lip, not an actually larger one.

Lip filler uses hyaluronic acid (a substance naturally present in your body) to add physical volume, redefine the border, reshape the cupid's bow, or hydrate the body of the lip. It creates real structural change.

| | Lip Flip | Lip Filler |
|---|---|---|
| Active ingredient | Neuromodulator | Hyaluronic acid |
| What it does | Relaxes the upper lip roll | Adds volume and/or definition |
| Best for | Thin upper lip that hides when smiling | Actual volume deficit, asymmetry, border definition |
| Duration | 6–8 weeks | 6–12 months |
| Reversible? | Fades naturally | Yes, dissolves with hyaluronidase |
| Cost | Lower | Higher |

**Who should choose the flip, not the filler:**
1. You have good natural lip shape but the upper lip disappears when you smile
2. You want to test the waters before committing to filler
3. You are concerned about looking "done" — the flip gives zero telltale swelling

**Who needs filler, not the flip:**
1. Your lips have genuinely reduced in volume over the years
2. There is asymmetry between upper and lower, or left and right
3. You want the cupid's bow more defined

> *The right answer for your lips lives in your anatomy — not in someone else's before-and-after. Bring your questions to a consultation and we will find it together.*

## Day 5 — "3 Signs Your Filler Has Migrated (And 3 Signs It Hasn't)"

Filler migration is one of the most searched and most misunderstood topics in aesthetics. The hashtag has millions of views, but most of what circulates is either genuine alarm about real misplaced product or a misattribution of normal filler behaviour. Let us separate the two carefully.

**What migration actually means:** Hyaluronic acid filler is a gel. It stays where it is placed — it does not travel through the bloodstream or migrate to unrelated parts of the face. What can happen, with repeated over-injection in the same area over years, is that the filler accumulates beyond the natural tissue boundaries and creates visible fullness in the wrong anatomical compartment. This is not the filler moving by itself; it is excess product placed progressively outside the intended zone.

| Genuine migration sign | Not migration — what it actually is |
|---|---|
| A visible ridge above the vermillion border that persists beyond 3 weeks | Swelling in the first 48 hours |
| Cheek filler creating heaviness that drags the midface down | Normal HA hydration — it draws water and can look larger at day 3 than day 14 |
| Under-eye filler appearing as a bluish ridge (Tyndall effect) | Slight irregularity felt under the skin immediately post-treatment — normal |

**3 signs that genuinely suggest migration:**
1. A visible ridge or bump outside the anatomical lip border that persists beyond three weeks
2. Cheek filler creating a roll near the nasolabial fold after repeated over-treatment cycles
3. Under-eye filler appearing as a persistent bluish discolouration beneath the orbital rim

**3 signs that are normal and not migration:**
1. Asymmetry visible in the first two weeks — oedema settles unevenly
2. Lips feeling firmer or looking slightly larger at day three — HA absorbs water initially
3. Slight irregularity felt under the skin immediately post-treatment

**Quick rules:**
1. Judge at four weeks, not four days
2. If you are concerned at two weeks, book a review — not a social media scroll
3. The best prevention is conservative dosing and a practitioner with sound anatomical knowledge

**Bottom line:** Migration is real, but rare when treatment is done correctly and conservatively. Most "migration" content online is showing normal healing — not a clinical emergency.

## Day 7 — "The Ozempic Face Fix — What the Scale Doesn't Tell You"

The scale tells you that you have lost weight. It does not tell you that the fat lost from your face may have been the very thing giving it structure.

GLP-1 medications (semaglutide, tirzepatide, and similar) have driven significant weight loss in a large number of patients over the past two to three years. For many people, this is genuinely positive for their overall health. The unintended consequence — now widely recognised in aesthetic medicine — is the facial volume loss that often accompanies it. Hollow temples, flattened cheeks, accentuated nasolabial folds, deflated under-eyes, early jowling. A slim body, but a face that looks older than it did before.

This happens because facial fat compartments are not passive tissue — they are active support structures. When they reduce rapidly, the overlying skin has less scaffolding, and it descends or appears hollow. This is not the same as the volume loss of natural ageing, which is gradual. Rapid weight-loss-induced volume loss can be more pronounced and abrupt.

| Zone affected | What it looks like | Best approach |
|---|---|---|
| Temples | Hollowing above the cheekbone | Sculptra or conservative HA filler |
| Cheeks | Flat midface, deepened nasolabial folds | Conservative HA filler |
| Under-eyes | Hollowed, dark circles worsen | Polynucleotides first; filler selectively |
| Jawline | Jowling accelerates | Collagen stimulators, structural HA filler |
| Lips | Thinner, more deflated appearance | Low-dose HA filler |

**The treatment approach:** Sculptra (poly-L-lactic acid) is often the most appropriate starting point for Ozempic face because it works by stimulating your own collagen — it restores structure gradually rather than adding volume on top of already diminished tissue. Combined with conservative HA filler in specific areas, the result is a face that looks restored, not refilled.

**Important:** The goal here is not to replace all the volume that was lost. We are restoring the structural scaffolding that keeps the face looking its age — not trying to reverse the weight loss.

**Bottom line:** Treating Ozempic face is not vanity. It is making sure your face reflects the health of your body, not the speed of its change.

## Day 8 — "4 Weeks of Profhilo — Real Photos, No Filter"

Think of Profhilo like a deeply intelligent moisturiser that works from the inside. Not a filler — it does not sit in one place and add volume. Not a traditional skin booster in the micro-injection sense. Profhilo is a hybrid hyaluronic acid product with an unusually high concentration that, once injected, spreads diffusely through the skin layers, hydrating, supporting, and bio-remodelling the tissue it moves through.

The science: Profhilo contains both high-molecular-weight and low-molecular-weight HA in a thermally bonded complex — no chemical cross-linking, which means the body processes it more naturally. The high-MW HA provides slow-release hydration; the low-MW HA is picked up by skin receptors and triggers a biostimulation response, telling your skin's own cells to increase collagen and elastin production.

What does this actually look like at four weeks?

At week zero, the skin is at its baseline — any laxity, dryness, or dullness is present. By day two to three, the small injection bumps (BAP technique — five points per side) have fully settled. Some patients notice a softening by week two. By week four, this is when the result becomes visible. The skin looks hydrated in a deep way — not glassy or shiny, but plump in the way that well-rested skin looks. Fine surface lines are softer. The skin feels more supple to the touch.

| Measure | Week 0 | Week 4 (post-2 sessions) |
|---|---|---|
| Skin hydration | Baseline | Visibly improved |
| Fine lines | Present | Softened |
| Laxity | Baseline | Early improvement |
| Overall tone | Dull / uneven | Luminous |

A two-session course is standard, spaced four weeks apart, with a maintenance session every six months. Results continue to improve for up to three months as collagen stimulation compounds.

**What Profhilo is not:** It does not add facial volume. It does not define the jawline. It will not correct deep static wrinkles or significant volume loss. For those, different tools exist — and we often layer them.

**Bottom line:** Profhilo is the foundation of a skin-quality programme. If your skin needs hydration, luminosity, and early laxity improvement, it is the most intelligent place to start.

## Day 9 — "I've Been Prescribing Masseter Treatment for Headaches, Not Just Jaw Slimming"

The masseter is one of the strongest muscles in the human body — proportional to its size, it exerts more force than almost any other muscle you have. Its job is chewing. But in many people, it is also doing significant overtime: clenching, grinding, bracing during sleep, tightening under stress. Over time, this overdevelopment (called hypertrophy) does two things. It changes the visible shape of the jaw — creating a squared, heavy lower face. And it causes real, measurable symptoms: tension headaches, jaw ache, tooth wear, morning facial tightness.

A muscle-relaxing injection in the masseter works the same way it does in any other overactive muscle: by reducing the intensity of contractions. The muscle does not disappear — it simply works at a more appropriate level of effort.

| Indication | What you notice | Timeline |
|---|---|---|
| Jaw slimming (aesthetic) | Softer, less squared lower face | 6–8 weeks for full reduction |
| Bruxism (teeth grinding) | Less jaw ache on waking, reduced tooth wear | 2–4 weeks |
| TMJ-related headaches | Reduced frequency and intensity of tension headaches | 2–4 weeks |
| Facial pain from clenching | Morning facial soreness improves | 2–4 weeks |

**Who is this for?** Anyone who wakes up with jaw ache, has been told by their dentist that they are grinding, gets tension headaches across the temples and sides of the head, or notices their jaw feels sore after stressful periods. These are medical indications — the aesthetic jaw-slimming result is secondary, though often welcome.

**Myth-bust:** Masseter treatment does not affect your ability to chew. The doses used are targeted to reduce hypertonicity — excess effort — not to impair normal function. Patients routinely describe eating comfortably within 24 to 48 hours.

**Quick rules:**
1. Treatment is typically bilateral (both sides), though one side may be more dominant
2. Results last four to six months — often longer in the masseter than in facial muscles
3. With repeated treatment, some long-term reduction in muscle bulk occurs, improving results over time

**Bottom line:** Masseter treatment is one of the few aesthetic treatments that also has a genuine quality-of-life medical benefit. If your jaw is working too hard, it is worth exploring — whatever the motivation for asking.

## Day 10 — "Why I Turn Down Tear Trough Filler Requests More Than I Accept Them"

The under-eye area is the most technically demanding site for filler placement in the face. The skin is the thinnest. The tissue beneath it is the most unforgiving. And the complications — when they occur — are among the most difficult to resolve. This is why a good practitioner always assesses before agreeing, and why "no" is sometimes the most clinically correct answer.

The tear trough is the groove that forms between the lower eyelid and the cheek. As we age, volume is lost from the orbital rim and the superficial fat compartments beneath the eye — this creates the hollowed, shadowed look most patients are concerned about. Hyaluronic acid filler *can* address this. But the variables that determine whether it will look beautiful or create problems are numerous.

| Patient type | Appropriate for tear trough HA filler? | Better alternative |
|---|---|---|
| Young (25–35), good skin quality, structural hollow | Sometimes yes — thin filler, careful placement | Polynucleotides first, to build tissue quality |
| Significant puffiness or festooning (fluid swelling) | No — filler worsens this | Conservative approach, address lifestyle factors |
| Very thin skin, prominent blue veins | Caution — Tyndall effect risk high | Polynucleotides; deeper filler only if appropriate |
| Malar oedema (tendency to retain fluid) | No — filler can worsen this significantly | Polynucleotides |
| Volume loss from cheek descent, not a true hollow | No — midface is the right place to treat | Cheek filler first |

**What is the Tyndall effect?** When HA filler is placed too superficially under the thin skin of the under-eye, it causes a bluish discolouration due to the way light scatters through the gel. It is not dangerous but it is difficult to dissolve without multiple sessions and can look worse than the original concern.

**Why do I turn patients away?** Because treating the wrong patient in the tear trough creates a problem I then have to spend several appointments correcting. Saying no early protects the patient and is the honest clinical choice.

**What I recommend instead in most cases:** A course of polynucleotides, which improves the tissue quality of the periorbital skin itself. For many patients, this gives a better and more durable result than filler with far less risk.

**Bottom line:** Your under-eye deserves a practitioner who thinks more than they inject. The best outcome for this area often begins with restraint.

## Day 11 — "Getting Married in Malta This Summer? Start Your Aesthetic Timeline Here"

Aesthetic treatments have lead times. Some results appear overnight; others build over weeks. If you are getting married — or attending any major event where you want to look your best in photographs — the single most important thing is starting early enough that every treatment has fully settled before the day arrives.

The basic principle: no new treatment on the week of the event. What happens when a bride books her filler two days before the wedding? Swelling. Bruising. Stress. None of those are on the mood board.

Here is how I structure a bridal timeline for a patient with a Malta wedding:

| Timeframe before wedding | Treatment | Why this timing |
|---|---|---|
| 12 weeks before | Profhilo (Session 1) | Start the bioremodelling process; skin quality builds over 3 months |
| 8 weeks before | Profhilo (Session 2) + anti-wrinkle assessment | Second Profhilo at 4-week interval; anti-wrinkle treatment at 8 weeks — full result at 2 weeks, settled well before the event |
| 6 weeks before | Filler (if planned) | Full settling occurs at 2–4 weeks; time for minor correction if needed |
| 4 weeks before | Anti-wrinkle top-up if clinically indicated | Full result visible by week 2, well settled by week 4 |
| 2 weeks before | Review appointment only | Assess symmetry; small corrections if clinically indicated |
| 1 week before | Nothing new | Skin should be calm, all results fully settled |

**Clinical note on the anti-wrinkle timing:** The protocol above uses anti-wrinkle treatment at 8 weeks (first assessment and treatment) with the option of a top-up at 4 weeks if indicated. This two-step window means you have a full result visible and time to correct any asymmetry before the event. Some practitioners work from a single session at 4 weeks (peak result at week 2); either is defensible — what is not defensible is treating the week before and hoping for the best.

**Malta-specific note:** If your wedding is outdoors (as many Malta weddings are — particularly during festa season from June to September), discuss SPF protocol with your practitioner. UV at index 10–11 degrades HA faster than in cooler climates, and post-treatment skin is more UV-sensitive for the first two weeks.

**Quick rules:**
1. Book a consultation at least 12 weeks before your event
2. Tell your practitioner the exact date — they will build the timeline backwards from there
3. Do not introduce a brand-new treatment in the final six weeks
4. Prioritise skin quality work first, volume work second

**Bottom line:** The best bridal aesthetics are invisible in the photos. The bride looks like herself — luminous, rested, and at her best. That result takes planning, not last-minute rushing.

## Day 12 — "This Is 6 Months of Lip Filler Done Right — 0.5ml at a Time"

The duck-lip era has not ended because hyaluronic acid filler got worse. It has ended because practitioners and patients both got smarter about how to use it.

Natural lip enhancement is entirely achievable — but it requires a completely different philosophy than the approach that created the overdone results that have made so many women hesitant. That philosophy is: *less, over time, rather than more, all at once.*

Here is why the gradual approach works so much better. Lips have an anatomical structure — the cupid's bow, the philtrum columns, the natural ratio between upper and lower. In the vast majority of patients, a good aesthetic outcome means respecting a 1:1.6 ratio of upper to lower lip. Push beyond that, or place filler outside the vermillion border, and you get distortion rather than enhancement.

**What a 6-month gradual programme looks like:**

| Session | Amount | Goal |
|---|---|---|
| Month 0 | 0.5ml | Very subtle — hydration, mild volume, initial border definition |
| Month 2 (optional) | 0.5ml additional | Build on the initial result; assess how the lips held the first treatment |
| Month 4 (if wanted) | 0.5ml additional | Reach desired final volume gradually; adjust technique based on response |
| Month 6 (review) | Top-up if needed | Maintain — some lips need this at 6 months, others at 12 |

**Why this beats "1ml all at once":**
1. Less risk of vascular compression — smaller amounts in one session mean less pressure on blood vessels
2. The result is easier to assess and correct if any asymmetry appears
3. The lips adjust naturally — less chance of distortion or tightness
4. If you decide 0.5ml is enough (many patients do), you stop there

**Myth-bust:** Lip filler does not permanently stretch your lips or leave them thinner when it dissolves. HA filler metabolises completely. Your lips return to their natural shape. There is no rebound.

0.5ml. That's it. That is what *Subtle by Design* looks like in a syringe.

## Day 13 — "What Actually Happens to Your Face When You Stop Getting Anti-Wrinkle Treatment?"

This is one of the most common questions I hear — and one of the most misunderstood areas in the whole of aesthetic medicine. So let me be completely honest about the science.

When you stop anti-wrinkle treatment, the nerve signals to your facial muscles gradually return. Your body has been quietly growing new nerve terminal connections throughout the months since your last appointment — a process called axonal sprouting. Over approximately three to four months after your last treatment, the muscle returns to its normal baseline activity.

What does this mean for your face? The dynamic lines — the ones that appear with expression — will return to wherever they were before you started. If you have been having treatment for several years, they may return more slowly, and some patients find that with long-term use, the habituated reduction in muscle movement means those lines are slightly less deep than they would have been without any treatment at all. But they do return.

| Scenario | What happens when you stop |
|---|---|
| Stopped after 1 year of treatment | Lines return to approximately where they were before |
| Stopped after 5+ years (preventative treatment) | Lines may return slightly softer than peers who never treated, due to years of reduced movement |
| Stopped mid-programme | No rebound — muscles do not become overactive to compensate |

**The myth this addresses:** "Once you start, you cannot stop — your face will look worse." This is categorically not true. There is no rebound effect. Your muscles do not weaken from treatment, nor do they overcompensate when the treatment wears off. The face simply returns to its natural baseline.

**Why do people believe this myth?** Because when anti-wrinkle treatment wears off after months of looking smoother, seeing those lines again feels dramatic — even if they are exactly as they were before. The contrast between treated and post-treated creates the illusion that lines have worsened.

**Quick rules:**
1. You can start and stop anti-wrinkle treatment without any long-term penalty
2. If you pause for a year, you pick up where you left off — not from a worse baseline
3. Preventative treatment in your late 20s and 30s may slow groove formation, but it is your choice entirely

**Bottom line:** There is no addiction. There is no trap. Treating is a choice, and so is stopping — and the science supports both equally.

## Day 14 — "Profhilo vs. Polynucleotides — Honest Verdict After Using Both"

Patients often ask which is better. The honest answer is that they are not competing — they address different problems. The real question is: what does your skin actually need?

**Profhilo** is a high-concentration hyaluronic acid product that spreads diffusely through the skin when injected, improving hydration from within and biostimulating collagen and elastin production. It is particularly effective for skin laxity, overall luminosity, and the general "my skin looks tired" presentation. It works across the face, neck, and décolletage and is injected at five points per side using the BAP protocol.

**Polynucleotides (PN/PDRN)** are purified DNA chains derived from salmon, injected intradermally. They stimulate fibroblast activity and tissue repair at a cellular level, reduce inflammation in the tissue, and improve skin quality — particularly in the periorbital area, where their anti-inflammatory and regenerative properties make them preferable to HA filler for many patients.

| Feature | Profhilo | Polynucleotides (PN/PDRN) |
|---|---|---|
| Active ingredient | Hybrid HA (high + low molecular weight) | Purified DNA chains |
| Mechanism | Hydration + biostimulation | Cellular repair + fibroblast activation |
| Best for | Global skin laxity, dullness, loss of firmness | Periorbital skin, dark circles, fine line repair |
| Number of sessions | 2 (1 month apart) + maintenance | 2–3 (4 weeks apart) |
| Result visible at | 4–6 weeks post-course | 4–6 weeks post-course |
| Spreads or stays localised? | Spreads diffusely | More localised |
| Can they be combined? | Yes — often used together | Yes |

**When I recommend Profhilo:** The patient's main concern is overall skin quality, laxity, and hydration across the face. They want a global reset.

**When I recommend PN:** The patient's primary concern is the under-eye area — dark circles, thin skin quality, a hollowed appearance that does not need volume. Or they want a more targeted intervention in a specific zone.

**When I recommend both:** Frequently. A course of PN for the periorbital area combined with Profhilo across the face and neck gives a comprehensive skin quality outcome.

> *Which one do you actually need? That is a question for your consultation — where we look at your skin, not your wishlist.*

## Day 16 — "3 Questions I'll Ask You Before I Inject Lip Filler"

These three questions are not gatekeeping. They are the clinical work that separates a result you will love from one you will regret. Every practitioner who treats lips well has versions of these — and every patient who has had a disappointing result wishes they had been asked them.

**Question 1: What bothers you about your lips right now — and can you show me a photo of your lips from five years ago?**

The reason: many patients come in describing "flat lips" or "no definition" when what has actually changed is the perioral area, not the lips themselves. Volume loss in the cheeks descends over time and creates the shadow around the mouth that reads as flat lips in selfies. Treating the lips in that scenario adds volume in the wrong place. A baseline photo tells us whether the lips have genuinely changed or whether something adjacent has.

**Question 2: Have you had lip filler before — and if so, where, how much, and how long ago?**

The reason: hyaluronic acid filler metabolises over time but not always completely. Residual product can change the architecture of the lip in ways that are not always visible on the surface. Knowing the treatment history lets me assess the tissue accurately and avoid stacking volume on top of something that is still present.

**Question 3: What result do you actually want — and have you ever seen it on someone else's face, not just in a social media filter?**

The reason: filters are not anatomy. The lip shape that looks proportionate on a digitally altered image is often not the lip shape that works on a real face. Patients who can point to a real person — not a celebrity, ideally someone with a similar facial structure — tend to have much more realistic expectations and much better results. We look at references together and I tell you what is achievable and what is not.

**Three quick rules before any lip appointment:**
1. Bring photos — of your own lips at their best, and of any reference results you like
2. Do not book on an empty stomach — blood sugar affects bruising tendency
3. First session: 0.5ml maximum. See how your tissue responds before adding more.

**Bottom line:** The questions are not obstacles. They are the consultation. A practitioner who skips them is skipping the part that makes the result good.

## Day 18 — "Malta Summer. No Sweat Patches. This Is Why."

Malta averages 300 days of sunshine a year. Temperatures regularly exceed 35°C from June through September. And for anyone who experiences excessive sweating — hyperhidrosis — that climate is not a backdrop. It is a clinical variable that makes an already limiting condition significantly worse.

Hyperhidrosis — the clinical term for excessive sweating beyond what thermoregulation requires — is more common than most people realise. Primary focal hyperhidrosis (excessive sweating in specific areas like the underarms, hands, or feet, disproportionate to temperature or exercise) affects an estimated 1–3% of adults globally, with axillary forms the most common presentation (Lakraj et al., *Toxins*, 2013). It is a recognised medical condition with a well-evidenced treatment: intradermal anti-wrinkle injections.

Here is how it works. The same mechanism that relaxes a facial muscle can also be applied to the eccrine sweat glands in the skin. These glands are activated by acetylcholine — the same neurotransmitter involved in muscle contraction. By injecting a small amount of neuromodulator across the axillary (underarm) skin in a grid pattern, you block the acetylcholine signal to those glands. The result: sweating in that area is dramatically reduced for approximately six to eight months.

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| Treatment area | Underarms most common; hands and feet also treatable |
| Number of injections per session | 15–20 intradermal micro-injections per side |
| Sensation | Mild — a starch-iodine test maps the sweat zones first |
| Onset | 5–7 days for initial reduction; full effect by day 14 |
| Duration | 6–8 months average; some patients report longer |
| Downtime | None — normal activities resume immediately |

**This is not purely a cosmetic treatment.** Many patients who book this have tried clinical-grade antiperspirants and prescription treatments without success. For them, hyperhidrosis treatment addresses a functional problem that affects daily life — clothing choices, professional situations, physical contact.

**Is it safe in Malta's climate?** Yes. The treatment is localised — it does not impair your body's ability to thermoregulate. You have four to five million sweat glands across the body; blocking a few thousand in the underarm does not affect your ability to cool down.

**Bottom line:** If excessive sweating is affecting your daily function — especially in Malta's climate — this is a straightforward, evidence-backed treatment worth discussing at consultation.

## Day 19 — "What Menopause Does to Your Face — And What We Can Actually Do About It"

The hormonal changes of perimenopause and menopause are among the most significant skin-affecting events in a woman's life — and they are discussed far too rarely in the aesthetics space. Understanding the mechanism makes the treatment logic clear.

Oestrogen plays a direct structural role in skin. It stimulates fibroblast activity (collagen production), supports hyaluronic acid production (natural hydration), and maintains the thickness of the dermis. When oestrogen levels decline — typically beginning in the mid-to-late 40s, with full menopause on average around age 51 — these effects reduce measurably. Studies suggest women can lose up to 30% of skin collagen in the years following menopause, with the most significant loss concentrated in the early post-menopausal period — though the rate and degree vary significantly between individuals. That is not a gradual decline; it is a substantial structural change.

Concurrently, the superficial fat compartments of the face — which provide the soft padding over the bony framework — reduce and redistribute. The result is a face that simultaneously looks thinner (less fat support) and less firm (less collagen in the dermis), with features that descend slightly due to reduced structural support.

| Change | Mechanism | Observable effect |
|---|---|---|
| Collagen loss | Reduced fibroblast stimulation | Loss of firmness, deeper static lines |
| Reduced skin HA | Less intrinsic moisture | Dryness, increased surface wrinkling |
| Fat compartment redistribution | Hormonal influence on adipose tissue | Hollowed temples, flattened cheeks, early jowling |
| Reduced skin thickness | Dermal thinning | More fragile skin, slower healing |

**What can actually be done?** The treatment approach for perimenopausal and menopausal patients prioritises rebuilding what was lost — not masking it.

1. **Sculptra (poly-L-lactic acid):** The most appropriate collagen stimulator for this cohort. Works gradually over three months to stimulate genuine collagen production — building structure, not adding bulk.
2. **Profhilo:** Addresses the HA deficit directly — improving skin hydration and firmness in a way that complements, not replaces, the collagen work.
3. **Conservative HA filler:** In specific areas of volume deficit (temples, midface) — anatomy-led and restrained, not volumising for its own sake.
4. **Polynucleotides:** Periorbital repair — the under-eye area is particularly affected by hormonal ageing.

**Bottom line:** Menopause changes the face in predictable, addressable ways. The most effective treatments for this stage are the ones that work *with* the biology — rebuilding, rather than simply filling.

## Day 26 — "A Patient Came to Me After a Bad Filler Experience. This Is Her Now."

Correction cases are some of the most nuanced and gratifying work in aesthetics — and some of the most instructive. They show what happens when technique, product choice, or patient selection goes wrong, and they demonstrate why dissolution and careful re-treatment are legitimate, structured clinical processes.

Hyaluronic acid filler is, by design, reversible. Hyaluronidase is an enzyme that breaks down HA — your body produces it naturally, and a concentrated injectable version can dissolve HA filler in hours to days, depending on the product and how long it has been in place.

The most common reason patients come to me for a second opinion is a result they did not ask for. That framing — rooted in clinical experience rather than any single study — reflects a real pattern: patients who had filler elsewhere and are now unhappy with what they are seeing. Overfilled lips. Cheeks that look heavy. Under-eye product that has created a bluish ridge. Results that looked plausible on day three and wrong by week six.

**When dissolution is the right first step:**

| Presentation | Clinical approach |
|---|---|
| Visible migration of lip filler above the vermillion border | Dissolve with hyaluronidase; allow 4–6 weeks for full resolution before any re-treatment |
| Tyndall effect under the eyes (bluish discolouration from superficially placed HA) | Careful targeted dissolution; may require 2 sessions |
| Overfilled cheeks causing a heavy, pillow-like appearance | Dissolution of excess HA; reassess volume needs after settling |
| Asymmetry unresponsive to massage or time | Targeted dissolution of the over-projected side; symmetry assessment before re-treatment |

**What the correction process looks like:**

Session 1 is assessment. The practitioner maps exactly what product is present, estimates how much has already metabolised, and decides whether partial or full dissolution is appropriate. Clinical photographs are taken. Session 2 (one to two weeks later) is dissolution if confirmed. Hyaluronidase is injected. Swelling occurs briefly — the area often looks worse in the 48 hours immediately after, which is entirely normal. Session 3 (four to six weeks after dissolution) is reassessment of the baseline — the face without any artificial product present. The practitioner and patient then agree on a re-treatment plan, if any.

**What patients get wrong about correction:** Many expect that dissolving filler returns them to the face they had before their original treatment. This is broadly true for HA. Setting realistic expectations at the outset is essential.

**Bottom line:** Correction is not failure. It is evidence that the system — reversible product, qualified practitioners, patient advocacy — works. The goal is always to leave you in a better position than you arrived.

## Day 27 — "The Menopause Beauty Protocol: Sculptra + Profhilo + Conservative Filler"

Treating menopausal skin well requires a sequenced strategy — not a single treatment, but a structured programme that addresses the different layers of change that hormonal transition has caused. When the protocol is designed correctly, the results compound: each treatment sets the stage for the next one to work better.

**Phase 1: Rebuild the structure (Sculptra)**

The foundational deficit in post-menopausal skin is collagen. Until that is addressed, adding surface hydration or volumising with HA filler is like painting a wall with cracks in it. Sculptra is injected first — typically two to three sessions over three to four months — allowing the collagen-stimulation response to establish before anything else is added. Patients at this stage often notice very little visible change. That is expected. The work is happening below the surface.

**Phase 2: Restore the hydration layer (Profhilo)**

After the first Sculptra session, a Profhilo course begins. This addresses the dermal HA deficit that oestrogen decline causes — improving skin quality, luminosity, and early laxity from within. The biostimulation effect of Profhilo works synergistically with Sculptra's collagen response. By the time both courses are complete, the skin has genuinely better structure and hydration than before either treatment started.

**Phase 3: Targeted volume (Conservative HA Filler)**

Only after the structural and quality work is any volumising filler considered. And it is conservative: specific areas only — temples if significantly hollowed, midface if structurally deficient, tear trough if the tissue is ready — at low doses. The goal is precision, not fullness.

## Day 29 — "Most Clinics Under-Train on Tear-Trough Anatomy. Here's What That Means for You."

I want to say something about the industry that most practitioners will not say publicly, because saying it generates uncomfortable conversations with colleagues. But it is true, it is verifiable, and your safety depends on you knowing it.

Tear-trough treatment — injectable filler in the periorbital area — is one of the highest-risk procedures in aesthetic medicine. Not because it goes wrong often, but because when it does go wrong, the consequences are among the hardest to correct in the field. Tyndall effect (the bluish discolouration from superficially placed HA), malar festoons, and — in rare but documented cases — vascular compromise to periorbital vessels. These are not theoretical risks; they are documented outcomes in published adverse event literature.

What makes tear-trough treatment safer — genuinely safer, not cosmetically compliant — is a specific set of anatomical competencies:

1. **Aspiration technique.** Before injecting in any area with proximity to named vessels, aspiration (drawing back the plunger slightly to check for intravascular placement) adds a meaningful safety check. Not every practitioner does this, and not every technique makes it practical — but the knowledge of when and why it matters is non-negotiable.

2. **Cannula technique.** A blunt-tipped cannula displaces vessels rather than piercing them. In zones like the periorbital area, where the angular and infraorbital arteries run in close proximity to treatment planes, cannula use materially reduces vascular risk compared to sharp-needle injection.

3. **Hyaluronidase accessibility.** A practitioner who offers tear-trough filler must have hyaluronidase on-hand, know the emergency dissolution protocol for vascular occlusion, and be willing to use it immediately if needed. Not in a fridge somewhere in the building. On the treatment tray.

4. **Patient selection rigour.** The most important safety decision is made before any product is drawn: the decision about which patients should not receive tear-trough filler. Malar oedema, festooning, very thin skin — these are contraindications that require clinical experience to recognise.

When I assess a clinic — in Malta or anywhere — these are the questions I would ask about their tear-trough protocol. Good answers are not hard to give for a well-trained practitioner. The difficulty of the question is itself informative.

*Subtle by Design* extended to clinical ethics: subtlety in the periorbital area requires exceptional training, not just restraint.

## Day 30 — "40–50: How I Actually Think About Full Facial Rejuvenation"

The 40s are the decade when aesthetic medicine becomes genuinely comprehensive. Before 40, for most patients, the conversation is about prevention and skin quality. After 50, the focus shifts to support and restoration. In the 40s, you often need to think about all three simultaneously — and the sequencing is everything.

**The five things I assess at consultation:**

| Assessment dimension | What I am looking for | Treatment implication |
|---|---|---|
| Volume | Where has the face lost fat compartment support? | HA filler in specific anatomical compartments |
| Movement | Which dynamic lines are forming? How deep are they? | Neuromodulator — dose depends on depth and skin laxity |
| Structure | Is the underlying bony framework declining? (Yes, gradually, from the 40s) | Collagen stimulators; sometimes structural filler at chin or jaw |
| Skin quality | Collagen loss, hydration, pigmentation, texture | Profhilo, polynucleotides, +/- collagen stim |
| Proportions | What is the relationship between facial thirds, chin-jaw line, orbital frame? | Guides where and what — the architectural foundation |

**The typical sequence for a 40s patient:**

Step 1 is anti-wrinkle treatment first. Softening dynamic lines before addressing volume is important — otherwise you can over-correct the volume, and the excess movement then works against the result.

Step 2 is skin quality work. Profhilo, and polynucleotides where relevant. The quality of the skin surface affects how every other treatment looks. Poor skin quality with good volume correction looks wrong.

Step 3 is conservative filler. Targeting specific volume deficits — usually midface, temples, and sometimes the lower face — at modest doses with the understanding that this is the first of probably two to three sessions rather than a one-time complete correction.

Step 4 is considering a collagen stimulator. For patients with more significant structural change, Sculptra or Radiesse running alongside the HA work builds long-term structural support.

**What I am always listening for in the consultation:** The patient who wants to "look younger." My answer is always the same: I do not aim to make you look younger. I aim to make you look like a well-rested, healthy version of yourself at your current age. Those are different goals — and the second one produces results that hold up over time.

**Bottom line:** Full rejuvenation in the 40s is a layered, sequenced programme — not a list of treatments. Done well, the result is cumulative, subtle, and sustained.

## Day 31 — "What Would I Spend €500 on at Carisma Aesthetics in 2026?"

Price transparency matters — especially in Malta, where aesthetic patients routinely research costs before they will pick up the phone. So let me tell you exactly how I would think about a €500 budget, depending on your age, concern, and what you are trying to achieve. This is the honest clinical answer I give in consultations every week.

**If you are 25–32 and your concern is prevention and skin quality:**

€500 buys you a full Profhilo course (two appointments, standard course) — and this is where I would spend it. The skin quality improvement from a course of Profhilo at this age sets a baseline that makes every future treatment work better. If there is budget remaining, a conservative low-dose anti-wrinkle treatment session (20–30 units) for early dynamic lines is the natural complement.

**If you are 35–45 and your concern is the first signs of volume loss and movement lines:**

€500 is a meaningful starting point, but a starting point only. I would prioritise one session of low-dose anti-wrinkle treatment for the upper face, and an initial Profhilo appointment to begin the skin quality work. This gives you two separate improvements — softened lines and improved skin — without committing to a full volumising filler session that you may not need once the quality work is done.

**If you are 45–60 and your primary concern is collagen loss and skin laxity:**

€500 gets you the first session of Sculptra — and this is how I would spend it, because Sculptra's long-term collagen benefit is best realised as a programme, and starting is the most important step. The remaining budget covers the planning consultation that maps the full course.

**What I would never spend €500 on:** A package deal at a clinic you have not researched. Product authenticity, practitioner training, and facility standards matter more than the price of the treatment. The cheapest clinic rarely has the cheapest real-world cost when you account for corrections.
