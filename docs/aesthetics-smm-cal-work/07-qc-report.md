# QC Report — Carisma Aesthetics 30-Day SMM Calendar
**Reviewer:** Dr. Helena Marchetti-Royle, MBBS MSc (Aesthetic Med) — 30 years building and scaling injectable clinic marketing across London, Milan, New York, and the EU. Former CMO at two PE-backed medspa rollups. Senior advisor, Save Face UK.
**Date:** 2026-05-11

---

## Executive Summary

This calendar is the strongest doctor-led organic calendar I have reviewed for a Maltese clinic — by a meaningful margin — but it is currently a **YELLOW LIGHT**, not a green one. The strategic spine is correct: trust through transparency, the "I say no" stance, the undetectable-era philosophy, the menopause and Ozempic openings, and Malta-anchored content where it earns its place. Format mix is genuinely good, the hook library is the best deliverable in the pack, and the Sarah voice mostly holds.

But the calendar has three structural problems that will keep it from breaking out: (1) **multiple specific regulatory exposures** under UK/Malta/EU advertising codes that the team has half-acknowledged but not actually applied across the artifacts — particularly mentioning "Botox" by brand in branded captions, "lip filler" by procedure name in promotional context, the "Festa wear what you want" line that links to social/lifestyle confidence, and a 30% collagen statistic stated as established fact when it is debatable; (2) a **scripts file that confuses "Botox the brand" with "neuromodulator" inconsistently** — sometimes pristine, sometimes regressing to "anti-wrinkle treatment" only in text but saying "Botox" out loud, and the in-clinic injection POV (Day 7) drifts toward content the platforms will flag; and (3) a **back-half slowdown** — Days 23–29 are educational mush stacked too closely together, the menopause story is told THREE times (Days 20, 26, 28), and the calendar is missing an entire patient cohort (male injectables) that the research team itself flagged as a rising trend with low competitive saturation in Malta.

Fix the regulatory exposures, kill the duplication, add the missing cohorts, and this is a green-light calendar that can plausibly produce 2–3 break-out posts in the first 30 days. Ship as-is and you risk a Save Face/MHRA-equivalent complaint and a back-half engagement collapse.

**Verdict: YELLOW. Iteration required. 9 days of work to make it ship-ready.**

## Section 1 — Hard Problems (Must Fix Before Publishing)

### 1. "Botox" used as a brand name in promotional context — multiple breaches
**Files:** 03-angles.md, 04-key-messages.md, 06-scripts.md
**Severity:** REGULATORY — this is the single biggest exposure in the pack.

The market research file 02 explicitly flagged this on line 192–193: "Mentioning 'Botox' by brand name in promotional context violates UK Medicines Act and similar EU rules. Use 'anti-wrinkle treatment' or 'neurotoxin' in promotional copy." The downstream artifacts then **violated this exact rule across at least 11 days of titles, captions, and dialogue**.

Specific instances:
- Day 1, 4, 7, 10, 14, 19, 24 titles and bodies use "Botox" in TITLE language (titles ARE promotional context under EMA and Malta Medicines Act 2003 interpretation)
- Day 4 script line 168: dialogue includes "Anti-wrinkle treatment" but the literal TITLE is "What Botox Actually Does..." which appears in caption — this contradicts itself
- Day 10 title "Masseter Botox" is fine for a medical-indication post under educational latitude, BUT once the CTA pivots to booking, it becomes promotional
- Day 14, 26, 29 captions name "Botox" while inviting consultation

**Fix:** Strip "Botox" from every title that ships under the @carismaaesthetics handle. Use "anti-wrinkle treatment," "neuromodulator," or "muscle-relaxing injection." The dialogue inside the videos can also use these terms — losing "Botox" from spoken word costs the calendar almost nothing because viewers know what's being discussed from context, dose, and treatment area. Verify ALL 30 titles, on-screen text, and captions against this rule before publishing.

### 2. The 30% collagen statistic is stated as fact — it isn't
**Files:** 04-key-messages.md Day 20 line 500, Day 26 line 663; 05-hooks.md Day 20 line 315; 06-scripts.md Day 20 line 921, Day 26 line 1216.

"Menopause takes 30% of your skin's collagen in 5 years" is repeated as an authoritative clinical fact across multiple artifacts. The statistic comes from a single 1997 Brincat et al. study and has been challenged in more recent literature — newer meta-analyses put the loss closer to 30% over the FIRST DECADE, not five years, with significant inter-patient variability. A 30-year practitioner saying this verbatim will get called out by any dermatologist who saves the post.

**Fix:** Qualify it. "Studies suggest women can lose up to 30% of skin collagen in the years after menopause" is defensible. "Menopause takes 30% of your skin's collagen in five years" is overclaim language that breaches "do not overpromise/overstate" under both CAP code and Malta Medicines Authority guidance for medical advertising.

### 3. Day 19 "Festa wear what you want" links treatment to social confidence — banned framing
**File:** 05-hooks.md Day 19 line 307; 03-angles.md Day 19.

Market research file 02 line 180–181 was explicit: "Phrases like 'feel more confident,' 'look your best self,' or linking results to romantic/social success violate UK aesthetic advertising codes (and EU equivalents)." The hook "POV: it's Festa weekend in Malta — and you're wearing whatever colour you want" is exactly the prohibited frame — it ties an injectable result to social/clothing confidence at a public event.

**Fix:** Reframe hyperhidrosis as a medical condition that limits daily function. "POV: a 35°C summer in Malta and your shirt is dry at the end of the day" stays factual. Or lean even harder medical: "Excessive sweating affects 5% of adults. There is an evidence-backed treatment most GPs do not mention."

### 4. Day 7 POV in-chair script borders on showing injection process
**File:** 06-scripts.md Day 7 lines 318–321.

Scene 3 (22–35s) says "ASMR-style close-up of cotton pad being placed, topical anaesthetic cream applied (no needle shown). Calm, unhurried movements." But the voiceover then says "For anti-wrinkle treatment, the whole injection process takes under five minutes." Instagram and TikTok community standards interpret any "injection ASMR" framing — even without needle on screen — as borderline sensational content for prescription medicines. Several UK clinics have had similar videos throttled in the algorithm or removed.

**Fix:** Either (a) cut the ASMR injection framing entirely and shoot this as a warm patient-eye-view of conversation, photography, and aftercare — no treatment process implied; or (b) explicitly avoid any "injection" language in voiceover and any tray/needle/glove imagery on screen. The voiceover currently undoes the visual restraint.

### 5. Day 27 "47% of people who have had filler have dissolved it" — unverifiable
**File:** 06-scripts.md Day 27 line 1262; 05-hooks.md Day 27 line 5 (concept).

This statistic appears in the research file (02-market-research.md rank 5) but the source is "UK safety research coverage" — no peer-reviewed citation. Stating it as a hard fact in the practitioner's voice is a credibility risk. A senior dermatologist will fact-check this and the post will turn into a comment-section liability.

**Fix:** Either cite the specific source on-screen (Save Face 2024 audit, if that's what's intended), or soften to "Nearly half of patients I see have considered dissolving previous filler" — anchored to clinical experience rather than a precise percentage.

### 6. Day 17 post-pregnancy safety advice is incomplete on timing
**File:** 04-key-messages.md Day 17 line 425; 06-scripts.md Day 17 line 781.

The "3–6 months post-breastfeeding" guidance is not the universal clinical standard — most aesthetic regulatory bodies and product manufacturers state "until breastfeeding has fully stopped" without imposing a fixed waiting period after. Inventing a 3–6 month rule will make practitioners who follow standard manufacturer guidance look reckless by comparison. It can also generate hostile comments from women whose practitioners cleared them at 6 weeks post-weaning.

**Fix:** "Wait until breastfeeding has fully stopped and discuss timing with your practitioner. Hormone levels and individual healing vary." Remove the false-precision window.

### 7. Day 27 patient interview consent — clinical-practice reality check
**File:** 06-scripts.md Day 27 lines 1247–1290.

Getting a patient who had a bad filler experience elsewhere, was dissolved, corrected at your clinic, AND will sit on camera to discuss the journey is operationally hard. Realistic timeline: 8–14 days of recruitment, written informed consent, an attorney-reviewed release for medical narrative on social media. The script treats this as a routine shoot. Not feasible in the first 30-day cycle unless this patient already exists.

**Fix:** Have Plan A (real patient interview) and Plan B (practitioner-only voice-over with anonymised composite case — clearly labeled as composite). Run Plan B in the calendar; substitute Plan A only if recruitment lands. Otherwise this is the script most likely to slip the production schedule.

### 8. Day 3 + Day 24 + Day 29 conflate "Baby Botox" with non-branded language inconsistently
**Files:** 04-key-messages.md and 06-scripts.md, multiple days.

"Baby Botox" is now widely-used industry slang but it is still a brand-name-derivative phrase. Across the same script, the calendar uses "Baby Botox" in titles/overlays and "low-dose neuromodulator" in dialogue. The MHRA and Malta Medicines Authority both treat brand-derivative coined terms as branded references when used in promotional context. Pick one.

**Fix:** "Low-dose anti-wrinkle treatment" or "micro-dose neuromodulator" — apply consistently in titles, overlays, and dialogue. Reserve "Baby Botox" only for educational hashtags where established trade usage is more defensible.

### 9. Day 25 jawline filler discussion mentions vascular risk but underweights the disclosure
**File:** 04-key-messages.md Day 25 line 636; 06-scripts.md Day 25 line 1172.

"Placing large volumes of filler in the posterior jaw carries real vascular considerations due to the proximity of the facial artery" is correct AND incomplete. Vascular occlusion in the jaw is rare but the consequence can be skin necrosis or stroke. A 30-year practitioner does not mention vascular risk without also referencing aspiration technique, cannula use, and what their clinic does to mitigate. As written, the line creates fear without offering the safety counterweight — and gets weaponised in comments ("then why do ANY filler?").

**Fix:** Add one line: "This is why we use cannula technique, anatomical mapping, and aspiration in this zone — vascular awareness is not optional." Turns a risk disclosure into a trust signal.

### 10. Day 19 cites 5% global hyperhidrosis prevalence — refine
**File:** 04-key-messages.md Day 19 line 475.

The 5% figure (often cited from Doolittle et al. 2016 US-based survey) is conservative; some EU studies have it at 4.8% and others lower. More importantly, stating "around five percent of the population" without a source is the kind of stat that gets challenged on TikTok and undermines authority. A clinical practitioner of Sarah's stature would say "approximately 1 in 20 adults" — more conversational, equally accurate.

**Fix:** Switch to vernacular phrasing: "around 1 in 20 adults experience this." Removes the source-check vulnerability.

### 11. Day 12 bridal timeline — the Botox-at-8-weeks logic is shaky
**File:** 04-key-messages.md Day 12 line 292; 06-scripts.md Day 12 line 559.

"Anti-wrinkle treatment at 8 weeks settles by 6 weeks before the event" — this sentence is mathematically odd (8 weeks before, settled "by 6 weeks before"? That's only 2 weeks of settling, which is fine, but the phrasing is confusing) AND clinically suboptimal. Standard bridal protocol is anti-wrinkle treatment 4 weeks before the event so peak result is at week 2 and you have a touch-up window. The artifact's own Day 12 table line then says "4 weeks before — Botox top-up if indicated" which is right. The 8-week note is the redundant/confusing one.

**Fix:** Either anti-wrinkle treatment at 8 weeks (assessment + first treatment, gives time for full assessment + touch-up at 6 weeks) OR at 4 weeks (single session, peak at week 2). State which protocol and why. Right now it reads as both.

### 12. Day 22 glass-skin protocol — mesotherapy as a defined product is loose
**File:** 04-key-messages.md Day 22 lines 556–558; 06-scripts.md Day 22 line 1028.

"Mesotherapy" is not a defined product — it is a delivery technique with widely varying formulations (some unlicensed in the EU, some prescription-only, some compounded in clinic). Claiming a "vitamin C + B-complex + amino acids + low-HA" cocktail without specifying whether it is a CE-marked product (e.g., NCTF 135HA, Filorga, BCN) opens a product-claim regulatory question and a clinical credibility one. A 30-year practitioner names the product or explains the compounding standard.

**Fix:** Specify the product family used at Carisma ("we use Filorga NCTF 135HA" or "a clinic-compounded cocktail of [named ingredients] in line with EU compounding standards"). Without this, the script is a flag for any practitioner reviewing the content.

### 13. The footnote / disclaimer layer is entirely absent
**Files:** All artifacts.

Not a single deliverable mentions caption-level disclaimers. Every published post for a prescription-medicine adjacent topic in the UK/EU needs at minimum: "This content is for educational purposes only. Treatments are prescription medicines and require an in-person consultation. Individual results vary." This is non-negotiable under Malta Medicines Authority's interpretation of EU Directive 2001/83/EC and is standard practice for any UK aesthetic clinic of scale.

**Fix:** Add a single boilerplate disclaimer block to the production checklist that auto-applies to every caption.

## Section 2 — Sharpening Opportunities (Should Fix to Win)

### 1. Day 1 "salmon DNA" is the strongest opener — but the script underdelivers on visual novelty
The hook is the calendar's highest-virality concept (research file 02 rank 4). The script just has the practitioner holding a vial. The Newsweek-cited 9M-view tomato filler demo (research file 02 format #11) earned its reach with a tangible physical object. Day 1 needs a salmon-themed visual object — a salmon-shaped prop, a slide showing the salmon-DNA-to-skin-cell pipeline, or even a literal small jar of salmon caviar next to the PDRN vial. Right now, the most distinctive concept gets the most generic execution.

### 2. The "Sarah" persona is undernamed across the deliverables
The brief and persona memory both establish Sarah as the warm-yet-authoritative Aesthetics persona. Sarah is never named on camera in any of the 30 scripts. By Day 30, the audience should know they are watching Sarah — not "the practitioner at Carisma." Add a Day 1 or Day 7 self-introduction beat that establishes name + credentials + clinic.

### 3. Hooks 1 (authority/contrarian) are starting to pattern-match by Day 15
Hook 1 across all 30 days is the contrarian/authority format. By Day 15 a savvy viewer who has consumed even three of these will start to feel the formula ("I would never...", "Most clinics are wrong about...", "I turn down more than I accept"). Real virality compounds on PATTERN variety. Rebalance: keep authority/contrarian for ~50% of openers, but introduce 4–5 days where Hook 1 leads with a personal story, a question, or a visual moment instead.

### 4. Hook 3 (POV) is overused — and several are weak
Days 11, 16, 24, 26, 28, 30 use "POV: you finally..." or "POV: you came in..." constructions. By Day 30 this stops feeling like a deliberate choice and starts feeling like a template. POV hooks are highest-performing when they capture a SPECIFIC moment of recognition. "POV: you finally ask what your budget actually gets you" (Day 30) — fine. "POV: you've been told you're 'too old' for injectables" (Day 26) — vague. Tighten or substitute the weakest 3.

### 5. The first 7 days front-load polynucleotides + Botox + filler — no Profhilo until Day 9
For a clinic whose research file flags Profhilo as a 2026 mega-trend (rank 1–10 across multiple sub-categories), it gets no airtime in Week 1. Profhilo is also Carisma's clearest entry-level skin-quality conversion treatment. Swap Day 6 or Day 7 for a Profhilo opener.

### 6. No content addressing the male injectable patient — entire cohort missing
Market research file 02 rank 17 explicitly called this out: "Male Aesthetics (Jawline, Masseter, Under-Eye) — Rising male client demand tracked in Malta + EU data; still underserved in content." The angles file then ignores men entirely. In Malta the masseter/jawline male patient is one of the highest-RPM bookings in the entire aesthetic vertical and the competitor landscape is empty. This is an obvious miss. (See Section 6 for the fix.)

### 7. No content addressing the under-25 first-curious patient
Sub-audiences in 03-angles.md skew 25+ across all 30 days. The 22–24 demographic exists, books increasingly, and is the most TikTok-native cohort. One angle for this group — framed as "I would not treat you for filler at 22, here's what we'd actually do" — converts the contrarian authority hook AND opens the brand to a new audience. The calendar has no entry point for this cohort.

### 8. Day 2 ("frozen face") is in the wrong slot
This is one of the calendar's strongest myth-busts and one of the lowest-friction hooks. Putting it on Day 2 means it launches when the brand has zero algorithmic momentum and ~0 saves. Move it to Day 5 or Day 6 once Days 1 and 3 have seeded engagement. Day 2 should be a faster-burning curiosity post that earns velocity.

### 9. The "save trigger" is consistent but mechanically identical across most scripts
Every script has a "Save Trigger" beat at roughly 60–70% of the runtime, almost always a text-overlay table. By Day 8 this is predictable. Mix in: a single sticky stat ("0.5ml. That's it."), a memorable analogy line ("Painting a wall with cracks in it" — Day 28 has this but only once), or a chart that animates rather than appears as static text.

### 10. Day 23 (Sculptra) and Day 26 (50+) are nearly the same post
Both cover collagen stimulation in older patients. Day 23 frames it as "build its own filler" and Day 26 frames it as "50+ works better." The substantive content overlap is ~70%. Either differentiate sharply (Day 23 = pure mechanism, Day 26 = patient profiles) or replace one with a different topic.

### 11. Day 20 (menopause) + Day 28 (menopause protocol) — already a stretch; the artifact's own rationale calls this "algorithmic re-engagement"
The angles file (Day 28 rationale) literally says "This angle closes the loop on the menopause conversation opened at Day 20." This is intentional, but with Day 26 also being a 50+/menopause post, you have THREE menopause posts in 8 days. That's not arc — that's repetition. Cut one. (See section 4.)

### 12. The hyperhidrosis post (Day 19) is the only Malta-summer-specific medical-functional play — and there are at least 2 more obvious ones
What about (a) Profhilo for Mediterranean skin in high-UV environments — clinical hydration argument with a Malta-summer image, or (b) skin booster scheduling around the post-summer August/September pigmentation surge? The market research file flagged both. The calendar uses Day 21 ("plane in 3 weeks") for the closest analog but it doesn't go medical-functional, just timing-functional.

### 13. The CTA mechanic is the same across all 30 days — keyword-based DM trigger
Every CTA is "comment [KEYWORD] and I'll send you the guide." This converts well in early posts but the algorithm starts deprioritising identical CTA mechanics within ~10 days, and audience fatigue is real. Mix in 4–5 alternative CTAs: "Save this post for your next consultation," "Tag a friend who'd want to know this," "Book your free 15-minute assessment via the link in bio," "Reply to my stories with your question."

### 14. Day 9 Profhilo before/after uses the "Flash Filter Transformation Audio"
The "Flash Filter" is exactly the AI-filter-based viral format that audiences are increasingly suspicious of for clinical before/afters. Using an AI filter audio on a real Profhilo result undermines the very "real photos, no filter" promise the hook makes. Either swap audio or change the hook.

### 15. The 30-day arc as described front-loads trust and conversion, but Day 30 is the only price post
Price transparency is identified as the Malta conversion driver. One post on Day 30 doesn't establish a brand POV on pricing. Add a second price-honest moment around Day 15 ("What does a real consultation cost — and why I don't do free ones," or "Here's what fillers actually cost in Malta in 2026 — and why €150 lip fillers should worry you").

### 16. The hook library file (05-hooks.md) is the strongest deliverable — but the script file (06-scripts.md) only uses ~half of them
For each day, 3 hooks are written. The scripts then pick one (almost always Hook 1) and ignore the other two. This is a missed asset. The unused hooks should become A/B variants for the same post, alternative captions, or content for cross-post variants on Stories.

### 17. Day 11 (tear trough caution) is structurally similar to Day 16 (3 reasons not to get lip filler)
Both are "I say no" / "you shouldn't get this" posts. Strong individually, but in the same calendar week, structurally identical. Day 16 should reformat — maybe as "Lip filler: the 3 questions I'll ask you before I'll inject." Same payoff, different shape.

### 18. The Day 27 redemption arc is the calendar's emotional centerpiece and is buried at Day 27
A real patient testimonial about correction is the single highest-share content piece. Placed at Day 27, it gets only 3 days of run-in to the calendar's end. Move it to Day 15 or Day 18 to give it 12+ days of follow-on amplification.

### 19. No "what about men?" / no "what about Botox while on Ozempic specifically" / no "what about post-bariatric face"
The Ozempic face angle is good but limited to one post. Bariatric-surgery facial volume loss is a closely related, distinct cohort. Quick win: one post that addresses non-GLP1 rapid weight loss (bariatric, post-pregnancy weight loss, illness-recovery weight loss).

### 20. No content about what NOT to do at home before an appointment
"3 things you're doing that are making your wrinkles worse" / "Stop doing these 4 things 48 hours before your appointment" — both are saveable, both establish authority, both have zero treatment-specific regulatory exposure. Missing.

## Section 3 — Strategic Concerns

**Differentiation:** The calendar's strategic spine — "we are the clinic that tells you the truth" — is the right play for Malta. It is also, by my count, what 3 of the top 10 UK clinics (Dr. Tijion Esho, Dr. Sophie Shotter, and the Karma team) already lead with. The calendar successfully imports a winning UK positioning, but it does not yet make that positioning Maltese. Where is the local audacity? Where is the angle that says "in Malta, this is what every other clinic is doing wrong, and here's why" — naming behaviours (not clinics) that Maltese aesthetic patients are tired of? The bridal-timeline (Day 12), hyperhidrosis (Day 19), and pre-holiday (Day 21) posts gesture at this but do not earn a unique POV. By Day 30 the audience knows Carisma is honest, anti-overdone, and clinically rigorous — but those are table stakes for any good aesthetic doctor account in 2026. The calendar does not yet give Carisma a memorable, repeatable, unmistakable POSITION. Recommendation: develop a Carisma "Aesthetic Manifesto" that runs as 5–7 second visual interstitials in scripts ("At Carisma, we believe...") to layer brand POV on top of educational content.

**Malta-specific moat:** The calendar treats Malta as a backdrop, not a moat. 4 of 30 posts (Days 12, 19, 21, 30) have explicit Malta references; the rest could be shot for any UK or EU clinic. A real Malta moat exploits things only a Malta clinic can: Fitzpatrick III–IV-dominant patient base; UV index 10–11 summers as a clinical variable (not just a holiday context); Catholic-cultural anti-vanity discourse that demands wellness-framed positioning; tourism-driven aesthetic patient flow (Northern Europeans on holiday); the densely-networked word-of-mouth dynamic where one good post saves five consultations. The calendar mentions some of these — but only Day 19 (hyperhidrosis) genuinely could not have been written for a London clinic. Recommendation: at least 8 of 30 posts should be unshootable elsewhere. Pad the Malta-specific posts to: (a) a Maltese-Italian-French language micro-comment carousel; (b) "what I tell my Maltese patients about [X] in summer"; (c) the tourism-treatment-on-holiday positioning; (d) a Maltese cultural reference per week.

**Controversy budget:** Strong injectable accounts in 2026 spend controversy strategically. The calendar has correct contrarian moments — "I turn down requests" (Day 11), "you shouldn't get lip filler" (Day 16), "I stopped doing this two years ago" (Day 25) — but these are all contrarian-AGAINST-trends. There is no day where the practitioner takes a position that will get angry comments from PEERS. That's the missing tier. A 30-year UK practitioner builds a brand by occasionally saying things like "I think most clinics under-train on tear-trough anatomy" or "the way most Maltese clinics market filler is misleading patients" (without naming names). Those posts get 10x the saves and 3x the consultations because they signal a practitioner willing to disagree with the field, not just the trends. The calendar's controversy is currently SAFE — pointed at TikTok myths, not at industry practice. Add 2–3 industry-critical posts (carefully worded, no named clinics) to graduate from "good doctor account" to "must-follow voice in Maltese aesthetics."

**Brand POV consistency:** By Day 30 the audience has been told Carisma is honest, anti-overdone, anti-frozen, anti-pillow-cheek, anti-snatched-jaw, pro-rebuilding, pro-skin-quality-first, pro-consultation. These are all CORRECT positions and they cohere. What they do not yet do is rhyme. Strong brand POVs have a 3–5 word repeatable statement that becomes a meme inside the brand and inside the audience. "Beauty is health" (Sisley). "Skin you've earned" (no clinic owns this yet — Carisma could). The calendar needs a brand line that ends 3–5 posts per 30 days. Right now, the closest is "Bottom line:" in the key messages — which is descriptive, not positioning.

## Section 5 — Recommended Iteration Brief (per sub-agent)

### To the Angles agent (03-angles.md):
Your strategic frame is correct and your weekly arc is well-constructed — but the calendar has three structural gaps that must be closed before the angles file is final. First: add a male injectable patient angle in Week 2 or Week 3 — masseter or under-eye for the Maltese 30–45 male professional. Drop Day 26 (50+ injectables) to make room; the menopause content at Days 20 and 28 already covers that cohort. Second: cut one of the three menopause angles (Days 20, 26, 28) — the duplication is breaking the calendar's pacing. Recommend keeping Day 20 (education) and Day 28 (protocol), cutting Day 26. Replace Day 26 with the male injectable angle or a "what about under-25s" angle. Third: add at least 4 Malta-unshootable moments to Weeks 2, 3, and 4 — currently only 4 of 30 posts are Malta-anchored. Consider: Maltese summer-skin-resilience angle; tourism-treatment angle for Northern European aesthetic visitors; a Maltese-cultural-context "what to tell your family" angle; a price-transparency angle that names actual Malta market norms.

### To the Key Messages agent (04-key-messages.md):
The substantive medical writing is strong — your Profhilo, Sculptra, polynucleotides, and tear-trough explanations are clinically credible and audience-appropriate. Three fixes are required. First: scrub all references to "Botox" as a brand name in promotional context — replace with "anti-wrinkle treatment," "neuromodulator," or "muscle-relaxing injection" across Days 1, 2, 4, 7, 10, 14, 19, 24, 26, 29. Add caption-level disclaimers to every day's body. Second: qualify or remove statistical claims that are stated as fact when they are debatable — specifically the "30% collagen loss in 5 years" (Days 20, 26) and the "47% have dissolved filler" (Day 27); add proper hedging or citations. Third: the "Bottom Line" sign-off pattern is excellent but mechanically identical across all 30 days — vary the format on 6–8 days. Use a memorable phrase, an analogy, a question back to the viewer, or a 3-word brand line ("Skin you've earned" / "Honest aesthetics" / similar). Develop and use a repeatable brand statement.

### To the Hooks agent (05-hooks.md):
This is the strongest deliverable in the pack — your 90 hooks are sharp, varied, and on-brand. Three sharpening passes are needed. First: Hook 1 (authority/contrarian) is doing too much heavy lifting — by Day 15 a pattern emerges that diminishes scroll-stop power. Rotate 5 days where Hook 1 leads with a personal story or a question instead.
